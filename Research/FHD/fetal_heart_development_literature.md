## 1. Overview & Comprehensive Reviews

### Tan & Lewandowski (2019) — The Transitional Heart: From Early Embryonic and Fetal Development to Neonatal Life
- **PMID:** 31533099 | **DOI:** [10.1159/000501906](https://doi.org/10.1159/000501906)
- **Journal:** Fetal Diagnosis and Therapy, 47(5):373-386
- **Summary:** Comprehensive review covering the full arc of heart development from specification of myocardial progenitor cells through heart tube looping, fetal cardiovascular adaptations for the hypoxemic intrauterine environment, and the complex physiological, anatomical, and biochemical changes during the transition from fetal to neonatal circulation. Discusses fetal shunts, cardiac remodeling tools (ultrasound, MRI), and how pregnancy complications (IUGR, preeclampsia, preterm delivery) affect cardiac remodeling.
- **Keywords:** Cardiac development, Developmental biology, Fetal heart, Neonatal heart, Transitional physiology

### Miquerol & Kelly (2013) — Organogenesis of the Vertebrate Heart
- **PMID:** 23799628 | **DOI:** [10.1002/wdev.68](https://doi.org/10.1002/wdev.68)
- **Journal:** WIREs Developmental Biology, 2(1):17-29
- **Summary:** Authoritative overview of the entire sequence of cardiac organogenesis: specification and differentiation of myocardial/endocardial cells in anterior lateral mesoderm, heart tube formation, rightward looping, second heart field contributions, progressive differentiation via intercellular signaling (pharyngeal mesoderm, foregut endoderm, neural crest mesenchyme), chamber morphogenesis, cardiac cushion/valve formation, septation, conduction system establishment, and epicardial contributions to coronary vasculature.

### Gittenberger-de Groot et al. (2005) — Basics of Cardiac Development for Understanding Congenital Heart Malformations
- **PMID:** 15611355 | **DOI:** [10.1203/01.PDR.0000148710.69159.61](https://doi.org/10.1203/01.PDR.0000148710.69159.61)
- **Journal:** Pediatric Research, 57(2):169-76
- **Summary:** Reviews the embryonic origin of myocardium, endocardium, and cardiac jelly from cardiogenic plates. Describes genetic cascades directing dextral looping, secondary looping, and outflow tract wedging. Highlights extracardiac cell contributions: neural crest cells (outflow tract septation, pharyngeal arch arteries) and epicardium-derived cells (myocardial maturation, coronary vasculature). Discusses how inductive signals and apoptosis instruct conduction system differentiation.

### Gittenberger-de Groot (2003) — The Quintessence of the Making of the Heart (Mannheimer Lecture)
- **PMID:** 12887075 | **DOI:** [10.1017/s1047951103000337](https://doi.org/10.1017/s1047951103000337)
- **Journal:** Cardiology in the Young, 13(2):175-83
- **Summary:** Clinically-oriented lecture presenting extracardiac cell populations — neural crest cells and epicardially-derived cells — as the "quintessence" of heart development. Without these contributions, embryos uniformly die of cardiac insufficiency. Links specific malformations to these extracardiac contributions.

### Zubrzycki et al. (2024) — Cardiac Development and Factors Influencing the Development of Congenital Heart Defects: Part I
- **PMID:** 39000221 | **DOI:** [10.3390/ijms25137117](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms25137117)
- **Journal:** Int J Mol Sci, 25(13)
- **Summary:** Modern review covering progression from cardiac crescent to linear heart tube to mature heart via cardiac loop formation and septation. Highlights neural crest cells, second heart field cells, and epicardial progenitor cells. Covers both genetic and environmental risk factors for CHDs. Discusses new techniques for studying heart development and their importance for understanding transposition of the great arteries.

## 2. Heart Tube Formation & Looping

### Ebrahimi, Bradley & Hunter (2022) — An Integrative Multiscale View of Early Cardiac Looping
- **PMID:** 35023324 | **DOI:** [10.1002/wsbm.1535](https://doi.org/10.1002/wsbm.1535)
- **Journal:** WIREs Mechanisms of Disease, 14(1):e1535
- **Summary:** Comprehensive computational and experimental review of cardiac looping. Covers formation of cardiac fields, linear heart tube formation, ventral bending, helical torsion, followed by ballooning, septation, and valve formation. Reviews anatomy, biology, genetics, and mechanical processes. Emphasizes the need for integrative computational modeling to understand this complex multiscale process. Aberrant looping results in various congenital heart diseases.

### Hierck et al. (1996) — Differential Expression of Laminin Binding Integrins During Murine Heart Development
- **PMID:** 9019241 | **DOI:** [10.1002/(SICI)1097-0177(199605)206:1<100::AID-AJA9>3.0.CO;2-M](https://doi.org/10.1002/(SICI)1097-0177(199605)206:1<100::AID-AJA9>3.0.CO;2-M)
- **Journal:** Developmental Dynamics, 206(1):100-11
- **Summary:** Detailed analysis of integrin α6 subunit expression during late murine heart development (looping to end of septation). Shows constant high expression in atrial myocardium, decreased expression in ventricular trabecular myocardium. Proposes role for α6-β1 laminin receptor in cell adhesion at sites of high mechanical stress. Provides insight into roles of integrins and ECM ligands during embryonic development.

## 3. Second Heart Field & Cardiac Progenitors

### Kelly (2012) — The Second Heart Field
- **PMID:** 22449840 | **DOI:** [10.1016/B978-0-12-387786-4.00002-6](https://doi.org/10.1016/B978-0-12-387786-4.00002-6)
- **Journal:** Current Topics in Developmental Biology, 100:33-65
- **Summary:** Landmark review of the second heart field (SHF) — multipotent progenitor cells in pharyngeal mesoderm that give rise to myocardium, smooth muscle, and endothelial cells. SHF cells contribute progressively to the poles of the elongating heart tube during looping morphogenesis. Reviews regulation by multiple intercellular signaling pathways and transcriptional regulatory networks. Discusses clinically relevant subpopulations, evolution, and lineage relationships with craniofacial skeletal muscles.

### Francou et al. (2013) — Second Heart Field Cardiac Progenitor Cells in the Early Mouse Embryo
- **PMID:** 23051926 | **DOI:** [10.1016/j.bbamcr.2012.10.003](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bbamcr.2012.10.003)
- **Journal:** Biochimica et Biophysica Acta, 1833(4):795-8
- **Summary:** Reviews how SHF cells originate in splanchnic mesoderm medial to the cardiac crescent, patterned into anterior and posterior domains. Perturbation of SHF deployment results in conotruncal and atrial septal defects. Focuses on regulation of progenitor cell proliferation, progressive differentiation control, and patterning in the dorsal pericardial wall.

### Cortes et al. (2018) — Epithelial Properties of the Second Heart Field
- **PMID:** 29301846 | **DOI:** [10.1161/CIRCRESAHA.117.310838](https://doi.org/10.1161/CIRCRESAHA.117.310838)
- **Journal:** Circulation Research, 122(1):142-154
- **Summary:** Highlights the importance of epithelial architecture and cell adhesion in SHF cells, which constitute an atypical apicobasally polarized epithelium with dynamic basal filopodia. Discusses 22q11.2 deletion syndrome gene TBX1, noncanonical Wnt signaling, planar cell polarity pathway, and new biomechanical models of heart tube elongation involving regional cell intercalation, cohesion, and epithelial tension.

### Kelly, Buckingham & Moorman (2014) — Heart Fields and Cardiac Morphogenesis
- **PMID:** 25274757 | **DOI:** [10.1101/cshperspect.a015750](https://doi.org/10.1101/cshperspect.a015750)
- **Journal:** Cold Spring Harbor Perspectives in Medicine, 4(10)
- **Summary:** Focuses on two key steps: (i) progressive addition of SHF progenitor cells driving heart tube extension, and (ii) emergence of patterned proliferation generating distinct cardiac chambers. During the transition, the major proliferation site switches from progenitor cells outside the heart to within the embryonic myocardium. Provides framework for understanding congenital heart defects and regulation of myocardial proliferation relevant for cardiac repair.

### Xiong & He (2020) — Single-Cell Transcriptomic Analysis of Cardiac Progenitor Differentiation
- **PMID:** 32430537 | **DOI:** [10.1007/s11886-020-01285-2](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11886-020-01285-2)
- **Journal:** Current Cardiology Reports, 22(6):38
- **Summary:** Reviews single-cell RNA sequencing to catalog cardiac cell types during early heart development. Pseudo-time analysis reveals temporal continuum from E6.5 to E9.5, implicating early cardiac lineage restriction during gastrulation. First and second heart field progenitors adopt different differentiation strategies with distinct transcriptional regulation.

## 4. Cardiac Neural Crest & Outflow Tract

### Yamagishi (2021) — Cardiac Neural Crest
- **PMID:** 32071091 | **DOI:** [10.1101/cshperspect.a036715](https://doi.org/10.1101/cshperspect.a036715)
- **Journal:** Cold Spring Harbor Perspectives in Biology, 13(1)
- **Summary:** Definitive review of cardiac neural crest (CNC) cells — pluripotent cells from the dorsal neural tube that migrate and contribute to pharyngeal arch artery remodeling and cardiac outflow tract septation. Reviews molecular cascades regulating induction, specification, delamination, and migration. Focuses on reciprocal signaling between CNC and second heart field, providing insights into CHDs and human syndromes.

### Erhardt et al. (2021) — The Cardiac Neural Crest Cells in Heart Development and Congenital Heart Defects
- **PMID:** 34436231 | **DOI:** [10.3390/jcdd8080089](https://doi.org/10.3390/jcdd8080089)
- **Journal:** J Cardiovasc Dev Dis, 8(8)
- **Summary:** Reviews contributions of cardiac NCCs to pharyngeal arch arteries, cardiac outflow tract, cardiac valves, and interventricular septum. Discusses orchestrating signaling pathways for proper migration, compaction, and differentiation. Any dysregulation leads to CHDs.

### George, Maldonado-Velez & Firulli (2020) — The Heart of the Neural Crest
- **PMID:** 33060096 | **DOI:** [10.1242/dev.188706](https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.188706)
- **Journal:** Development, 147(20)
- **Summary:** Reviews origin, migration and fates of cardiac NCCs. Highlights recent lineage-tracing experiments showing cNCCs can give rise to mature cardiomyocytes. These cNCC-derived cardiomyocytes appear required for successful repair and regeneration of injured hearts (zebrafish, potentially mammalian).

### Thattaliyath & Firulli (2024) — Neural Crest (Book Chapter)
- **PMID:** 38884708 | **DOI:** [10.1007/978-3-031-44087-8_6](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-031-44087-8_6)
- **Journal:** Advances in Experimental Medicine and Biology, 1441:125-143
- **Summary:** Discusses cardiac neural crest cells in formation of the septum dividing the arterial pole. Reviews their influence on development of great arteries, thymus, thyroid, parathyroids. Covers associated syndromes: 22q11.2 deletion syndrome, DiGeorge, CHARGE, Noonan, LEOPARD, Treacher Collins, Jacobsen, and Velocardiofacial syndromes.

### Neeb et al. (2013) — Cardiac Outflow Tract Anomalies
- **PMID:** 24014420 | **DOI:** [10.1002/wdev.98](https://doi.org/10.1002/wdev.98)
- **Journal:** WIREs Developmental Biology, 2(4):499-530
- **Summary:** The OFT is a focal point for many multifactorial CHDs. Establishes that OFT alignment and septation are independent processes, invested via separate SHF and CNC lineages. Reviews complex reciprocal signaling between SHF and cardiac NCC during various stages of OFT morphogenesis.

### Plein, Fantin & Ruhrberg (2015) — Neural Crest Cells in Cardiovascular Development
- **PMID:** 25662261 | **DOI:** [10.1016/bs.ctdb.2014.11.006](https://doi.org/10.1016/bs.ctdb.2014.11.006)
- **Journal:** Current Topics in Developmental Biology, 111:183-200
- **Summary:** Reviews CNC-mediated vascular remodeling of pharyngeal artery pairs into aortic arch and outflow tract septation. Discusses signaling pathways important for these processes, contributions to cardiac valves, and the contentious role of cardiac NCCs in myocardium and conductive system development.

### Jain, Rentschler & Epstein (2010) — Notch and Cardiac Outflow Tract Development
- **PMID:** 20201902 | **DOI:** [10.1111/j.1749-6632.2009.05099.x](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1749-6632.2009.05099.x)
- **Journal:** Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences, 1188:184-90
- **Summary:** Demonstrates that Jagged1/Notch signaling within the second heart field initiates a cascade involving Fgf8, Bmp4, and downstream effectors that modulate OFT development and aortic arch artery patterning. Complex tissue-tissue interactions and pathway integration orchestrate proper patterning.

## 5. Conduction System Development

### Moorman et al. (1998) — Development of the Cardiac Conduction System
- **PMID:** 9546372 | **DOI:** [10.1161/01.res.82.6.629](https://doi.org/10.1161/01.res.82.6.629)
- **Journal:** Circulation Research, 82(6):629-44
- **Summary:** Classic review of cardiac conduction system development, covering embryonic induction, contractile protein expression, and the formation of specialized conduction tissues in the developing heart.

### Jongbloed et al. (2012) — Normal and Abnormal Development of the Cardiac Conduction System
- **PMID:** 22664174 | **DOI:** [10.1016/j.diff.2012.04.006](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.diff.2012.04.006)
- **Journal:** Differentiation, 84(1):131-48
- **Summary:** Reviews CCS development in relation to maturation of the four-chambered heart. Disturbed cardiac structure development is often accompanied by CCS formation defects. Conduction disturbances and arrhythmias occur at anatomical predilection sites. Covers embryonic CCS development in relation to clinical arrhythmias and specific abnormalities in patients with CHD.

### Gourdie et al. (2003) — Development of the Cardiac Pacemaking and Conduction System
- **PMID:** 12768657 | **DOI:** [10.1002/bdrc.10008](https://doi.org/10.1002/bdrc.10008)
- **Journal:** Birth Defects Research Part C, 69(1):46-57
- **Summary:** Reviews the heterogeneous tissues collectively forming the pacemaking and conduction system (PCS). Molecular biology and modern embryology are uncovering mechanisms governing induction, patterning, and developmental integration. Significant advances in understanding the His-Purkinje system using chick and mouse models.

### Cheng et al. (1999) — Development of the Cardiac Conduction System Involves Recruitment Within a Multipotent Cardiomyogenic Lineage
- **PMID:** 10529421 | **DOI:** [10.1242/dev.126.22.5041](https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.126.22.5041)
- **Journal:** Development, 126(22):5041-9
- **Summary:** Using lineage-tracing with adenoviral and retroviral constructs, demonstrates that both peripheral and central conduction tissues originate from cardiomyogenic progenitors with no detectable contribution from neuroectoderm. The CCS does not develop by outgrowth from a prespecified pool but via progressive and localized recruitment of multipotent cardiomyogenic cells.

### Mohan et al. (2018) — Embryonic Tbx3+ Cardiomyocytes Form the Mature Cardiac Conduction System by Progressive Fate Restriction
- **PMID:** 30042181 | **DOI:** [10.1242/dev.167361](https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.167361)
- **Journal:** Development, 145(17)
- **Summary:** Using genetic inducible fate mapping, shows that Tbx3+ cells in the early heart tube form the definitive CCS components (except Purkinje fibers) by progressive fate restriction. Identifies a Tbx3+ population in the outflow tract forming the atrioventricular bundle. Tbx3 as a useful tool for studying and treating CCS diseases.

### Sedmera et al. (2015) — Arrhythmias in the Developing Heart
- **PMID:** 25363044 | **DOI:** [10.1111/apha.12418](https://doi.org/10.1111/apha.12418)
- **Journal:** Acta Physiologica, 213(2):303-20
- **Summary:** Reviews specific rhythm disturbances that appear prior to birth and affect fetal development. Covers congenital heart block, pre-excitation, extrasystoles, and long QT syndrome. Includes prenatal animal model literature providing insights into pathogenesis and possible treatment strategies.

### de la Rosa et al. (2013) — Functional Suppression of Kcnq1 Leads to Early Sodium Channel Remodelling and Cardiac Conduction System Dysmorphogenesis
- **PMID:** 23542581 | **DOI:** [10.1093/cvr/cvt076](https://doi.org/10.1093/cvr/cvt076)
- **Journal:** Cardiovascular Research, 98(3):504-14
- **Summary:** Using a Long QT Syndrome transgenic mouse model, demonstrates early sodium channel remodeling secondary to IKs blockage, leading to morphological and functional anomalies in the developing ventricular conduction system and cardiac hypertrophy. Provides new insights into fetal and neonatal cardiac electrophysiological disorders.

## 6. Signaling Pathways (Wnt, Notch, BMP)

### Wang et al. (2013) — Endocardial to Myocardial Notch-Wnt-BMP Axis Regulates Early Heart Valve Development
- **PMID:** 23560082 | **DOI:** [10.1371/journal.pone.0060244](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0060244)
- **Journal:** PLoS ONE, 8(4):e60244
- **Summary:** Demonstrates that Jagged1-Notch1 signaling in endocardial cells induces Wnt4 expression, which acts as a paracrine factor to upregulate Bmp2 in adjacent AVC myocardium, signaling EMT for heart valve formation. Disruption of endocardial Jagged1-Notch1 is essential for endocardial-to-mesenchymal transformation and early valvular cushion formation.

### Klaus et al. (2012) — Wnt/β-catenin and Bmp Signals Control Distinct Sets of Transcription Factors in Cardiac Progenitor Cells
- **PMID:** 22711842 | **DOI:** [10.1073/pnas.1121236109](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1121236109)
- **Journal:** PNAS, 109(27):10921-6
- **Summary:** Defines the hierarchy of signaling events controlling cardiac-specific transcription factors during differentiation. Wnt/β-catenin and Bmp act downstream of Notch/RBPJ. Wnt and Bmp4 activate specific and non-overlapping cardiac genes: Nkx2-5, Isl1, Baf60c (Wnt/β-catenin) and Gata4, SRF, Mef2c (Bmp signaling).

### Chen et al. (2008) — Notch Signaling Respecifies the Hemangioblast to a Cardiac Fate
- **PMID:** 18820686 | **DOI:** [10.1038/nbt.1497](https://doi.org/10.1038/nbt.1497)
- **Journal:** Nature Biotechnology, 26(10):1169-78
- **Summary:** Shows that Notch pathway signaling can efficiently respecify hemangioblasts to a cardiac fate, generating >60% cardiomyocyte populations. Microarray analyses reveal respecification is mediated through coordinated regulation of BMP and Wnt pathways by Notch. Uncovers potential role for Notch in cardiac development and provides approach for generating cardiac progenitors from ES cells.

### Sarmah et al. (2016) — Embryonic Ethanol Exposure Dysregulates BMP and Notch Signaling, Leading to Persistent AV Valve Defects in Zebrafish
- **PMID:** 27556898 | **DOI:** [10.1371/journal.pone.0161205](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0161205)
- **Journal:** PLoS ONE, 11(8):e0161205
- **Summary:** Uses zebrafish FASD model to show that early ethanol exposure disrupts the Bmp signaling gradient and causes mislocalization of Notch signaling cells during AV valve formation. Leads to faulty valve morphogenesis that persists into juvenile stages. Demonstrates how environmental insults can disrupt normal signaling cascades.

### Manisastry, Han & Linask (2006) — Early Temporal-Specific Responses and Differential Sensitivity to Lithium and Wnt-3A During Heart Development
- **PMID:** 16804895 | **DOI:** [10.1002/dvdy.20878](https://doi.org/10.1002/dvdy.20878)
- **Journal:** Developmental Dynamics, 235(8):2160-74
- **Summary:** Demonstrates temporal-specific responses and differential sensitivities to lithium/Wnt-3A exposure during early heart development. Lithium, Wnt-3A, and GSK-3β inhibitor SB415286 affect cardiac specification stages. Notch-1 and Dkk-1 expression patterns are altered by lithium exposure and BMP inhibition.

## 7. Epicardium & Coronary Vessel Development

### He, Lui & Zhou (2020) — The Formation of Coronary Vessels in Cardiac Development and Disease
- **PMID:** 31636078 | **DOI:** [10.1101/cshperspect.a037168](https://doi.org/10.1101/cshperspect.a037168)
- **Journal:** Cold Spring Harbor Perspectives in Biology, 12(5)
- **Summary:** Identifies novel origins and new cellular/molecular mechanisms of coronary vessel formation in the postnatal heart: collateral artery formation, endocardial-to-endothelial differentiation, and mesenchymal-to-endothelial transition. Opens new possibilities for targeting neovascularization in cardiac repair and regeneration.

### Reese, Mikawa & Bader (2002) — Development of the Coronary Vessel System
- **PMID:** 12411389 | **DOI:** [10.1161/01.res.0000038961.53759.3c](https://doi.org/10.1161/01.res.0000038961.53759.3c)
- **Journal:** Circulation Research, 91(9):761-8
- **Summary:** Presents descriptive embryology and developmental mechanisms of coronary vessel formation. Reviews cell fate determination, cell and epithelial migration, epithelial/mesenchymal transition, and patterning of the complex three-dimensional coronary vasculature.

### Olivey, Compton & Barnett (2004) — Coronary Vessel Development: The Epicardium Delivers
- **PMID:** 15451517 | **DOI:** [10.1016/j.tcm.2004.07.001](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tcm.2004.07.001)
- **Journal:** Trends in Cardiovascular Medicine, 14(6):247-51
- **Summary:** Identifies the importance of cells derived from the proepicardium and epicardium in coronary vessel formation. Reviews basic steps, implicated molecules, and pressing research questions.

### Tomanek (2005) — Formation of the Coronary Vasculature During Development
- **PMID:** 16308734 | **DOI:** [10.1007/s10456-005-9014-9](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10456-005-9014-9)
- **Journal:** Angiogenesis, 8(3):273-84
- **Summary:** Reviews the temporally regulated events of coronary formation: vasculogenesis, angiogenesis, arteriogenesis, and remodeling. From proepicardial cell migration to epicardium formation, through epithelial-mesenchymal transformation of endothelial, smooth muscle, and fibroblast cells, to aortic ring penetration forming ostia. Covers regulatory molecules and angiogenic growth factor interplay.

### Mu et al. (2005) — Cellular and Molecular Mechanisms of Coronary Vessel Development
- **PMID:** 15920999 | **DOI:** [10.1191/1358863x05vm584ra](https://doi.org/10.1191/1358863x05vm584ra)
- **Journal:** Vascular Medicine, 10(1):37-44
- **Summary:** Reviews the key events: proepicardial organ generation, epicardium formation, subepicardial mesenchymal cell generation, and formation/remodeling/maturation of the vascular plexus. Discusses transcription factors, adhesion molecules, growth factors, and signaling molecules.

## 11. Fetal-to-Neonatal Transition & Brain Impact

### Donofrio, Duplessis & Limperopoulos (2011) — Impact of Congenital Heart Disease on Fetal Brain Development and Injury
- **PMID:** 21881507 | **DOI:** [10.1097/MOP.0b013e32834aa583](https://doi.org/10.1097/MOP.0b013e32834aa583)
- **Journal:** Current Opinion in Pediatrics, 23(5):502-11
- **Summary:** Reviews the impact of CHD on developing brain in utero and during infancy. Neurologic abnormalities are detectable prior to surgery. Discusses altered cerebral blood flow in utero, brain development risk, and in-utero/postnatal brain injury. Advocates for routine neurodevelopmental evaluation and fetal management strategies to improve in-utero hemodynamics and cerebral oxygen delivery.

### Barker (2004) — The Developmental Origins of Adult Disease
- **PMID:** 15640511 | **DOI:** [10.1080/07315724.2004.10719428](https://doi.org/10.1080/07315724.2004.10719428)
- **Journal:** J Am Coll Nutr, 23(6 Suppl):588S-595S
- **Summary:** Establishes that low birthweight is associated with increased rates of coronary heart disease, stroke, hypertension, and type 2 diabetes. These are consequences of developmental plasticity — one genotype giving rise to different states in response to different environmental conditions during development. Impaired prenatal growth combined with rapid childhood weight gain exacerbates effects.

## 12. Macrophages, Immune Cells & Regeneration

### Xu, Gonzalez & Yutzey (2024) — Macrophage Lineages in Heart Development and Regeneration
- **PMID:** 38556420 | **DOI:** [10.1016/bs.ctdb.2024.01.004](https://doi.org/10.1016/bs.ctdb.2024.01.004)
- **Journal:** Current Topics in Developmental Biology, 156:1-17
- **Summary:** During development, macrophage subpopulations from hematopoietic progenitors take up residence in the developing heart. ### Diguet & Meilhac (2014) — Cilia and Heart Morphogenesis

### Giudice & Cooper (2014) — RNA-Binding Proteins in Heart Development

- **Summary:** Reviews RNA-binding proteins (RBPs) as key players of posttranscriptional regulation during heart development. RBPs control alternative splicing, mRNA stability, translation, and poly-adenylation. The heart is the first organ to form and acquire functionality during embryonic development.
