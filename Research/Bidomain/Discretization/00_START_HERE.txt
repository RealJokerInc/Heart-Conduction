WHAT YOU NEED TO KNOW FIRST
================================================================================

THE PROBLEM:
  Monodomain model assumes equal anisotropy ratios between intracellular and
  extracellular conductivities. In reality:
    - Intracellular: σ_L/σ_T ≈ 10:1
    - Extracellular: σ_L/σ_T ≈ 2.5:1
  
  This invalidates monodomain for:
    • Defibrillation
    • Extracellular stimulation
    • Virtual electrode effects
    • ECG generation
    • Ischemia modeling

THE SOLUTION:
  Use bidomain model with separate intracellular (φ_i) and extracellular (φ_e)
  potentials. This:
    • Captures extracellular field effects
    • Maintains current conservation
    • Doubles computational cost (2-4× per timestep)
    • Increases memory by 4×
    • Requires advanced solvers (AMG preconditioner essential)

THE EQUATIONS:
  Parabolic (time-varying):
    χ·Cm·∂Vm/∂t = -χ·Iion(Vm,w) + ∇·(Di·∇Vm) + ∇·(Di·∇φe) + Istim_i

  Elliptic (instantaneous):
    0 = ∇·((Di+De)·∇φe) + ∇·(Di·∇Vm) + Istim_e

  where: Vm = φ_i - φ_e (transmembrane voltage)

KEY CONCEPTS AT A GLANCE
================================================================================

DISCRETIZATION METHODS:
  
  FEM (Finite Element Method)
    - Accuracy: O(h²)
    - Geometry: Complex, arbitrary
    - Implementation: Medium complexity
    - Memory: Higher
    - Recommendation: PRIMARY METHOD for ventricle

  FDM (Finite Difference Method)
    - Accuracy: O(h²)
    - Geometry: Structured grids only
    - Implementation: Simple
    - Memory: Lower
    - Recommendation: Alternative for regular domains

  FVM (Finite Volume Method)
    - Accuracy: O(h)
    - Geometry: Unstructured
    - Implementation: Complex
    - Memory: Medium
    - Recommendation: Conservation-focused applications

COMPUTATIONAL COSTS:
  - Monodomain: Baseline (1×)
  - Bidomain: 2-4× per timestep
  - With solver optimization (AMG): Still 2× overall
  - Memory: 4× baseline

WHEN TO USE BIDOMAIN:
  YES if:
    ✓ Defibrillation/high-voltage stimulation
    ✓ Extracellular recording (ECG, body surface)
    ✓ Virtual electrode polarization important
    ✓ Ischemia with regional conductivity changes
    ✓ Research requiring highest accuracy

  NO if:
    ✗ Simple conduction velocity measurements
    ✗ Limited computational resources
    ✗ Computational time critical
    ✗ Monodomain produces acceptable results

COMMON QUESTIONS ANSWERED
================================================================================

Q: How long will implementation take?
A: 2-8 weeks depending on existing infrastructure
   - Phase 1 (FEM): 2-3 weeks
   - Phase 2 (solvers): 1-2 weeks
   - Phase 3-4 (optional): 1-3 weeks
   - Validation: 1-2 weeks

Q: How much memory is needed?
A: ~4× monodomain baseline
   - Monodomain ventricle: ~100 MB
   - Bidomain ventricle: ~400 MB
   - With bath: ~1-2 GB

Q: Will it be faster or slower?
A: Slower per timestep (2-4×) but physiologically richer
   - Enables defibrillation studies
   - Produces ECG output
   - Captures virtual electrode effects

Q: Can I optimize it?
A: Yes, several strategies:
   - AMG preconditioner (5-10× solver speedup)
   - Operator splitting (cheaper reaction solve)
   - Coarse timesteps for φ_e equation
   - GPU acceleration (10-50× potential)

Q: Do I need all three methods (FEM/FDM/FVM)?
A: No, FEM recommended as primary
   - FEM: Most versatile for cardiac geometry
   - FDM: Alternative for structured meshes
   - FVM: If conservation is critical

Q: Is monodomain ever sufficient?
A: Yes, for many applications:
   - Conduction velocity mapping
   - Activation sequences
   - Simple stimulus-response
   - Limited resources

Q: How accurate is it?
A: Grid-dependent but predictable:
   - FEM: O(h²) convergence (100 μm → 50 μm = 4× smaller error)
   - FVM: O(h) convergence
   - Typical 100 μm mesh: ~2% error vs. finer mesh
