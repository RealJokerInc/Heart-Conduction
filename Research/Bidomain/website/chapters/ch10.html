Chapter 10

Explicit Diffusion Solvers

After the reaction substep updates the ionic variables, the diffusion substep must advance $V_m$ under pure diffusion. Explicit time-stepping methods — from the workhorse Forward Euler to the high-accuracy RK4 — form a family of increasing sophistication. This chapter walks through each one, starting from intuition and building to implementation-ready equations.

10.1   Forward Euler and the CFL Condition

The simplest idea for advancing the diffusion equation is also the most intuitive: look at the current state, compute how fast things are changing, and take a step forward. This is Forward Euler — the computational equivalent of "if I'm heading north at 60 mph, in one hour I'll be 60 miles north." It works beautifully as long as you don't take steps that are too large. Take too big a step, and the solution oscillates wildly and blows up — this limit is called the CFL condition, and it is the fundamental price of simplicity.

Here is the core idea: at each timestep, we evaluate the right-hand side of the diffusion equation (the spatial Laplacian acting on $V_m$), multiply by $\Delta t$, and add the result to the current state. We never look ahead — only at where we are. That is what makes it "explicit": the new state is computed entirely from the old state, with no system of equations to solve.

In the semi-discrete form from equation (8.2), the diffusion substep is the ODE system $\mathbf{M}\,d\mathbf{V}/dt = -\mathbf{K}\,\mathbf{V}$. Forward Euler approximates the time derivative by a forward difference:

(10.1) $$\mathbf{V}^{n+1} = \mathbf{V}^n + \Delta t\;\mathbf{M}^{-1}\bigl(-\mathbf{K}\,\mathbf{V}^n\bigr)$$

When using FDM with lumped mass ($\mathbf{M} = \mathbf{I}$), this simplifies to $\mathbf{V}^{n+1} = \mathbf{V}^n - \Delta t\,\mathbf{K}\,\mathbf{V}^n$. Each grid point's update depends only on its neighbors at time level $n$ — no global communication, no matrix solve. This is why Forward Euler is trivially parallelizable and ideal for GPU computation.

The price is the CFL stability condition. For a regular grid with spacing $h$ and diffusivity $d$, Forward Euler is stable only when:

(10.2) $$\Delta t \leq \frac{h^2}{2\,n_{\text{dim}}\,d_{\max}}$$

where $n_{\text{dim}}$ is the spatial dimension and $d_{\max}$ is the largest eigenvalue of the conductivity tensor. For typical cardiac parameters ($h = 0.2$ mm, $d_{\max} = 0.174$ mm$^2$/ms, 2D), this gives $\Delta t \leq 0.057$ ms — tight, but workable for the $\Delta t = 0.01{-}0.05$ ms range that the ionic model already requires.

Worked CFL example. Suppose we want to simulate a 2D cardiac sheet with $h = 0.1$ mm (a fine mesh) and longitudinal conductivity $d_L = 0.174$ mm$^2$/ms. Then $\Delta t_{\max} = (0.1)^2 / (2 \times 2 \times 0.174) = 0.01 / 0.696 = 0.0144$ ms. That is below the typical ionic timestep of 0.02 ms — so for fine meshes, the diffusion stability constraint becomes the bottleneck, not the ionic kinetics. This is exactly why Chapter 10's implicit methods exist.

The CFL condition scales as $h^2$. Halve the mesh spacing and the maximum timestep drops by a factor of four. For fine 3D meshes ($h = 0.1$ mm), this can force $\Delta t$ below what the ionic solver needs, making explicit methods the bottleneck. This is the primary motivation for implicit methods (Chapter 10).

10.2   Why Go Beyond Forward Euler?

Imagine driving from New York to Boston. Forward Euler checks your speedometer once at the start and assumes that speed holds for the entire trip. If the road curves, you end up in the ocean. A smarter driver checks the speedometer multiple times along the way — at the start, at the midpoint, near the end — and uses all those readings to estimate where they actually end up. That is what Runge–Kutta methods do: sample the slope multiple times within each timestep and combine the samples into a much better estimate of the true solution.

The key insight is that Forward Euler's error comes from assuming the derivative is constant across the entire step. In reality, the derivative changes as $V_m$ changes. By evaluating the derivative at intermediate points within the step, we can capture this curvature. More evaluations = better curvature tracking = higher-order accuracy. The family of methods that does this systematically is called Runge–Kutta (RK) methods, named after Carl Runge and Martin Kutta who developed them in the early 1900s.

Every explicit RK method follows the same recipe: (1) evaluate the right-hand side at several carefully chosen points, (2) take a weighted average of those evaluations, (3) use that average as the effective slope for the timestep. The differences between RK1 (Forward Euler), RK2, and RK4 are simply how many sample points they use and how they weight them.

10.3   RK2 — Heun's Method (The Two-Sample Correction)

RK2 is like a rough draft followed by a revision. You start by making the same guess Forward Euler would (the "predictor") — but instead of stopping there, you evaluate the slope at that predicted point too, and then average the two slopes. The result is far more accurate because you have sampled the derivative at both ends of the interval instead of just one.

Figure 9.1 — RK2 (Heun's method) visualized. The red arrow is the Forward Euler predictor ($k_1$ slope). The blue arrow is the slope evaluated at the predicted point ($k_2$). The green arrow is the average of both slopes — which lands much closer to the true solution (dashed gray).

Formally, RK2 (Heun's method) advances from $V^n$ to $V^{n+1}$ in two stages. Let $f(V) = \mathbf{M}^{-1}(-\mathbf{K}\,V)$ denote the right-hand side of the diffusion ODE:

(10.3) $$\boxed{\begin{aligned} k_1 &= \Delta t \; f\!\left(V^n\right) & &\text{(slope at start)} \\[4pt] k_2 &= \Delta t \; f\!\left(V^n + k_1\right) & &\text{(slope at FE predictor)} \\[4pt] V^{n+1} &= V^n + \tfrac{1}{2}\left(k_1 + k_2\right) & &\text{(average both slopes)} \end{aligned}}$$

That final line is the key: $V^{n+1}$ is not the Forward Euler predictor $V^n + k_1$. Instead, we go back to $V^n$ and use the average of the two slopes, $\frac{1}{2}(k_1 + k_2)$. This is where the name "predictor–corrector" comes from: $k_1$ predicts, and the averaging with $k_2$ corrects.

Expanding $f(\mathbf{V}) = \mathcal{L}\,\mathbf{V}$ where $\mathcal{L}$ is the discrete diffusion operator (i.e., apply_diffusion(V) from Chapter 7), the matrix form computed at each timestep is:

(10.3b) $$\boxed{\begin{aligned} \mathbf{k}_1 &= \mathcal{L}\,\mathbf{V}^n \\[4pt] \mathbf{k}_2 &= \mathcal{L}\bigl(\mathbf{V}^n + \Delta t\,\mathbf{k}_1\bigr) \\[4pt] \mathbf{V}^{n+1} &= \mathbf{V}^n + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\bigl(\mathbf{k}_1 + \mathbf{k}_2\bigr) \end{aligned}}$$

Note that each $\mathbf{k}_i$ here is a bare rate vector (units of mV/ms) — the factor $\Delta t$ is applied only at the final update. This is the convention used by the solver code and is mathematically equivalent to the standard form (10.3) where $k_i = \Delta t \cdot f(V)$.

Why exactly $\frac{1}{2}$? Heun's method is designed so that the Taylor expansion of $V^{n+1}$ matches the true solution through $O(\Delta t^2)$. Forward Euler matches only through $O(\Delta t)$. By evaluating $f$ at the predicted point and averaging, we capture the second-order term $\frac{1}{2}\Delta t^2 f'(V^n) f(V^n)$ that Forward Euler misses. The coefficient $\frac{1}{2}$ is not arbitrary — it is the unique weight that achieves this cancellation for the two-stage Heun form.

Worked example. Consider $dV/dt = -2V$ with $V^0 = 1.0$ and $\Delta t = 0.1$. The exact solution at $t = 0.1$ is $e^{-0.2} = 0.81873$.

Forward Euler: $V^1 = 1.0 + 0.1 \times (-2 \times 1.0) = 0.800$. Error = $0.01873$.

RK2 (Heun):
$k_1 = 0.1 \times (-2 \times 1.0) = -0.2$
$\tilde{V} = 1.0 + (-0.2) = 0.8$
$k_2 = 0.1 \times (-2 \times 0.8) = -0.16$
$V^1 = 1.0 + \frac{1}{2}(-0.2 + (-0.16)) = 1.0 - 0.18 = 0.820$. Error = $0.00127$.

RK2's error ($0.00127$) is 15× smaller than Forward Euler's ($0.01873$) for the same timestep — and it only cost one extra function evaluation.

10.4   RK4 — The Classical Four-Stage Method

If RK2 is checking your speedometer twice, RK4 checks it four times: at the start, twice at the midpoint (using two different estimates), and once at the end. It then combines all four readings with a specific weighted average that is tuned to capture the curvature of the solution remarkably well. RK4 is so accurate that it has been the default ODE integrator in science and engineering since the 1900s — a century of trust.

The genius of RK4 is its midpoint sampling. Rather than just checking the endpoints (like RK2), it takes two independent samples at $t^n + \Delta t/2$. Why the midpoint? Because the midpoint rule for integration is second-order accurate all by itself, and by doing it twice (with refined estimates), the errors cancel to fourth order. The final weighted average — giving the midpoint samples twice the weight of the endpoints — is not a coincidence. It mirrors Simpson's rule from numerical integration, which achieves fourth-order accuracy by weighting the endpoints and midpoint as $\frac{1}{6}$, $\frac{4}{6}$, $\frac{1}{6}$.

Figure 9.2 — The four stages of RK4. Each colored line shows a slope evaluation; the green result is the weighted average $\frac{1}{6}(k_1 + 2k_2 + 2k_3 + k_4)$. The midpoint slopes ($k_2$, $k_3$) get double weight, mirroring Simpson's rule.

The classical RK4 method evaluates the right-hand side $f(V) = \mathbf{M}^{-1}(-\mathbf{K}\,V)$ at four carefully chosen points:

(10.4) $$\boxed{\begin{aligned} k_1 &= \Delta t \; f\!\left(V^n\right) & &\text{(slope at } t^n\text{)} \\[4pt] k_2 &= \Delta t \; f\!\left(V^n + \tfrac{1}{2}\,k_1\right) & &\text{(1st midpoint, using } k_1 \text{ to get there)} \\[4pt] k_3 &= \Delta t \; f\!\left(V^n + \tfrac{1}{2}\,k_2\right) & &\text{(2nd midpoint, using } k_2 \text{ to get there)} \\[4pt] k_4 &= \Delta t \; f\!\left(V^n + k_3\right) & &\text{(slope at } t^{n+1}\text{, using } k_3 \text{ to get there)} \\[6pt] V^{n+1} &= V^n + \tfrac{1}{6}\bigl(k_1 + 2k_2 + 2k_3 + k_4\bigr) & &\text{(weighted average)} \end{aligned}}$$

Read the stages as a conversation: $k_1$ says "here's the slope at the start." $k_2$ says "okay, I used your slope to walk to the midpoint — here's the slope there." $k_3$ says "let me try the midpoint again using $k_2$'s improved estimate." $k_4$ says "and here's the slope at the far end, using $k_3$." The final weighted average gives the midpoint samples ($k_2, k_3$) double weight because the midpoint carries more information about the interval's curvature — exactly as in Simpson's 1-4-1 quadrature rule.

In discrete matrix form with $\mathcal{L}$ as the spatial operator:

(10.4b) $$\boxed{\begin{aligned} \mathbf{k}_1 &= \mathcal{L}\,\mathbf{V}^n \\[3pt] \mathbf{k}_2 &= \mathcal{L}\bigl(\mathbf{V}^n + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\,\mathbf{k}_1\bigr) \\[3pt] \mathbf{k}_3 &= \mathcal{L}\bigl(\mathbf{V}^n + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\,\mathbf{k}_2\bigr) \\[3pt] \mathbf{k}_4 &= \mathcal{L}\bigl(\mathbf{V}^n + \Delta t\,\mathbf{k}_3\bigr) \\[5pt] \mathbf{V}^{n+1} &= \mathbf{V}^n + \frac{\Delta t}{6}\bigl(\mathbf{k}_1 + 2\,\mathbf{k}_2 + 2\,\mathbf{k}_3 + \mathbf{k}_4\bigr) \end{aligned}}$$

Again, each $\mathbf{k}_i$ is a bare rate (mV/ms), not pre-multiplied by $\Delta t$. The factor $\Delta t$ appears only at the final update, which avoids four unnecessary multiplications per step.

Why these specific weights? The weights $\frac{1}{6}(1, 2, 2, 1)$ are not chosen by trial and error. They come from matching the Taylor expansion of $V(t^n + \Delta t)$ through the $\Delta t^4$ term. There are 8 "order conditions" that a 4th-order method must satisfy (corresponding to the 8 rooted trees with up to 4 vertices in Butcher's theory). The classical RK4 tableau — with its midpoint-endpoint structure — is the simplest 4-stage method satisfying all 8 conditions. No 3-stage explicit method can achieve 4th-order accuracy.

Worked example. Same test: $dV/dt = -2V$, $V^0 = 1.0$, $\Delta t = 0.1$. Exact answer: $e^{-0.2} = 0.818731$.

$k_1 = 0.1 \times (-2 \times 1.0) = -0.200$
$k_2 = 0.1 \times (-2 \times (1.0 - 0.100)) = 0.1 \times (-1.800) = -0.180$
$k_3 = 0.1 \times (-2 \times (1.0 - 0.090)) = 0.1 \times (-1.820) = -0.182$
$k_4 = 0.1 \times (-2 \times (1.0 - 0.182)) = 0.1 \times (-1.636) = -0.1636$

$V^1 = 1.0 + \frac{1}{6}(-0.200 + 2(-0.180) + 2(-0.182) + (-0.1636))$
$\phantom{V^1} = 1.0 + \frac{1}{6}(-1.0876) = 1.0 - 0.18127 = 0.81873$

Error = $0.000001$ — five orders of magnitude smaller than Forward Euler, for just 4× the cost. This is the power of fourth-order convergence.

10.5   Stability Regions: How Large Can $\Delta t$ Be?

Every explicit method has a "safe zone" — a region in the complex plane where the timestep is small enough that the solution stays bounded. Step outside this zone, and the solution blows up exponentially. The size and shape of this safe zone determines the practical maximum timestep.

For the test equation $dV/dt = \lambda V$ (where $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of the diffusion operator), one timestep gives $V^{n+1} = R(z)\,V^n$ where $z = \lambda\,\Delta t$ and $R(z)$ is the stability function. The method is stable when $|R(z)| \leq 1$. For diffusion, $\lambda$ is real and negative (eigenvalues of $-\mathbf{K}$), so we only need the intersection of the stability region with the negative real axis.

The stability functions for each method are:

(10.5) $$\begin{aligned} \text{Forward Euler:} \quad R(z) &= 1 + z & &\text{stable for } z \in [-2, 0] \\[4pt] \text{RK2 (Heun):} \quad R(z) &= 1 + z + \tfrac{1}{2}z^2 & &\text{stable for } z \in [-2, 0] \\[4pt] \text{RK4:} \quad R(z) &= 1 + z + \tfrac{1}{2}z^2 + \tfrac{1}{6}z^3 + \tfrac{1}{24}z^4 & &\text{stable for } z \in [-2.785, 0] \end{aligned}$$

Figure 9.3 — Stability regions in the complex $z = \lambda\,\Delta t$ plane. The method is stable when $z$ lies inside the boundary. Along the negative real axis (which is what matters for diffusion), RK4 allows $\sim$39% larger timesteps than FE/RK2.

A critical observation: RK2 and Forward Euler have the same stability limit along the real axis ($|z| \leq 2$). So RK2 cannot take larger timesteps than Forward Euler for pure diffusion — its advantage is purely in accuracy per step, not stability. RK4, however, extends the real-axis limit to $|z| \leq 2.785$, allowing about 39% larger steps. In terms of the CFL condition:

(10.6) $$\Delta t_{\text{RK4}} \leq \frac{2.785\; h^2}{2\,n_{\text{dim}}\,d_{\max}} \approx 1.39\;\Delta t_{\text{FE}}$$

10.6   Cost vs. Accuracy: Choosing Your Explicit Method

Each RK method trades cost for accuracy. The question is: for a given error tolerance, which method minimizes total work? The answer depends on whether you are stability-limited (the CFL condition forces small $\Delta t$) or accuracy-limited (you could take larger $\Delta t$ but the error would be too large).

Method $f$ evals/step Order Real-axis stability $\Delta t_{\max}$ (relative) Best when...
Forward Euler 1 1 $[-2, 0]$ 1.0× CFL-limited (fine mesh, ionic $\Delta t$ ≫ CFL)
RK2 (Heun) 2 2 $[-2, 0]$ 1.0× Accuracy matters, not quite CFL-limited
RK4 4 4 $[-2.785, 0]$ 1.39× Smooth solution, accuracy-limited regime

The practical verdict for cardiac simulation: Most cardiac simulations are CFL-limited — the sharp wavefront during depolarization demands small $\Delta t$ regardless of accuracy concerns. In this regime, Forward Euler is usually the best choice: the CFL condition forces a small enough $\Delta t$ that first-order accuracy is adequate, and the cost per step is minimal. RK4 shines in accuracy-limited regimes (coarse mesh, smooth solutions) or when coupled with adaptive timestepping, which Chapter 10 does not cover but is an active research direction.

10.7   When Explicit Methods Shine

Despite the CFL constraint, explicit methods remain competitive in several important scenarios:

The mesh is coarse enough that the CFL limit does not bite. For $h = 0.2{-}0.5$ mm (common in 2D studies), the allowed $\Delta t$ is comparable to or larger than what the ionic solver needs anyway.

GPU parallelism is critical. Explicit methods are embarrassingly parallel — each cell's update is independent. No linear system needs to be solved, no global communication is required. On modern GPUs with thousands of cores, this parallelism translates directly to speed.

Memory is limited. Explicit methods store only the current state and the Laplacian evaluation — no preconditioner, no Krylov vectors, no mass matrix factorization.

In Engine V5.4, all three explicit methods inherit from a DiffusionSolver ABC (solver/diffusion_time_stepping/base.py). Each calls self._spatial.apply_diffusion(V) — the $\mathcal{L}$ operator — which encapsulates FDM, FEM, or FVM (Chapter 7). Swapping between spatial methods is a one-line configuration change; the time integrator only sees the operator $\mathcal{L}$. Explicit solvers need no pre-built matrices; only the implicit solvers (Chapter 10) build DiffusionOperators.

Memory consideration for RK4. Each $\mathbf{k}_i$ is a full-length voltage vector (one float per mesh node). RK4 stores 4 of these simultaneously, plus the original $\mathbf{V}^n$. For a 3D mesh with $10^6$ nodes at 8 bytes/float, that is $5 \times 8$ MB $= 40$ MB — trivial on a CPU, but meaningful on a GPU where shared memory is limited. Forward Euler needs only one extra vector ($2 \times 8$ MB). This is another reason why Forward Euler dominates GPU cardiac codes.
