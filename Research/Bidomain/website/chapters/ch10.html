Chapter 10

Implicit Diffusion Solvers

When meshes are fine or three-dimensional, the CFL constraint makes explicit methods impractical. Implicit methods trade the simplicity of a matrix-free update for unconditional stability — at the cost of solving a linear system at every timestep.

10.1   Why Implicit? The CFL Wall

Explicit methods (Chapter 9) are like steering a boat by looking only at where you are. If the river is calm, this works fine. But in rapids (fine meshes, fast diffusion), the boat oscillates wildly because you are always reacting to where you were, not where you are going. Implicit methods fix this by peeking ahead: they ask "where will I be at the end of this step?" and solve for that future state directly. This costs more per step (you must solve a system of equations), but the payoff is unconditional stability — you can take any step size without blowing up.

The fundamental issue is that the CFL condition (equation 9.2) scales as $h^2$: halve the mesh spacing and the maximum timestep drops by a factor of four. For a 3D mesh with $h = 0.1$ mm ($d_{\max} = 0.174$ mm$^2$/ms), the CFL limit is $\Delta t \leq 0.004$ ms — far below the $0.01{-}0.05$ ms range the ionic solver prefers. Running 5–12 explicit diffusion substeps per ionic step wastes enormous effort. Implicit methods eliminate this constraint entirely: they are unconditionally stable, meaning $\Delta t$ is limited only by accuracy, not by stability. In practice, this lets us match the diffusion timestep to the ionic timestep, halving or more the total simulation time.

The price is that at each timestep, the new voltage $\mathbf{V}^{n+1}$ appears on both sides of the equation — so we must solve a linear system $\mathbf{A}\,\mathbf{V}^{n+1} = \mathbf{b}$ to find it. This is where the machinery of Chapter 7 pays off: the stiffness matrix $\mathbf{K}$ and mass matrix $\mathbf{M}$ from the spatial discretization become the building blocks of $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{b}$.

10.2   Backward Euler (BDF1): The Simplest Implicit Method

If Forward Euler asks "where am I now, and where does the slope say I should go?", Backward Euler asks the reverse: "where do I end up such that the slope there points back to where I started?" This backward-looking logic makes it inherently stable — it cannot overshoot because it is defined by the destination, not the departure.

Formally, Backward Euler evaluates the diffusion operator at the new time level:

(10.1) $$\mathbf{V}^{n+1} = \mathbf{V}^n + \Delta t\;\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{V}^{n+1})$$

Since $\mathbf{V}^{n+1}$ appears on both sides, this is an implicit equation. To solve it, we substitute the semi-discrete diffusion operator from Chapter 7: $\mathbf{M}\,d\mathbf{V}/dt = -\mathbf{K}\,\mathbf{V}$. Replacing $d\mathbf{V}/dt$ with the backward difference $(\mathbf{V}^{n+1} - \mathbf{V}^n)/\Delta t$ and rearranging:

(10.2) $$\boxed{\underbrace{\bigl(\mathbf{M} + \Delta t\,\mathbf{K}\bigr)}_{\mathbf{A}_{\text{lhs}}}\;\mathbf{V}^{n+1} = \underbrace{\mathbf{M}}_{\mathbf{B}_{\text{rhs}}}\;\mathbf{V}^n}$$

This is the exact linear system the code solves. The left-hand side matrix $\mathbf{A}_{\text{lhs}} = \mathbf{M} + \Delta t\,\mathbf{K}$ is symmetric positive definite (SPD) for the standard monodomain problem with Neumann boundary conditions, so the conjugate gradient method converges reliably. The right-hand side is simply $\mathbf{B}_{\text{rhs}} \cdot \mathbf{V}^n$ — a single sparse matrix-vector product.

Backward Euler is first-order accurate ($O(\Delta t)$) and L-stable — meaning it not only stays bounded but actively damps high-frequency modes. This aggressive damping is useful after discontinuous events (stimulus application, wavefront collision) where oscillations would otherwise pollute the solution. It is also the natural first step for multi-step methods like BDF2.

For FDM with lumped mass ($\mathbf{M} = \mathbf{I}$), the system simplifies to $(\mathbf{I} + \Delta t\,\mathbf{K})\,\mathbf{V}^{n+1} = \mathbf{V}^n$.

10.3   Crank–Nicolson: Second-Order Implicit

Backward Euler looks only at the destination; Forward Euler looks only at the start. Crank–Nicolson averages the two — evaluating the diffusion operator at both the old and new time levels and taking the midpoint. This averaging is what gives it second-order accuracy: by sampling both ends of the interval, it captures the curvature that either method alone would miss.

The Crank–Nicolson method (also called the trapezoidal rule) applies the diffusion operator at the half-way point between old and new:

(10.3) $$\mathbf{V}^{n+1} = \mathbf{V}^n + \frac{\Delta t}{2}\bigl[\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{V}^n) + \mathcal{D}(\mathbf{V}^{n+1})\bigr]$$

Substituting $\mathcal{D}(\mathbf{V}) = -\mathbf{M}^{-1}\mathbf{K}\,\mathbf{V}$, multiplying through by $\mathbf{M}$, and collecting terms:

(10.4) $$\boxed{\underbrace{\bigl(\mathbf{M} + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\mathbf{K}\bigr)}_{\mathbf{A}_{\text{lhs}}}\;\mathbf{V}^{n+1} = \underbrace{\bigl(\mathbf{M} - \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\mathbf{K}\bigr)}_{\mathbf{B}_{\text{rhs}}}\;\mathbf{V}^n}$$

Compare with Backward Euler (10.2): the only difference is the $\frac{1}{2}$ on the $\mathbf{K}$ terms and the appearance of $\mathbf{K}$ on the right-hand side. Both sides now involve the stiffness matrix, but the computation is the same: one sparse matrix-vector product for the RHS, then a linear solve with $\mathbf{A}_{\text{lhs}}$.

Crank–Nicolson is second-order accurate ($O(\Delta t^2)$) and A-stable — unconditionally stable, but not L-stable. The practical consequence: at very sharp wavefronts, CN can produce mild 2$\Delta t$ oscillations (the Crank–Nicolson "ringing" artifact). These oscillations do not grow (A-stability) but they can be aesthetically unpleasant and may trigger spurious ionic model activation. For most cardiac simulations with $\Delta t \leq 0.05$ ms, the oscillations are negligible.

Worked example. Consider a 5-node 1D cable (FDM, lumped mass, $\mathbf{M} = \mathbf{I}$) with stiffness matrix $\mathbf{K}$ from Chapter 7 and $\Delta t = 0.02$ ms. The CN matrices are:

$\mathbf{A}_{\text{lhs}} = \mathbf{I} + 0.01\,\mathbf{K}$,   $\mathbf{B}_{\text{rhs}} = \mathbf{I} - 0.01\,\mathbf{K}$

At each timestep: (1) compute $\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{B}_{\text{rhs}} \cdot \mathbf{V}^n$ (sparse matvec, $\sim 5N$ flops), then (2) solve $\mathbf{A}_{\text{lhs}} \cdot \mathbf{V}^{n+1} = \mathbf{b}$ (CG iteration, 10–30 iterations × $\sim 5N$ flops per iteration). Compare with Forward Euler: one matvec per step, but requiring $\Delta t \leq h^2/(2d) \approx 0.003$ ms — meaning CN can take $\sim$7× larger steps for the same total cost per unit simulation time.

Both $\mathbf{A}_{\text{lhs}}$ and $\mathbf{B}_{\text{rhs}}$ are precomputed once at the start of the simulation (or when $\Delta t$ changes) and reused at every timestep. They are sparse matrices with the same sparsity pattern as $\mathbf{K}$. This one-time assembly cost is amortized over thousands of timesteps.

10.4   BDF2: Second-Order with Strong Damping

BDF2 is like Crank–Nicolson's tougher cousin. Both are second-order accurate, but BDF2 achieves this by using two previous time levels — $\mathbf{V}^n$ and $\mathbf{V}^{n-1}$ — rather than averaging the diffusion operator. The payoff is L-stability: BDF2 aggressively damps stiff transients that would cause CN to ring. For the steep cardiac wavefront ($\sim$0.3 mm wide, crossing a grid cell in $\sim$0.01 ms), this damping is usually beneficial.

The BDF2 formula replaces the first-order backward difference with a second-order version using two history levels:

(10.5) $$\frac{3\,\mathbf{V}^{n+1} - 4\,\mathbf{V}^n + \mathbf{V}^{n-1}}{2\,\Delta t} = \mathcal{D}(\mathbf{V}^{n+1})$$

The left side is a second-order backward difference approximation to $d\mathbf{V}/dt$ at time $t^{n+1}$, using the three points $\{t^{n-1}, t^n, t^{n+1}\}$. Multiplying through by $2\,\Delta t\,\mathbf{M}$ and substituting $\mathcal{D} = -\mathbf{M}^{-1}\mathbf{K}$:

(10.6) $$\boxed{\underbrace{\bigl(3\,\mathbf{M} + 2\,\Delta t\,\mathbf{K}\bigr)}_{\mathbf{A}_{\text{lhs}}}\;\mathbf{V}^{n+1} = \underbrace{4\,\mathbf{M}}_{\mathbf{B}_{\text{rhs}}}\;\mathbf{V}^n - \mathbf{M}\,\mathbf{V}^{n-1}}$$

The right-hand side now involves two previous voltage vectors: $4\,\mathbf{M}\,\mathbf{V}^n - \mathbf{M}\,\mathbf{V}^{n-1}$. This is the price of being a two-step method: the solver must store the previous timestep's voltage $\mathbf{V}^{n-1}$ in addition to the current $\mathbf{V}^n$. Since $\mathbf{V}^{n-1}$ is not available at $t = 0$, the first step must use BDF1 (Backward Euler) as a bootstrap — a one-step method that requires only $\mathbf{V}^0$.

BDF2 is A-stable but (despite its strong damping) technically not L-stable — that distinction belongs to BDF1. However, BDF2's damping of high-frequency modes is much stronger than CN's. The stability function $R(z) = (4 - 1/(1 + 2z/3))$ satisfies $|R(\infty)| = 1/3$, compared to $|R(\infty)| = 1$ for CN. In practice, this means BDF2 suppresses the "ringing" at sharp wavefronts that CN can exhibit.

10.5   Comparing the Three Methods

Method Order $\mathbf{A}_{\text{lhs}}$ $\mathbf{B}_{\text{rhs}} \cdot \mathbf{V}^n$ Stability Best when...
BDF1 1 $\mathbf{M} + \Delta t\,\mathbf{K}$ $\mathbf{M}\,\mathbf{V}^n$ L-stable First step; after discontinuities
CN 2 $\mathbf{M} + \frac{\Delta t}{2}\mathbf{K}$ $(\mathbf{M} - \frac{\Delta t}{2}\mathbf{K})\,\mathbf{V}^n$ A-stable Default choice; smooth solutions
BDF2 2 $3\mathbf{M} + 2\Delta t\,\mathbf{K}$ $4\mathbf{M}\,\mathbf{V}^n - \mathbf{M}\,\mathbf{V}^{n-1}$ A-stable, strongly damping Sharp wavefronts; large $\Delta t$

All three methods share the same computational skeleton: precompute $\mathbf{A}_{\text{lhs}}$ and $\mathbf{B}_{\text{rhs}}$ once, then at each timestep perform one sparse matrix-vector product (the RHS) followed by one linear solve (the LHS). The linear solve dominates the cost — typically 60–80% of wall-clock time in an implicit monodomain simulation.

10.6   Linear Solvers: PCG, Chebyshev, and FFT

The linear system $\mathbf{A}_{\text{lhs}}\,\mathbf{V}^{n+1} = \mathbf{b}$ must be solved efficiently at every timestep. Since $\mathbf{A}_{\text{lhs}}$ is SPD (for Neumann boundary conditions), the conjugate gradient method is the natural choice. Engine V5.4 offers three solver strategies, each suited to different hardware and problem configurations:

PCG (Preconditioned Conjugate Gradient) with Jacobi preconditioning is the default. It is robust, well-understood, and converges in $O(\sqrt{\kappa})$ iterations, where $\kappa$ is the condition number of the system. For the monodomain problem, $\kappa \propto h^{-2}$, so finer meshes require more iterations — but typically 10–30 suffice for the tolerance levels needed in cardiac simulation.

Chebyshev iteration is a fixed-polynomial method that requires no inner products — making it ideal for GPU computation, where global reductions (inner products) are expensive. It requires an estimate of the eigenvalue bounds of $\mathbf{A}_{\text{lhs}}$, but these can be precomputed once. The Chebyshev solver achieves zero-synchronization operation: every thread computes independently for a fixed number of iterations.

FFT/DCT solver applies when the domain is a regular rectangle with uniform spacing. The discrete Laplacian diagonalizes under a cosine transform (for Neumann BC), giving a direct solve: transform → divide by eigenvalues → inverse transform. This is $O(N \log N)$ and exact to machine precision — no iterations needed. It is the fastest option when applicable, but limited to rectangular domains with constant conductivity.

The choice of linear solver often dominates the total simulation cost. In a typical implicit monodomain simulation, 60–80% of wall-clock time is spent in the linear solver. This is why the Chebyshev (GPU-friendly) and FFT (direct) solvers exist in V5.4 alongside PCG — each targets a different performance bottleneck.

In Engine V5.4, the implicit solvers (CrankNicolsonSolver, BDF1Solver, BDF2Solver) each inherit from DiffusionSolver and additionally own a LinearSolver. At construction, each calls spatial.get_diffusion_operators(dt, scheme) with scheme = "CN", "BDF1", or "BDF2" — this returns a DiffusionOperators object containing the precomputed sparse matrices $\mathbf{A}_{\text{lhs}}$ and $\mathbf{B}_{\text{rhs}}$. The step() method is then identical for all three: compute rhs = B_rhs @ V, solve A_lhs @ V_new = rhs. BDF2 additionally stores V_prev and subtracts M @ V_prev from the RHS, with an automatic fallback to BDF1 for the first timestep.
