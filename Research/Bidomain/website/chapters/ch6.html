Chapter 6

The O'Hara–Rudy 2011 Model

The O'Hara–Rudy dynamic (ORd) model was built from an unprecedented dataset of undiseased human ventricular myocyte recordings. With 41 state variables, 15 membrane currents, and a CaMKII signaling pathway that confers rate adaptation, it represents the current gold standard for human ventricular electrophysiology simulation. This chapter provides the same comprehensive treatment given to TTP06 in Chapter 5 — every current, every equation, organized by physiological function.

6.1   41 State Variables: What and Why

ORd has more than twice the state variables of TTP06 (41 vs. 18). The additional complexity arises from three sources: (1) separate fast and slow inactivation gates for most currents, (2) CaMKII-phosphorylated variants of several gates, and (3) two additional ionic compartments (subspace sodium and potassium). Every state variable has a clear biophysical justification.

A key design principle of ORd is the CaMKII-phosphorylated population concept. For currents modulated by CaMKII ($I_{\text{Na}}$, $I_{\text{NaL}}$, $I_{\text{CaL}}$, $I_{\text{to}}$), the model maintains separate gating variables for the phosphorylated channel population. The total current is then a weighted average: $(1 - \Phi)\cdot I_{\text{normal}} + \Phi\cdot I_{\text{phosphorylated}}$, where $\Phi$ is the CaMKII-dependent phosphorylation fraction. This doubles the gate count for affected currents but allows rate-dependent modulation of channel kinetics.

The most important structural differences from TTP06 are the addition of subspace Na$^+$ and K$^+$ compartments (reflecting the fact that these ions also have local concentrations near the membrane), the split of the SR into network and junctional compartments (SERCA pumps into the nSR; RyR releases from the jSR), and the CaMKII trapped state that provides long-term "memory" of recent heart rate.

Why does ORd track subspace Na$^+$ and K$^+$? In TTP06, only Ca$^{2+}$ had a subspace compartment because its concentration can change dramatically during a single beat ($\times$10–100 locally). Na$^+$ and K$^+$ concentrations change by much less ($\lt$1%), so a single bulk compartment seemed sufficient. But ORd found that even small Na$^+$ gradients near the membrane affect NCX and Na$^+$/K$^+$-ATPase activity — the subspace compartments improve accuracy when these transporters are the focus.

6.2   The Complete Current Inventory

ORd models 15 distinct membrane currents. Every current contributes to the total ionic current:

(6.1) $$\begin{aligned} I_{\text{ion}} \;=\; & \underbrace{I_{\text{Na}} + I_{\text{NaL}} + I_{\text{CaL}}}_{\text{depolarizing}} \;+\; \underbrace{I_{\text{to}} + I_{\text{Kr}} + I_{\text{Ks}} + I_{\text{K1}} + I_{\text{Kb}}}_{\text{repolarizing}} \\[6pt] & +\; \underbrace{I_{\text{NaK}} + I_{\text{NaCa,i}} + I_{\text{NaCa,ss}} + I_{\text{pCa}}}_{\text{pumps/exchangers}} \;+\; \underbrace{I_{\text{Nab}} + I_{\text{Cab}}}_{\text{background}} \;+\; \underbrace{I_{\text{ClCa}} + I_{\text{Clb}}}_{\text{chloride}} \end{aligned}$$

Compared to TTP06's 12 currents, ORd adds $I_{\text{NaL}}$ (late sodium), splits $I_{\text{NaCa}}$ into two populations, adds chloride currents ($I_{\text{ClCa}}$, $I_{\text{Clb}}$), and replaces the plateau potassium current $I_{\text{pK}}$ with a background potassium current $I_{\text{Kb}}$. The L-type calcium current is further decomposed into three ionic components ($I_{\text{CaL,Ca}}$, $I_{\text{CaL,Na}}$, $I_{\text{CaL,K}}$). The table below provides the complete reference.

6.3   Voltage-Gated Depolarizing Currents

Fast Sodium Current ($I_{\text{Na}}$) — Phase 0

The fast sodium current retains the classical HH structure from TTP06, but with two key upgrades: refitted kinetics from undiseased human data, and CaMKII phosphorylation of the slow inactivation gate. The total current blends non-phosphorylated and phosphorylated channel populations:

(6.2) $$I_{\text{Na}} = \bar{g}_{\text{Na}}\,m^3\,\bigl[(1 - \Phi_{\text{Na}})\,h\,j + \Phi_{\text{Na}}\,h_p\,j_p\bigr]\,(V_m - E_{\text{Na}})$$

Here $\Phi_{\text{Na}}$ is the CaMKII phosphorylation fraction for the sodium channel, and the composite inactivation gate $h$ is a weighted sum of fast and slow components: $h = A_{hf}\,h_f + (1 - A_{hf})\,h_s$, with the weight $A_{hf}$ depending on voltage (fast inactivation dominates at depolarized potentials). The phosphorylated population uses $h_p = A_{hf}\,h_f + (1 - A_{hf})\,h_{sp}$, where $h_{sp}$ has a shifted voltage dependence — CaMKII phosphorylation slows recovery from inactivation, contributing to rate-dependent sodium channel availability. The maximal conductance $\bar{g}_{\text{Na}} = 75$ mS/$\mu$F is substantially larger than TTP06's 14.838 nS/pF (note different units), reflecting the refitting to human data.

Late Sodium Current ($I_{\text{NaL}}$) — Phase 2

The late sodium current is the most important addition in ORd relative to TTP06. It represents a small but persistent Na$^+$ influx that flows throughout the plateau — generated by sodium channels that briefly reopen or fail to fully inactivate:

(6.3) $$I_{\text{NaL}} = \bar{g}_{\text{NaL}}\,m_L\,\bigl[(1 - \Phi_{\text{NaL}})\,h_L + \Phi_{\text{NaL}}\,h_{Lp}\bigr]\,(V_m - E_{\text{Na}})$$

The maximal conductance $\bar{g}_{\text{NaL}} = 0.0075$ mS/$\mu$F is roughly 1/10,000th of peak $I_{\text{Na}}$, but because $I_{\text{NaL}}$ flows for 200+ ms (vs. $\lt$2 ms for peak $I_{\text{Na}}$), it carries significant charge and contributes approximately 5% of APD. The activation gate $m_L$ is similar to the fast sodium $m$ gate but with slightly slower kinetics. The inactivation gate $h_L$ has a time constant of $\sim$200 ms — orders of magnitude slower than the fast sodium $h$ gate — explaining why this current persists through the plateau.

CaMKII phosphorylation of $I_{\text{NaL}}$ ($h_{Lp}$) shifts the inactivation, increasing the late current at faster heart rates. This creates a positive feedback loop: faster rate → more Ca$^{2+}$ entry → more CaMKII activation → more $I_{\text{NaL}}$ → more Na$^+$ entry → potentially dangerous if unchecked. Clinically, this pathway is relevant to arrhythmia in heart failure.

$I_{\text{NaL}}$ is the primary target of ranolazine, an antianginal drug that selectively inhibits late sodium current without significantly affecting peak $I_{\text{Na}}$. This specificity is impossible to study computationally without explicitly modeling $I_{\text{NaL}}$ — one of the main reasons TTP06 is inadequate for drug safety assessment. In Long QT syndrome type 3 (LQT3), gain-of-function mutations in SCN5A increase $I_{\text{NaL}}$ by 2–5 fold, dangerously prolonging the plateau. ORd can reproduce this pathology by simply scaling $\bar{g}_{\text{NaL}}$.

L-Type Calcium Current ($I_{\text{CaL}}$) — Phase 2

The L-type calcium current in ORd is substantially more complex than in TTP06. It is decomposed into three ionic components, reflecting the imperfect selectivity of the Cav1.2 channel:

(6.4) $$I_{\text{CaL}} = I_{\text{CaL,Ca}} + I_{\text{CaL,Na}} + I_{\text{CaL,K}}$$

Each component uses the GHK flux equation with its respective permeability. The calcium component (by far the largest) is:

(6.5) $$I_{\text{CaL,Ca}} = P_{\text{Ca}}\,d\,\bigl[(1 - \Phi_{\text{CaL}})\,f\,j_{\text{Ca}}\,f_{\text{Ca}} + \Phi_{\text{CaL}}\,f_p\,j_{\text{Ca}}\,f_{\text{Ca},p}\bigr] \cdot \Psi_{\text{Ca}}(V_m, [\text{Ca}^{2+}]_{\text{ss}})$$

where $\Psi_{\text{Ca}}$ is the GHK flux: $\Psi_{\text{Ca}} = 4\tfrac{F^2}{RT}\,V_m\,\tfrac{[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_{\text{ss}}\,e^{2FV_m/RT} - 0.341\,[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_o}{e^{2FV_m/RT}-1}$, and the composite gates are: $f = A_{ff}\,f_f + (1 - A_{ff})\,f_s$ (voltage inactivation, blending fast and slow components) and $f_{\text{Ca}} = A_{\text{Ca},f}\,f_{\text{Ca},f} + (1 - A_{\text{Ca},f})\,f_{\text{Ca},s}$ (calcium-dependent inactivation).

The CaMKII-phosphorylated population ($\Phi_{\text{CaL}}$) uses separate gates $f_p$ and $f_{\text{Ca},p}$ with shifted kinetics — phosphorylation facilitates channel reopening and slows CDI, increasing calcium influx at faster rates. This CaMKII-mediated enhancement of $I_{\text{CaL}}$ is counterbalanced by increased SERCA activity (also CaMKII-dependent), maintaining calcium homeostasis while strengthening contraction — a key component of the positive force-frequency relationship.

The Na$^+$ and K$^+$ components follow the same gating but use sodium and potassium permeabilities ($P_{\text{Na}} = 0.00125\,P_{\text{Ca}}$ and $P_{\text{K}} = 3.574\times10^{-4}\,P_{\text{Ca}}$) and their respective GHK fluxes. Though individually small, these components contribute to the ion balance over many beats.

Why split $I_{\text{CaL}}$ into three components? In TTP06, only Ca$^{2+}$ permeation was modeled because Ca$^{2+}$ is the dominant charge carrier. But when tracking Na$^+$ and K$^+$ concentrations dynamically over thousands of beats (as ORd does with its subspace compartments), even the small Na$^+$ and K$^+$ fluxes through L-type channels contribute to concentration drift. Ignoring them causes slow, insidious accumulation errors that manifest as AP duration changes after hundreds of beats — exactly the kind of subtle bug that plagues long-running simulations.

6.4   Voltage-Gated Repolarizing Currents

Transient Outward Current ($I_{\text{to}}$) — Phase 1

The transient outward current in ORd has separate fast and slow inactivation gates (replacing TTP06's single $s$ gate), plus CaMKII-phosphorylated variants:

(6.6) $$I_{\text{to}} = \bar{g}_{\text{to}}\,\bigl[(1 - \Phi_{\text{to}})\,a\,i + \Phi_{\text{to}}\,a_p\,i_p\bigr]\,(V_m - E_{\text{K}})$$

where $i = A_{iF}\,i_F + (1 - A_{iF})\,i_S$ blends fast and slow inactivation, with the weight $A_{iF}$ depending on voltage. The CaMKII-phosphorylated inactivation $i_p = A_{iF}\,i_{Fp} + (1-A_{iF})\,i_{Sp}$ has shifted kinetics. The maximal conductance $\bar{g}_{\text{to}}$ retains the dramatic transmural gradient: 0.02 mS/$\mu$F in endocardium vs. 0.08 mS/$\mu$F in epicardium and M-cells.

Rapid Delayed Rectifier ($I_{\text{Kr}}$) — Phase 3

$I_{\text{Kr}}$ in ORd uses refitted kinetics from undiseased human hERG data. Like TTP06, it has two activation components, but ORd blends fast and slow activation:

(6.7) $$I_{\text{Kr}} = \bar{g}_{\text{Kr}}\,\sqrt{[\text{K}^+]_o / 5.4}\,x_r\,r_{Kr}(V_m)\,(V_m - E_{\text{K}})$$

Here $x_r = A_{xr,f}\,x_{r,f} + (1 - A_{xr,f})\,x_{r,s}$ is a weighted blend of fast and slow activation, and $r_{Kr}(V_m)$ is an instantaneous rectification factor that captures the voltage-dependent inactivation of hERG channels. The $\sqrt{[\text{K}^+]_o/5.4}$ factor preserves the extracellular K$^+$ dependence from TTP06. Notably, $I_{\text{Kr}}$ is not CaMKII-modulated in ORd — its kinetics are purely voltage-dependent. This is the key current for the CiPA (Comprehensive in vitro Proarrhythmia Assay) initiative, which uses ORd as its reference model for predicting drug-induced arrhythmia risk.

Slow Delayed Rectifier ($I_{\text{Ks}}$) — Repolarization Reserve

(6.8) $$I_{\text{Ks}} = \bar{g}_{\text{Ks}}\,K_{\text{s,Ca}}\,x_{s1}\,x_{s2}\,(V_m - E_{\text{Ks}})$$

A key difference from TTP06 is the calcium-sensitivity factor $K_{\text{s,Ca}} = 1 + 0.6\bigl/\bigl(1 + (3.8\times10^{-5}/[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_i)^{1.4}\bigr)$, which increases $I_{\text{Ks}}$ when intracellular calcium is elevated. At resting calcium ($\sim$100 nM), $K_{\text{s,Ca}} \approx 1$ and the current is near baseline. During systolic calcium peaks ($\sim$1 $\mu$M), $K_{\text{s,Ca}}$ increases to $\sim$1.6, boosting repolarization reserve precisely when the cell needs it most. Combined with CaMKII modulation of $\bar{g}_{\text{Ks}}$ at faster heart rates, this creates robust rate-dependent enhancement of repolarization reserve. The reversal potential $E_{\text{Ks}}$ remains a mixed Na$^+$/K$^+$ potential, computed from the Goldman equation. The maximal conductance shows strong transmural variation: $\bar{g}_{\text{Ks}} = 0.0034$ mS/$\mu$F (baseline) in endocardial cells, scaled $1.4\times$ in epicardial cells, with reduced values in M-cells explaining the prolonged M-cell APD and the T-wave morphology on the ECG.

Inward Rectifier ($I_{\text{K1}}$) — Phase 4 and Late Phase 3

(6.9) $$I_{\text{K1}} = \bar{g}_{\text{K1}}\,\sqrt{[\text{K}^+]_o}\,x_{K1}\,r_{K1}(V_m)\,(V_m - E_{\text{K}})$$

Unlike TTP06 — where $I_{\text{K1}}$ used only an instantaneous steady-state function — ORd gives $I_{\text{K1}}$ a dynamic gating variable $x_{K1}$ with its own time constant (though still fast, $\tau \sim 1{-}10$ ms). The instantaneous rectification factor $r_{K1}(V_m)$ captures the polyamine block at depolarized voltages. This formulation better reproduces the terminal repolarization phase and the resting membrane potential measured in human ventricular myocytes.

Background Potassium Current ($I_{\text{Kb}}$)

(6.10) $$I_{\text{Kb}} = \bar{g}_{\text{Kb}}\,x_{Kb}(V_m)\,(V_m - E_{\text{K}})$$

$I_{\text{Kb}}$ replaces TTP06's $I_{\text{pK}}$. The gating is instantaneous (a sigmoidal function of voltage). Like its predecessor, it provides a small outward current during the plateau.

6.5   Pumps, Exchangers, and Background Currents

Na$^+$/K$^+$ ATPase ($I_{\text{NaK}}$) — Thermodynamic Cycle Model

The Na$^+$/K$^+$-ATPase in ORd represents the most significant single-equation change from TTP06. Instead of the Hill-type empirical formulation, ORd uses a thermodynamically constrained cycle model that explicitly accounts for the pump's conformational states:

(6.11) $$I_{\text{NaK}} = 30\,\frac{z_{\text{Na}} \cdot a_1 \cdot k_{1p} - z_{\text{Na}} \cdot b_1 \cdot k_{1m}}{1 + \frac{a_1\,k_{1p}}{b_2\,k_{2m}} + \frac{b_1\,k_{1m}}{a_2\,k_{2p}} + \frac{a_2\,k_{2p}}{b_3\,k_{3m}} + \frac{b_2\,k_{2m}}{a_3\,k_{3p}} + \frac{a_3\,k_{3p}}{b_4\,k_{4m}} + \frac{b_3\,k_{3m}}{a_4\,k_{4p}}}$$

The rate constants $a_i$, $b_i$, $k_{ip}$, $k_{im}$ depend on $[\text{Na}^+]_i$, $[\text{K}^+]_o$, ATP, and $V_m$ through microscopic detailed balance. This formulation satisfies the second law of thermodynamics — the pump cannot generate flux against its electrochemical gradient without ATP hydrolysis, a constraint that the simpler TTP06 formulation does not enforce. The practical consequence is more accurate pump behavior under extreme conditions (ischemia, low ATP, hyperkalemia) where the driving forces are close to equilibrium.

Na$^+$/Ca$^{2+}$ Exchanger — Split Populations

ORd explicitly models two NCX populations operating in different calcium environments:

(6.12) $$I_{\text{NaCa}} = I_{\text{NaCa,i}} + I_{\text{NaCa,ss}}$$

where $I_{\text{NaCa,i}}$ (80% of NCX) senses bulk cytoplasmic $[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_i$ and $[\text{Na}^+]_i$, while $I_{\text{NaCa,ss}}$ (20% of NCX) senses subspace $[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_{\text{ss}}$ and $[\text{Na}^+]_{\text{ss}}$. Each uses a thermodynamic formulation:

(6.13) $$I_{\text{NaCa,x}} = 0.8\,\bar{g}_{\text{NaCa}}\cdot\frac{k_{3p}\,k_{4pp}\,[\text{Na}^+]_x^3 - k_{3pp}\,k_{4p}\,[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_x}{(1 + Q_{10})(h_1 \cdot h_2 + h_3)(1/h_7 + h_8)}$$

The split is physiologically motivated: NCX molecules near the dyadic cleft "see" a very different calcium signal (high, fast transient from CICR) than those in the bulk cytoplasm (lower, slower rise). In TTP06's single-population NCX, forward-mode extrusion is driven by a spatially averaged $[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_i$ that underestimates the local signal near the release sites. The subspace NCX population responds to the actual local calcium in the dyad, providing more accurate calcium cycling and a better match to experimental measurements of NCX current during the action potential.

Sarcolemmal Ca$^{2+}$ Pump ($I_{\text{pCa}}$)

(6.14) $$I_{\text{pCa}} = \frac{\bar{g}_{\text{pCa}}\,[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_i}{K_{m,\text{pCa}} + [\text{Ca}^{2+}]_i}$$

The PMCA formulation is essentially unchanged from TTP06: a Michaelis–Menten-style saturating function of cytoplasmic calcium. It contributes roughly 1–2% of total calcium extrusion per beat.

Background Leak Currents ($I_{\text{Nab}}$, $I_{\text{Cab}}$)

(6.15) $$I_{\text{Nab}} = \bar{g}_{\text{Nab}}\,(V_m - E_{\text{Na}})$$
(6.16) $$I_{\text{Cab}} = P_{\text{Cab}}\cdot\Psi_{\text{Ca}}(V_m, [\text{Ca}^{2+}]_i)$$

The sodium background current retains TTP06's Ohmic formulation. However, the calcium background current in ORd switches from an Ohmic to a GHK formulation ($\Psi_{\text{Ca}}$), providing more physically correct behavior at the very low intracellular Ca$^{2+}$ concentrations where the Ohmic approximation breaks down.

Chloride Currents ($I_{\text{ClCa}}$, $I_{\text{Clb}}$) — New in ORd

(6.17) $$I_{\text{ClCa}} = \bar{g}_{\text{ClCa}}\,\frac{[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_{\text{ss}}}{K_{d,\text{ClCa}} + [\text{Ca}^{2+}]_{\text{ss}}}\,(V_m - E_{\text{Cl}})$$
(6.18) $$I_{\text{Clb}} = \bar{g}_{\text{Clb}}\,(V_m - E_{\text{Cl}})$$

ORd introduces chloride currents, absent from TTP06. The calcium-activated chloride current $I_{\text{ClCa}}$ is gated by subspace calcium and contributes to the Phase 1 notch alongside $I_{\text{to}}$. With $E_{\text{Cl}} \approx -40$ mV (between rest and plateau), this current is depolarizing at rest and repolarizing during the plateau, adding nuance to the Phase 1–2 transition. The background chloride leak $I_{\text{Clb}}$ is small but ensures chloride balance.

Engine V5.4's ORd implementation omits the chloride currents ($I_{\text{ClCa}}$ and $I_{\text{Clb}}$). Their combined contribution is typically $\lt$1% of total ionic current, and many benchmark ORd implementations (including the CiPA reference) also exclude them. If chloride sensitivity is needed for a specific study, these currents can be added as post-hoc corrections without modifying the core state variable system.

6.6   The CaMKII Signaling Pathway

The calcium/calmodulin-dependent protein kinase II (CaMKII) signaling pathway is the largest conceptual addition in ORd and the primary mechanism underlying rate adaptation. CaMKII is activated by calcium/calmodulin binding and can autophosphorylate to maintain activity even after calcium levels fall — creating a molecular "memory" of recent heart rate.

The active CaMKII fraction in ORd is:

(6.19) $$\text{CaMK}_{\text{active}} = \text{CaMK}_{\text{bound}} + \text{CaMK}_{\text{trap}}$$

where $\text{CaMK}_{\text{bound}} = \text{CaMK}_0\,(1 - \text{CaMK}_{\text{trap}})/(1 + K_m/[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_{\text{ss}})$ is the fraction bound to calcium/calmodulin (instantaneous, set by current subspace Ca$^{2+}$), and $\text{CaMK}_{\text{trap}}$ is the autophosphorylated fraction that decays slowly:

(6.20) $$\frac{d\,\text{CaMK}_{\text{trap}}}{dt} = \alpha_{\text{CaMK}}\,\text{CaMK}_{\text{bound}}\,\text{CaMK}_{\text{active}} - \beta_{\text{CaMK}}\,\text{CaMK}_{\text{trap}}$$

The time constant for CaMKII trapping is on the order of seconds — much slower than any gating variable. This creates a hierarchical timescale structure:

Milliseconds: Gate kinetics (m, h, d, f, etc.) — respond within a single AP. Hundreds of milliseconds: Concentration changes ($[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_i$, $[\text{Na}^+]_i$) — settle over a few beats. Seconds to minutes: CaMKII trapping — requires dozens to hundreds of beats to equilibrate.

Active CaMKII phosphorylates four main targets in ORd:

TargetEffect of CaMKII PhosphorylationConsequence for AP
$I_{\text{CaL}}$Slowed voltage inactivation; enhanced recoveryIncreased Ca$^{2+}$ entry → stronger contraction
$I_{\text{NaL}}$Slowed inactivation → increased late Na$^+$Slight APD prolongation at fast rates
RyR / $J_{\text{rel}}$Increased open probability and release fractionLarger Ca$^{2+}$ transient; potential for diastolic leak
SERCA / $J_{\text{up}}$Faster Ca$^{2+}$ reuptake into SRFaster relaxation; maintains SR load at fast rates

The net effect of CaMKII at faster heart rates is: more calcium entry per beat (via enhanced $I_{\text{CaL}}$), faster calcium reuptake (via enhanced SERCA), larger SR release (via enhanced RyR sensitivity), and slightly increased late sodium influx. The enhanced SERCA activity dominates the APD effect — by removing calcium from the cytoplasm faster, it reduces the duration of the calcium transient and the plateau currents that depend on it, resulting in APD shortening. This is how ORd achieves rate adaptation.

CaMKII makes ORd simulations significantly more expensive than TTP06. With 41 state variables (vs. 18), the per-cell cost roughly doubles. But the bigger issue is the slow CaMKII equilibration time: reaching steady state after a rate change requires 300–600 beats (2–5 minutes of simulated time), compared to 50–100 beats for TTP06. For tissue-scale studies where CaMKII effects are not the primary focus, TTP06 remains the pragmatic choice.

The M-cell phenotype in ORd arises from the combination of reduced $I_{\text{Ks}}$ (less repolarization reserve), reduced $I_{\text{Kr}}$ ($0.8\times$), enhanced $I_{\text{CaL}}$ ($2.5\times$), and reduced $I_{\text{NaK}}$ ($0.7\times$). These changes collectively produce the longest APD of any cell type — a feature that is important for generating the T-wave on the surface ECG. The transmural APD gradient (endo $\lt$ epi $\lt$ M-cell) drives the voltage gradient that produces the terminal T-wave inscription.

In Engine V5.4, both TTP06 and ORd implement the same IonicModel interface. The cell_type parameter selects transmural variant by switching maximal conductances. A simulation can mix cell types spatially — endocardial cells on the inner surface, epicardial on the outer surface, M-cells in between — reproducing the full transmural gradient with a single model class.

6.9   Calcium Handling: Two SR Compartments

ORd refines the calcium subsystem beyond TTP06's three-compartment model (subspace, cytoplasm, SR) by splitting the SR into two functionally distinct regions:

Network SR (nSR): The bulk of the SR volume. SERCA pumps calcium from the cytoplasm into the nSR. Calcium diffuses freely within the nSR toward the junctional terminals.

Junctional SR (jSR): The terminal cisternae directly apposed to the T-tubule membrane, containing the RyR2 clusters. This compartment empties rapidly during CICR and must refill from the nSR between beats.

The calcium fluxes in ORd are:

$J_{\text{up}}$ (SERCA2a, cytoplasm → nSR): CaMKII-enhanced pump with phospholamban relief. At faster rates, CaMKII increases the maximum pump rate, enabling faster reuptake.

$J_{\text{tr}}$ (nSR → jSR diffusion): A simple first-order transfer with time constant $\tau_{\text{tr}} = 60$ ms. This creates a delay between SERCA refilling and jSR recovery — the jSR content lags behind the nSR, explaining why premature beats (before full jSR recovery) produce weaker calcium transients.

$J_{\text{rel}}$ (RyR, jSR → subspace): CaMKII-enhanced release. The release flux is proportional to the jSR-subspace calcium gradient and is activated by subspace calcium (CICR trigger). CaMKII phosphorylation increases the RyR open probability, producing larger releases at faster rates. The ORd release model blends non-phosphorylated and CaMKII-phosphorylated populations, with the total release $J_{\text{rel}} = (1 - \Phi)\,J_{\text{rel,NP}} + \Phi\,J_{\text{rel,CaMK}}$.

$J_{\text{xfer}}$ (subspace → cytoplasm diffusion): First-order transfer with time constant $\tau_{\text{xfer}} = 8$ ms. Combined with the subspace Na$^+$ and K$^+$ diffusion, this maintains the subspace as a distinct microenvironment that equilibrates with the bulk cytoplasm on a timescale of tens of milliseconds.

$J_{\text{leak}}$ (nSR → cytoplasm): A small passive leak proportional to $[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_{\text{nsr}}$. This leak is the starting point for modeling diastolic SR calcium leak in heart failure — by increasing the leak rate, one can simulate the RyR2 dysfunction that drives spontaneous calcium waves and DADs.

Switching from TTP06 to ORd requires changing a single configuration parameter — the spatial solver, mesh, operator splitting strategy, and output pipeline remain identical.
