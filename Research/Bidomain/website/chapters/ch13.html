Chapter 13

Spatial Discretization

Chapter 8 developed the spatial discretization toolkit — FDM, FEM, FVM — for a single diffusion operator. Here we apply those same methods to the bidomain system, where two coupled PDEs produce a fundamentally different algebraic structure: a 2×2 block matrix system with $2N$ unknowns.

13.1   From One Stiffness Matrix to Two

In the monodomain model (Chapter 7), spatial discretization produces a single stiffness matrix $\mathbf{K}$ assembled from the effective conductivity tensor $\mathbf{D}$. The bidomain model has two conducting domains — intracellular and extracellular — each with its own conductivity tensor. Applying the same discretization machinery (whether FDM, FEM, or FVM) to each domain separately produces two stiffness matrices:

Intracellular stiffness $\mathbf{K}_i$: assembled from $\mathbf{D}_i$ using exactly the same procedure as the monodomain $\mathbf{K}$, with entries $(K_i)_{jk} = \int_\Omega \nabla\varphi_j \cdot (\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\varphi_k)\,dx$ for FEM, or the corresponding stencil matrix $\mathbf{L}_i$ for FDM.

Extracellular stiffness $\mathbf{K}_e$: assembled identically but with $\mathbf{D}_e$. The combined stiffness $\mathbf{K}_i + \mathbf{K}_e$ corresponds to the bulk conductivity tensor $\mathbf{D}_i + \mathbf{D}_e$.

Engine V5.4's spatial discretization infrastructure already assembles a single stiffness matrix $\mathbf{K}$ (or Laplacian $\mathbf{L}$) from a conductivity tensor field. Extending to bidomain requires calling this same assembly routine twice — once with $\mathbf{D}_i$ and once with $\mathbf{D}_e$ — then combining the results into the 2×2 block format described below.

13.2   The Bidomain Weak Form

For FEM specifically, the bidomain weak form involves both unknowns ($V_m$ and $\phi_e$) simultaneously. Multiplying the parabolic equation by test function $v \in H^1(\Omega)$ and integrating by parts:

$$\begin{aligned} \int_\Omega \chi C_m \frac{\partial V_m}{\partial t}\,v\,dx &+ \int_\Omega \nabla V_m \cdot (\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla v)\,dx + \int_\Omega \nabla\phi_e\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla v)\,dx \\[4pt] &= -\int_\Omega \chi\,I_{\text{ion}}\,v\,dx + \int_\Omega I_{\text{stim}}^i\,v\,dx \end{aligned}$$

For the elliptic equation, multiply by $q \in H^1(\Omega)$:

$$\begin{aligned} \int_\Omega \nabla\phi_e \cdot \bigl[(\mathbf{D}_i + \mathbf{D}_e)\,\nabla q\bigr]\,dx &+ \int_\Omega \nabla V_m \cdot (\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla q)\,dx \\[4pt] &= -\int_\Omega I_{\text{stim}}^e\,q\,dx \end{aligned}$$

Notice the critical structural feature: the intracellular conductivity $\mathbf{D}_i$ appears in both equations — it is the coupling agent between $V_m$ and $\phi_e$. The extracellular conductivity $\mathbf{D}_e$ appears only in the elliptic equation. Expanding in the finite element basis produces the semi-discrete system:

(13.1) $$\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{M} & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0}\end{pmatrix}\!\begin{pmatrix}\dot{\mathbf{V}}_m \\ \dot{\boldsymbol{\phi}}_e\end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{K}_i & \mathbf{K}_i \\ \mathbf{K}_i & \mathbf{K}_i\!+\!\mathbf{K}_e\end{pmatrix}\!\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{V}_m \\ \boldsymbol{\phi}_e\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{F}_V \\ \mathbf{F}_\phi\end{pmatrix}$$

Notice the elegant structure: $\mathbf{K}_i$ appears in every block of the 2×2 stiffness matrix — it is the "glue" coupling $V_m$ and $\phi_e$. The extracellular stiffness $\mathbf{K}_e$ appears only in the (2,2) block, strengthening the elliptic equation. The mass matrix $\mathbf{M}$ (from Chapter 7) appears only in the (1,1) position because only $V_m$ has a time derivative.

13.3   The 2×2 Block System

Regardless of the spatial discretization method (FDM, FEM, or FVM — all described in Chapter 7), the final algebraic system at each time step has the same form:

(13.2) $$\underbrace{\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{A}_{11} & \mathbf{A}_{12} \\ \mathbf{A}_{21} & \mathbf{A}_{22}\end{pmatrix}}_{\mathbf{A}}\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{V}_m \\ \boldsymbol{\phi}_e\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{b}_1 \\ \mathbf{b}_2\end{pmatrix}$$

With the typical identification (after applying a semi-implicit time stepper like Crank–Nicolson):

BlockDefinitionProperties
$\mathbf{A}_{11}$$\mathbf{M}/\Delta t + \mathbf{K}_i$Symmetric positive definite (SPD)
$\mathbf{A}_{12}$$\mathbf{K}_i$Coupling: intracellular stiffness
$\mathbf{A}_{21}$$\mathbf{K}_i$$= \mathbf{A}_{12}^T$ (symmetric)
$\mathbf{A}_{22}$$\mathbf{K}_i + \mathbf{K}_e$SPD (combined stiffness)

The total system has $2N$ unknowns (where $N$ is the number of spatial nodes) — double the monodomain system's $N$ unknowns. This is the fundamental object that all subsequent solver strategies (Chapter 16) must handle efficiently. The system is symmetric but indefinite — a saddle-point problem that requires specialized linear algebra. This is a qualitative departure from the monodomain, where the implicit system is SPD and solvable by conjugate gradient (Chapter 10).

13.4   Boundary Conditions: Formulation and Implementation

In the monodomain (Section 8.7), we needed one boundary condition for one unknown ($V_m$). The bidomain model has two unknowns — the intracellular potential $\phi_i$ and the extracellular potential $\phi_e$ — so we need two boundary conditions at every point on the tissue surface. This doubles the number of choices and creates new physical situations that have no monodomain analogue.

To build intuition, think of a piece of cardiac tissue sitting in a glass dish. The intracellular space (inside the cells) is sealed at the tissue surface — cell membranes continue right up to the edge, and no intracellular current can escape. But the extracellular space (between the cells) is open — it may be in contact with a conductive bath (saline solution), or it may be exposed to air. What happens at the boundary depends on what is outside.

13.4.1   Two Domains, Two Sets of Boundary Conditions

There are three canonical scenarios, each with a different physical story and a different mathematical consequence:

(a) Isolated tissue (Neumann on both). The tissue sits in a non-conductive medium (air, or a poorly conducting pericardial sac). No current can leave through either the intracellular or extracellular space:

(13.3) $$\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\phi_i \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}} = 0 \quad\text{and}\quad \mathbf{D}_e\,\nabla\phi_e \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}} = 0 \quad\text{on } \partial\Omega$$

This is the most physically realistic condition for an isolated heart. But it has a mathematical consequence: the elliptic equation (12.5) with pure Neumann BCs on $\phi_e$ has a null space. Adding a constant to $\phi_e$ everywhere does not change any gradient, so $\phi_e$ is determined only up to an additive constant. The linear system is singular.

(b) Grounded bath (Neumann on $\phi_i$, Dirichlet on $\phi_e$). The tissue is submerged in a conductive bath (saline), and the bath is connected to a reference electrode that fixes the extracellular potential. Intracellular current still cannot escape (cells are sealed at the surface), but the extracellular potential is clamped:

(13.4) $$\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\phi_i \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}} = 0 \quad\text{and}\quad \phi_e = \phi_{\text{bath}} \quad\text{on } \partial\Omega$$

This is the standard experimental setup for Langendorff-perfused hearts and tissue wedge preparations. The Dirichlet condition on $\phi_e$ removes the null space: the extracellular potential is pinned to a reference, and the system matrix becomes non-singular. This makes the linear solve easier.

(c) Current injection (Dirichlet on $\phi_i$ at electrode, Neumann elsewhere). An electrode is inserted into the tissue, clamping the intracellular potential at the electrode site. Elsewhere, normal sealed-cell conditions apply. This represents an intracellular stimulation protocol.

The following table summarizes how each combination affects the mathematical structure:

Scenario$\phi_i$ BC$\phi_e$ BCNull space?Physical setting

The null space is not a bug — it is physics. When the entire tissue boundary has Neumann conditions on $\phi_e$, the extracellular potential is defined only relative to some reference point, just like altitude is defined relative to sea level. You can add any constant to $\phi_e$ everywhere without changing any current flow. In practice, we remove this ambiguity by pinning one node (setting $\phi_e = 0$ at a single point) or by projecting out the constant mode in the linear solver (Section 15.3).

13.4.2   Mixed Boundary Conditions: What Do They Mean?

A natural question arises: what happens when the intracellular and extracellular domains have different boundary condition types? Let us think through the two most interesting combinations:

Neumann on $\phi_i$ + Dirichlet on $\phi_e$ (the grounded-bath case). This is the most common experimental configuration. The physical picture: cell membranes seal the intracellular space at the surface (no intracellular current escapes), but the extracellular space opens into a conductive bath that is held at a reference potential. The transmembrane voltage $V_m = \phi_i - \phi_e$ is not clamped — it is free to evolve according to the bidomain equations. Only $\phi_e$ is fixed at the boundary.

Dirichlet on $\phi_i$ + Neumann on $\phi_e$. This represents an intracellular electrode that clamps $\phi_i$ to a specified voltage, while the extracellular space is sealed (no extracellular current leaves). This is rare experimentally — intracellular electrodes are typically small and affect only a few cells — but it is mathematically well-posed and occasionally used in theoretical studies to model localized stimulation.

Dirichlet on both. Both $\phi_i$ and $\phi_e$ are fixed at the boundary. Since $V_m = \phi_i - \phi_e$, this also fixes $V_m$ at the boundary — effectively voltage-clamping the transmembrane potential at the tissue surface. This is extremely rare in practice but might be used to model an idealized experimental chamber with both intracellular and extracellular potential control.

Not all combinations are physically realistic. While the mathematics allows any combination of Dirichlet and Neumann on $\phi_i$ and $\phi_e$, the physics constrains the choices. The intracellular space is almost always Neumann (cells are sealed). The extracellular space depends on the experimental setup: Neumann for isolated tissue, Dirichlet for grounded bath, Robin for partial bath coupling. If you are unsure, start with Neumann on both — it is the most physically conservative choice.

13.4.3   How BCs Modify the Block System

Recall the 2×2 block system from Chapter 13, equation (13.2):

$$\begin{pmatrix} A_{11} & A_{12} \\ A_{21} & A_{22} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{V}_m \\ \boldsymbol{\phi}_e \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{b}_1 \\ \mathbf{b}_2 \end{pmatrix}$$

where $A_{22}$ involves the sum $\mathbf{K}_i + \mathbf{K}_e$ (intracellular plus extracellular stiffness matrices). The boundary conditions affect this system as follows:

Pure Neumann on both (isolated tissue). The matrix $A_{22} = \mathbf{K}_i + \mathbf{K}_e$ inherits the Neumann null space from both stiffness matrices. The all-ones vector $\mathbf{1}$ satisfies $(\mathbf{K}_i + \mathbf{K}_e)\mathbf{1} = \mathbf{0}$, so $A_{22}$ is singular. The block system has a one-dimensional null space: any solution plus a constant shift in $\phi_e$ is also a solution.

Dirichlet on $\phi_e$ (grounded bath). The rows and columns of $A_{22}$ corresponding to Dirichlet boundary nodes are eliminated (as in Section 8.7.3), and their known values are moved to the right-hand side. The reduced $A_{22}$ is non-singular — the system can be solved directly without null-space handling.

Worked Example: 5-Node 1D Bidomain Cable, Isolated Tissue

Consider a 1D bidomain cable with 5 nodes, spacing $\Delta x = 0.025$ cm, intracellular conductivity $\sigma_i = 0.17$ S/m, and extracellular conductivity $\sigma_e = 0.62$ S/m. With Neumann BCs on both $\phi_i$ and $\phi_e$, the 10×10 block system has the structure:

(13.5) $$\begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{K}_i & \mathbf{K}_i \\ \mathbf{K}_i & \mathbf{K}_i + \mathbf{K}_e \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{V}_m \\ \boldsymbol{\phi}_e \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{f} \\ \mathbf{0} \end{pmatrix}$$

Each $\mathbf{K}_d$ ($d = i$ or $e$) is a 5×5 tridiagonal matrix with the Neumann-modified boundary stencil from equation (8.4):

$$\mathbf{K}_d = \frac{\sigma_d}{\Delta x^2}\begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 & & & \\ 1 & -2 & 1 & & \\ & 1 & -2 & 1 & \\ & & 1 & -2 & 1 \\ & & & 1 & -1\end{pmatrix}$$

The bottom-right block $\mathbf{K}_i + \mathbf{K}_e$ is singular (every row sums to zero). To solve the system, we must pin one node — for instance, set $\phi_{e,0} = 0$ by replacing the first equation of the lower block with $\phi_{e,0} = 0$. After pinning:

Row 5 of the 10×10 system becomes: $1 \cdot \phi_{e,0} = 0$. The corresponding column entries for $\phi_{e,0}$ in other rows are moved to the right-hand side. The modified $(\mathbf{K}_i + \mathbf{K}_e)$ block is now non-singular, and the system can be solved by any standard method (direct or iterative).

Why pinning works. Pinning one node removes the constant from the null space without changing the physics. If $\boldsymbol{\phi}_e$ is a solution, then $\boldsymbol{\phi}_e + c\,\mathbf{1}$ is also a solution for any constant $c$. Pinning $\phi_{e,0} = 0$ selects the unique member of this family with $\phi_{e,0} = 0$. All physical quantities ($V_m = \phi_i - \phi_e$, current densities, ion channel states) depend only on differences in $\phi_e$, so the choice of reference does not affect the simulation output.

13.4.4   Summary and Forward Pointers

Boundary conditions in the bidomain are richer than in the monodomain because two unknowns require two BCs per boundary segment. The physically dominant case — Neumann on both $\phi_i$ and $\phi_e$ (isolated tissue) — introduces a null space that must be handled in the linear solver. The grounded-bath case (Neumann on $\phi_i$, Dirichlet on $\phi_e$) eliminates this null space.
