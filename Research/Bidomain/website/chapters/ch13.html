Chapter 13

Spatial Discretization

Chapter 7 developed the spatial discretization toolkit — FDM, FEM, FVM — for a single diffusion operator. Here we apply those same methods to the bidomain system, where two coupled PDEs produce a fundamentally different algebraic structure: a 2×2 block matrix system with $2N$ unknowns.

13.1   From One Stiffness Matrix to Two

In the monodomain model (Chapter 7), spatial discretization produces a single stiffness matrix $\mathbf{K}$ assembled from the effective conductivity tensor $\mathbf{D}$. The bidomain model has two conducting domains — intracellular and extracellular — each with its own conductivity tensor. Applying the same discretization machinery (whether FDM, FEM, or FVM) to each domain separately produces two stiffness matrices:

Intracellular stiffness $\mathbf{K}_i$: assembled from $\mathbf{D}_i$ using exactly the same procedure as the monodomain $\mathbf{K}$, with entries $(K_i)_{jk} = \int_\Omega \nabla\varphi_j \cdot (\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\varphi_k)\,dx$ for FEM, or the corresponding stencil matrix $\mathbf{L}_i$ for FDM.

Extracellular stiffness $\mathbf{K}_e$: assembled identically but with $\mathbf{D}_e$. The combined stiffness $\mathbf{K}_i + \mathbf{K}_e$ corresponds to the bulk conductivity tensor $\mathbf{D}_i + \mathbf{D}_e$.

Engine V5.4's spatial discretization infrastructure already assembles a single stiffness matrix $\mathbf{K}$ (or Laplacian $\mathbf{L}$) from a conductivity tensor field. Extending to bidomain requires calling this same assembly routine twice — once with $\mathbf{D}_i$ and once with $\mathbf{D}_e$ — then combining the results into the 2×2 block format described below.

13.2   The Bidomain Weak Form

For FEM specifically, the bidomain weak form involves both unknowns ($V_m$ and $\phi_e$) simultaneously. Multiplying the parabolic equation by test function $v \in H^1(\Omega)$ and integrating by parts:

$$\begin{aligned} \int_\Omega \chi C_m \frac{\partial V_m}{\partial t}\,v\,dx &+ \int_\Omega \nabla V_m \cdot (\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla v)\,dx + \int_\Omega \nabla\phi_e\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla v)\,dx \\[4pt] &= -\int_\Omega \chi\,I_{\text{ion}}\,v\,dx + \int_\Omega I_{\text{stim}}^i\,v\,dx \end{aligned}$$

For the elliptic equation, multiply by $q \in H^1(\Omega)$:

$$\begin{aligned} \int_\Omega \nabla\phi_e \cdot \bigl[(\mathbf{D}_i + \mathbf{D}_e)\,\nabla q\bigr]\,dx &+ \int_\Omega \nabla V_m \cdot (\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla q)\,dx \\[4pt] &= -\int_\Omega I_{\text{stim}}^e\,q\,dx \end{aligned}$$

Notice the critical structural feature: the intracellular conductivity $\mathbf{D}_i$ appears in both equations — it is the coupling agent between $V_m$ and $\phi_e$. The extracellular conductivity $\mathbf{D}_e$ appears only in the elliptic equation. Expanding in the finite element basis produces the semi-discrete system:

(13.1) $$\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{M} & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0}\end{pmatrix}\!\begin{pmatrix}\dot{\mathbf{V}}_m \\ \dot{\boldsymbol{\phi}}_e\end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{K}_i & \mathbf{K}_i \\ \mathbf{K}_i & \mathbf{K}_i\!+\!\mathbf{K}_e\end{pmatrix}\!\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{V}_m \\ \boldsymbol{\phi}_e\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{F}_V \\ \mathbf{F}_\phi\end{pmatrix}$$

Notice the elegant structure: $\mathbf{K}_i$ appears in every block of the 2×2 stiffness matrix — it is the "glue" coupling $V_m$ and $\phi_e$. The extracellular stiffness $\mathbf{K}_e$ appears only in the (2,2) block, strengthening the elliptic equation. The mass matrix $\mathbf{M}$ (from Chapter 7) appears only in the (1,1) position because only $V_m$ has a time derivative.

13.3   The 2×2 Block System

Regardless of the spatial discretization method (FDM, FEM, or FVM — all described in Chapter 7), the final algebraic system at each time step has the same form:

(13.2) $$\underbrace{\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{A}_{11} & \mathbf{A}_{12} \\ \mathbf{A}_{21} & \mathbf{A}_{22}\end{pmatrix}}_{\mathbf{A}}\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{V}_m \\ \boldsymbol{\phi}_e\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{b}_1 \\ \mathbf{b}_2\end{pmatrix}$$

With the typical identification (after applying a semi-implicit time stepper like Crank–Nicolson):

BlockDefinitionProperties
$\mathbf{A}_{11}$$\mathbf{M}/\Delta t + \mathbf{K}_i$Symmetric positive definite (SPD)
$\mathbf{A}_{12}$$\mathbf{K}_i$Coupling: intracellular stiffness
$\mathbf{A}_{21}$$\mathbf{K}_i$$= \mathbf{A}_{12}^T$ (symmetric)
$\mathbf{A}_{22}$$\mathbf{K}_i + \mathbf{K}_e$SPD (combined stiffness)

The total system has $2N$ unknowns (where $N$ is the number of spatial nodes) — double the monodomain system's $N$ unknowns. This is the fundamental object that all subsequent solver strategies (Chapter 16) must handle efficiently. The system is symmetric but indefinite — a saddle-point problem that requires specialized linear algebra. This is a qualitative departure from the monodomain, where the implicit system is SPD and solvable by conjugate gradient (Chapter 10).
