Chapter 13

From Equations to Matrices

Chapter 8 turned the monodomain PDE into a single matrix equation. Here we do the same for the bidomain — but because we have two coupled PDEs, we get a 2×2 block matrix system. The good news: the assembly tools from Chapter 8 carry over directly. We just call them twice.

13.1   From One Stiffness Matrix to Two

Recall from Chapter 8: spatial discretization converts a continuous PDE into a matrix equation. The monodomain model has one conductivity tensor $\mathbf{D}$, which produces one stiffness matrix (or Laplacian operator) $\mathbf{L}$. The bidomain model has two conducting domains, each with its own conductivity tensor. Applying the same discretization machinery to each domain separately produces two operators:

  • Intracellular Laplacian $\mathbf{L}_i$: discretizes $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\,\cdot\,)$ using exactly the same stencil or assembly procedure as the monodomain $\mathbf{L}$.
  • Extracellular Laplacian $\mathbf{L}_e$: discretizes $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_e\,\nabla\,\cdot\,)$, assembled identically but with $\mathbf{D}_e$.

The combined operator $\mathbf{L}_i + \mathbf{L}_e$ corresponds to the bulk conductivity $\mathbf{D}_i + \mathbf{D}_e$.

Think of it this way. In the monodomain world, you built one road network (one $\mathbf{L}$) that carries all the traffic. In the bidomain world, there are two road networks overlaid on the same city — one for intracellular current ($\mathbf{L}_i$) and one for extracellular current ($\mathbf{L}_e$). Each network is built the same way, just with different road widths (conductivities). The two networks are coupled at every intersection: current can cross from one to the other through the cell membrane.

Engine V1 connection. The BidomainSpatialDiscretization class in discretization/fdm.py builds $\mathbf{L}_i$ and $\mathbf{L}_e$ by calling the same stencil-assembly routine twice — once with $D_i$ and once with $D_e$. The matrices are stored as sparse tensors and reused across all time steps.

13.2   The 2×2 Block System

Now let us put these two operators together. After discretization and time-stepping (details in Chapter 14), the bidomain system at each time step becomes a linear system with $2N$ unknowns — $N$ values for $V_m$ and $N$ values for $\phi_e$:

(13.1) $$\underbrace{\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{A}_{11} & \mathbf{A}_{12} \\ \mathbf{A}_{21} & \mathbf{A}_{22}\end{pmatrix}}_{\mathbf{A}}\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{V}_m \\ \boldsymbol{\phi}_e\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{b}_1 \\ \mathbf{b}_2\end{pmatrix}$$

The blocks have a simple structure:

BlockDefinitionRole
$\mathbf{A}_{11}$$\frac{1}{\Delta t}\mathbf{I} - \theta\,\mathbf{L}_i$Parabolic: mass + intracellular diffusion
$\mathbf{A}_{12}$$\mathbf{L}_i$Coupling: how $\phi_e$ affects $V_m$
$\mathbf{A}_{21}$$\mathbf{L}_i$Coupling: how $V_m$ drives $\phi_e$
$\mathbf{A}_{22}$$-(\mathbf{L}_i + \mathbf{L}_e)$Elliptic: combined bulk conductivity

Here $\theta$ is the implicitness parameter ($\theta = 0.5$ for Crank–Nicolson, $\theta = 1$ for backward Euler), and $\mathbf{I}$ is the identity matrix (using lumped mass, standard for FDM/FVM). Notice: $\mathbf{L}_i$ appears in every block — it is the glue coupling the two unknowns. The extracellular operator $\mathbf{L}_e$ appears only in $\mathbf{A}_{22}$.

Why does $\mathbf{L}_i$ appear everywhere? Go back to the physics. The intracellular domain is the only path connecting $V_m$ and $\phi_e$. Current crosses the membrane (changing $V_m$), flows through intracellular space (governed by $\mathbf{D}_i$), and must be balanced by extracellular return current. So $\mathbf{L}_i$ — the intracellular operator — naturally shows up in both the parabolic equation (how $V_m$ evolves) and the elliptic equation (how $\phi_e$ responds).

Figure 13.1. The 2×2 block structure of the bidomain linear system. The diagonal blocks are SPD; the off-diagonal coupling blocks are the intracellular Laplacian $\mathbf{L}_i$.

13.3   The FDM Face-Based Stencil

In Chapter 8, we built the monodomain Laplacian using the standard 5-point (2D) or 7-point (3D) FDM stencil. For Neumann boundary conditions, we used a ghost-node mirror approach (Section 8.7.1). This works perfectly for the monodomain, but the bidomain requires a subtle change.

The issue is with the elliptic equation. In the monodomain implicit system, the matrix is $\frac{1}{\Delta t}\mathbf{I} + \theta\,\mathbf{L}$ — the identity term dominates and guarantees symmetry and positive-definiteness regardless of how $\mathbf{L}$ handles boundaries. But the elliptic operator $-(\mathbf{L}_i + \mathbf{L}_e)$ has no identity term. If the ghost-node mirror produces an asymmetric boundary stencil (which it does — boundary rows have halved coefficients), the elliptic matrix is non-symmetric, non-SPD, and iterative solvers like PCG will fail.

The fix: face-based stencils. Instead of mirroring ghost nodes, simply count the faces around each node. Interior nodes have 4 faces (in 2D); boundary nodes have 3 faces; corner nodes have 2. Each face contributes one neighbor term to the stencil. If a neighbor would be outside the domain, that face does not exist — skip it. The result is a symmetric Laplacian by construction: if node $j$ contributes to node $i$'s stencil, then node $i$ contributes equally to node $j$'s stencil.

This is a hard-won practical lesson. The ghost-node approach and the face-based approach produce the same results for the monodomain (the identity term papers over the asymmetry), but only the face-based approach works for the bidomain elliptic equation. The Engine V1 FDM discretization uses the face-based stencil for exactly this reason.

13.4   A Note on the Weak Form (FEM)

If you are using finite elements rather than finite differences, the bidomain weak form produces the same block structure as equation (13.1), but with mass matrices $\mathbf{M}$ replacing the identity $\mathbf{I}$ in the (1,1) block, and stiffness matrices $\mathbf{K}_i$, $\mathbf{K}_e$ replacing the Laplacians $\mathbf{L}_i$, $\mathbf{L}_e$. The FEM weak form and its derivation follow the same pattern as the monodomain weak form in Section 8.4 — multiply by test functions, integrate by parts, expand in the basis. The resulting 2×2 block structure is identical; only the matrix entries differ.

For the remainder of Part III, we work with the FDM operators $\mathbf{L}_i$, $\mathbf{L}_e$ and the identity mass matrix $\mathbf{I}$. Everything translates to FEM by replacing $\mathbf{I} \to \mathbf{M}$ and $\mathbf{L} \to \mathbf{K}$.

13.5   Worked Example: 5-Node Bidomain Cable

Let us build the complete bidomain system for a concrete case. Consider a 1D cable with 5 nodes, spacing $\Delta x = 0.025$ cm, and Formulation B diffusivities $D_i = 1.214 \times 10^{-3}$ cm$^2$/ms, $D_e = 4.429 \times 10^{-3}$ cm$^2$/ms (corresponding to Clerc 1976 conductivities with $\chi = 1400$ cm$^{-1}$, $C_m = 1.0$ $\mu$F/cm$^2$).

Step 1: Build the Laplacians.

Using the face-based stencil, each $\mathbf{L}_d$ is a 5×5 tridiagonal matrix:

$$\mathbf{L}_d = \frac{D_d}{\Delta x^2}\begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 & & & \\ 1 & -2 & 1 & & \\ & 1 & -2 & 1 & \\ & & 1 & -2 & 1 \\ & & & 1 & -1\end{pmatrix}$$

For $\mathbf{L}_i$: the scale factor is $D_i / \Delta x^2 = 1.214 \times 10^{-3} / (6.25 \times 10^{-4}) = 1.943$ ms$^{-1}$. For $\mathbf{L}_e$: the scale factor is $D_e / \Delta x^2 = 4.429 \times 10^{-3} / (6.25 \times 10^{-4}) = 7.086$ ms$^{-1}$.

Step 2: Build the block system.

With $\Delta t = 0.02$ ms and $\theta = 0.5$ (Crank–Nicolson), the parabolic block is:

$$\mathbf{A}_{11} = \frac{1}{\Delta t}\mathbf{I} - \theta\,\mathbf{L}_i = 50\,\mathbf{I} - 0.5\,\mathbf{L}_i$$

The diagonal entries of $\mathbf{A}_{11}$ are $50 + 0.5 \times 1.943 \times 2 = 51.943$ for interior nodes and $50 + 0.5 \times 1.943 = 50.971$ for boundary nodes. The off-diagonal entries are $-0.5 \times 1.943 = -0.971$. This is clearly SPD (diagonally dominant with positive diagonal).

The elliptic block is:

$$\mathbf{A}_{22} = -(\mathbf{L}_i + \mathbf{L}_e)$$

Combined scale factor: $(D_i + D_e)/\Delta x^2 = 9.029$ ms$^{-1}$. Interior diagonal: $2 \times 9.029 = 18.058$. Off-diagonal: $-9.029$. This matrix is symmetric positive semi-definite (every row sums to zero). After pinning $\phi_{e,0} = 0$ (replacing row/column 0 with the identity), it becomes SPD.

Step 3: Solve via decoupled Gauss-Seidel.

Rather than solving the full 10×10 system, the decoupled approach solves two separate 5×5 systems sequentially. This is exactly what the code does — and it is the subject of the next chapter.
