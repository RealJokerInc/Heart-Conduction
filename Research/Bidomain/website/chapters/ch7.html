Chapter 7

The Monodomain Equation

A single cell model tells you what happens at one point. To simulate a wave of activation spreading across the heart, we need to couple millions of these cells through the physics of electrical diffusion. The monodomain equation is this coupling — and it is the equation that Engine V5.4 was built to solve.

7.1   From Single Cell to Tissue: Adding Diffusion

Imagine you have a row of light bulbs connected by wires. If you turn on one bulb in the middle, electrical current flows through the wires to its neighbors, and they start to glow too — then their neighbors light up, and so on down the line. That is exactly what happens in cardiac tissue. One cell depolarizes (the "bulb turns on"), and current flows through gap junctions (the "wires") to neighboring cells, triggering them in sequence. This chain reaction is the propagating action potential.

In Chapter 1, we wrote the membrane current equation for a single cell: $C_m \, dV_m/dt = -I_{\text{ion}}$. This says "the voltage changes because ions flow through channels." But in tissue, there is a second source of current: electrotonic current from neighboring cells, flowing through gap junctions.

When cell A depolarizes before cell B, a voltage gradient exists between them, and current flows from A to B through the intracellular pathway. This is just Ohm's law — voltage difference divided by resistance gives current — written in continuous form:

$$\mathbf{J}_i = -\mathbf{D}_i \nabla V_m$$

where $\mathbf{D}_i$ is the effective intracellular conductivity tensor — it encodes both the gap-junction coupling strength and the geometric anisotropy of the tissue. The divergence of this current, $\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{D}_i \nabla V_m)$, gives the net current arriving at each point from its neighbors. Adding this to the single-cell equation gives us the monodomain equation.

7.2   The Reaction–Diffusion Formulation

Before writing the equation, let us understand what it will say in plain language. At every point in the tissue, the voltage changes for exactly two reasons: (1) ion channels at that point open and close, pushing the voltage up or down — this is the reaction, the local chemistry; (2) current leaks in from depolarized neighbors or out to resting neighbors through gap junctions — this is the diffusion, the spatial coupling. The monodomain equation is simply the mathematical statement that "voltage change = reaction + diffusion."

Written out, the monodomain equation is:

(7.1) $$\chi C_m \frac{\partial V_m}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot (\mathbf{D}\,\nabla V_m) - \chi\,I_{\text{ion}}(V_m, \mathbf{w}) + I_{\text{stim}}$$

This is a reaction–diffusion equation — one of the most important classes of PDEs in mathematical biology. The "reaction" is the ionic current $I_{\text{ion}}$ (local chemistry at each cell), and the "diffusion" is $\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{D}\,\nabla V_m)$ (spatial coupling between cells). The stimulus current $I_{\text{stim}}$ represents external pacing.

The monodomain equation says: "at every point in the tissue, the voltage changes for two reasons — (1) ion channels open and close locally (the reaction), and (2) current leaks in from depolarized neighbors or out to resting neighbors (the diffusion)." These two processes compete: the reaction creates sharp voltage fronts, while the diffusion smooths them out. The balance determines the conduction velocity — typically 60–80 cm/s along fibers in healthy ventricular tissue.

The state vector $\mathbf{w}$ contains all the gating variables and concentrations from the ionic model (18 for TTP06, 40 for ORd). These evolve by their own ODEs at each spatial point:

(7.2) $$\frac{\partial \mathbf{w}}{\partial t} = \mathbf{g}(V_m, \mathbf{w})$$

Note that $\mathbf{w}$ has no diffusion term — gating variables do not diffuse from cell to cell. Only voltage (and in the bidomain model, extracellular potential) spreads spatially. This locality of the ionic state is what makes operator splitting so natural.

7.3   Conductivity, Fibers, and Anisotropy

Think of cardiac tissue like a bundle of straws, all pointing roughly the same direction. If you push water into one straw, it flows easily along the straw's length but very poorly sideways into neighboring straws. Heart muscle works the same way: myocytes are long, thin cells connected end-to-end by gap junctions. Electrical current zips along the fiber direction about three times faster than it crosses fibers. This directional preference is called anisotropy, and it shapes the entire wavefront: elliptical in a tissue slab, spiraling in the ventricle wall where fibers rotate transmurally from epicardium to endocardium.

Mathematically, a single number (scalar conductivity) cannot capture "fast in one direction, slow in another." We need a tensor — essentially a $2 \times 2$ or $3 \times 3$ matrix that converts a voltage gradient in any direction into the resulting current in that direction. If $\mathbf{f}$ is the local fiber direction (a unit vector), the conductivity tensor is:

(7.3) $$\mathbf{D} = d_\parallel \mathbf{f}\mathbf{f}^T + d_\perp(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{f}\mathbf{f}^T)$$

where $d_\parallel$ and $d_\perp$ are the conductivities along and across fibers, respectively. Typical values are $d_\parallel \approx 0.174$ and $d_\perp \approx 0.019$ mm$^2$/ms, giving an anisotropy ratio of about 3:1 in conduction velocity (since CV $\propto \sqrt{d}$). In 3D, a sheet-normal direction adds a third axis with even lower conductivity.

7.4   Spatial Discretization: What It Means Physically

The continuous diffusion operator $\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{D}\,\nabla V_m)$ answers a simple physical question: how much net electrical current is flowing into this point from its neighbors? Spatial discretization replaces this continuous question with a discrete one: given the voltage at every mesh point, compute the net current arriving at each point from its neighbors. This computation turns out to be a matrix-vector product.

Think of it this way. If node $i$ is at $-85$ mV (resting) and its neighbor $j$ is at $+20$ mV (depolarized), there is a voltage difference of 105 mV driving current from $j$ to $i$ through the intracellular resistance. The amount of current depends on the conductivity between them and the distance. Summing up these pairwise contributions from all neighbors of node $i$ gives the net diffusive current — and that sum is exactly one row of the matrix-vector product $\mathbf{K}\cdot\mathbf{V}$.

Three classical methods exist for building this matrix. Each approaches the problem from a different mathematical direction, but all three produce the same object: a sparse matrix that encodes how current flows through the tissue mesh. The matrix is sparse because each node couples only to its immediate neighbors — a node in the middle of a 3D mesh might have 6–26 neighbors out of millions of total nodes.

7.5   Finite Difference Method (FDM)

The Central Idea

FDM is the most direct approach: place nodes on a regular grid with uniform spacing $h$, and approximate the second derivative using the values at neighboring grid points. The fundamental approximation in 1D is:

$$d\,\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial x^2}\bigg|_i \;\approx\; \frac{d}{h^2}\bigl(V_{i-1} - 2V_i + V_{i+1}\bigr)$$

This is second-order accurate (error $\sim O(h^2)$), derived from Taylor expansion. But the deeper insight is geometric: the expression $V_{i-1} - 2V_i + V_{i+1}$ measures how much $V_i$ deviates from the average of its neighbors. If $V_i$ is higher than the average $\tfrac{1}{2}(V_{i-1} + V_{i+1})$, the Laplacian is negative — current flows outward, pulling $V_i$ down. If $V_i$ is lower than the average, current flows inward, pushing $V_i$ up. Diffusion always acts to smooth out voltage differences.

Worked Example: 5-Node 1D Cable

Consider a tiny 1D cardiac cable with 5 nodes, spacing $h = 0.2$ mm, and uniform conductivity $d = 0.1$ mm$^2$/ms. The no-flux (Neumann) boundary condition means no current flows out of the cable ends.

Figure 7.1. A 5-node 1D cable with node 3 depolarized to +20 mV (wavefront). All other nodes at resting potential −85 mV.

The FDM stencil $[1, -2, 1]$ is applied at each interior node. At boundary nodes (0 and 4), the no-flux condition $\partial V/\partial x = 0$ modifies the stencil to $[-1, 1]$ (the "missing" neighbor is replaced by mirroring). Collecting all stencils into a matrix, we get the stiffness matrix $\mathbf{K}$:

$$\mathbf{K} = \frac{d}{h^2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 2 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = 2.5\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 2 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Three properties of $\mathbf{K}$ to notice: (1) it is symmetric — the coupling from node $i$ to $j$ equals the coupling from $j$ to $i$; (2) it is tridiagonal — each node couples only to its immediate neighbors; (3) every row sums to zero — applying a uniform voltage $V = c\,\mathbf{1}$ gives $\mathbf{K}\cdot V = \mathbf{0}$, meaning diffusion does nothing to a spatially constant voltage (as it should).

Now let us compute the diffusion term $-\mathbf{K}\cdot\mathbf{V}$ for the wavefront scenario $\mathbf{V} = [-85, -85, -85, +20, -85]^T$ (mV):

$$-\mathbf{K}\cdot\mathbf{V} = -2.5\begin{pmatrix}1(-85) + (-1)(-85)\\(-1)(-85) + 2(-85) + (-1)(-85)\\(-1)(-85) + 2(-85) + (-1)(20)\\(-1)(-85) + 2(20) + (-1)(-85)\\(-1)(20) + 1(-85)\end{pmatrix} = -2.5\begin{pmatrix}0\\0\\-190\\210\\-105\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0\\0\\475\\-525\\262.5\end{pmatrix}$$

Read this result physically:

Node 2 ($-85$ mV, next to wavefront): receives $+475$ mV/ms of diffusive current — a large positive push from the depolarized node 3. This is the electrotonic current that will soon trigger node 2's sodium channels, propagating the wave to the left.

Node 3 ($+20$ mV, the wavefront): loses $-525$ mV/ms — current flows out to both resting neighbors. The depolarized cell is the "source" that drives the wavefront forward.

Node 4 ($-85$ mV, behind the wavefront): receives $+262.5$ mV/ms from node 3. Only half the push that node 2 gets, because node 4 has only one neighbor (boundary).

Nodes 0–1 (far from wavefront): zero diffusive current — all neighbors have the same voltage, so there is nothing to diffuse.

This example reveals the core mechanism of wave propagation. The depolarized region (node 3) acts as a current source, pushing electrotonic current into the resting neighbors (nodes 2 and 4). When this current is strong enough to bring a neighbor above threshold ($\sim-65$ mV), the neighbor's sodium channels fire explosively, depolarizing it and extending the wavefront. The matrix $\mathbf{K}$ encodes all of this neighbor-to-neighbor coupling.

The 5-Point and 9-Point Stencils (2D)

In two dimensions with isotropic conductivity $d$, the Laplacian is approximated by the 5-point stencil:

$$d\,\nabla^2 V\big|_{i,j} \approx \frac{d}{h^2}\bigl(V_{i+1,j} + V_{i-1,j} + V_{i,j+1} + V_{i,j-1} - 4V_{i,j}\bigr)$$

Same idea as 1D: the value at the center minus the average of its four axis-aligned neighbors. The stiffness matrix now has 5 nonzeros per row (the center node and its four neighbors). On an $N \times N$ grid, $\mathbf{K}$ is $N^2 \times N^2$ — potentially millions of rows — but with only $\sim 5N^2$ nonzero entries. This extreme sparsity is what makes large-scale cardiac simulation tractable.

When the conductivity tensor $\mathbf{D}$ has off-diagonal entries (because cardiac fibers are rotated relative to the grid axes):

$$\mathbf{D} = \begin{pmatrix}D_{xx} & D_{xy} \\ D_{xy} & D_{yy}\end{pmatrix}$$

the 5-point stencil can only handle the diagonal terms $D_{xx}$ and $D_{yy}$. The cross-derivative $D_{xy}\,\partial^2 V / \partial x\,\partial y$ requires the 9-point stencil, which includes diagonal neighbors:

$$D_{xy}\,\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial x\,\partial y}\bigg|_{i,j} \approx \frac{D_{xy}}{4h^2}\bigl(V_{i+1,j+1} - V_{i+1,j-1} - V_{i-1,j+1} + V_{i-1,j-1}\bigr)$$

In 3D, the full anisotropic operator requires a 27-point stencil — the node plus all 26 neighbors in a $3 \times 3 \times 3$ cube. The stiffness matrix then has up to 27 nonzeros per row.

Figure 7.2. FDM stencils for the 2D diffusion operator. Left: The 5-point stencil uses axis-aligned neighbors only (isotropic or axis-aligned anisotropic). Right: The 9-point stencil adds diagonal neighbors (green) for the cross-derivative $D_{xy}$ terms from rotated fiber fields.

When to use FDM: FDM is fastest, simplest to implement, and cache-friendly — ideal for regular geometries (tissue slabs, rectangular patches), GPU computation, and rapid prototyping. Its limitation is that it requires a structured Cartesian grid. You cannot fit a regular grid to the curved walls of the left ventricle without staircase artifacts at the boundary. For realistic anatomical geometries, use FEM or FVM instead.

7.6   Finite Element Method (FEM)

Why FEM? The Geometry Problem

Real hearts are not rectangular. The left ventricle is a thick-walled ellipsoidal shell with curved boundaries, trabeculations, and papillary muscles. To represent this geometry faithfully, we need meshes made of triangles (2D) or tetrahedra (3D) that can conform to any shape. FDM cannot do this. FEM can.

The mathematical trick that enables unstructured meshes is a change of perspective. Instead of approximating derivatives pointwise (as FDM does), FEM reformulates the PDE in weak form — multiplying by a test function and integrating. This seemingly abstract step has a profound practical consequence: it lowers the smoothness requirement on the solution, allowing us to use simple piecewise-linear "hat functions" on arbitrary triangulations.

Weak Form: What Integration by Parts Really Does

Before diving into the math, let us understand the idea in plain language. We want to ask: "does my approximate solution satisfy the PDE?" But checking this point-by-point (like FDM does) fails on triangular meshes because our piecewise-linear hat functions have kinks — the second derivative is undefined at element boundaries. So instead, we check the PDE on average, by multiplying both sides by a smooth "test function" $v$ and integrating over the whole domain. If the average checks out for every possible test function, the solution must be correct. This is the weak form.

The magic trick is integration by parts (the multidimensional version of the product rule from calculus). It shifts one derivative from $V_m$ onto the test function $v$, so we only need first derivatives of both — and first derivatives of our hat functions are perfectly well-defined (they are just constant slopes on each triangle). This is why FEM can use simple piecewise-linear approximations on arbitrary meshes, while FDM needs smooth grids.

Now let us see this concretely. Start with the monodomain diffusion term. Multiply by a test function $v$, integrate over the domain $\Omega$, and apply integration by parts (the divergence theorem):

$$\underbrace{\int_\Omega \chi C_m \frac{\partial V_m}{\partial t}\,v\,dx}_{\text{time derivative}} + \underbrace{\int_\Omega \nabla V_m \cdot (\mathbf{D}\,\nabla v)\,dx}_{\text{diffusion (after IBP)}} = \underbrace{-\int_\Omega \chi\,I_{\text{ion}}\,v\,dx}_{\text{ionic current}} + \underbrace{\int_\Omega I_{\text{stim}}\,v\,dx}_{\text{stimulus}}$$

The crucial step is the integration by parts on the diffusion term. The original PDE contains $\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{D}\,\nabla V_m)$ — a second derivative of $V_m$. After integration by parts, we have $\nabla V_m \cdot (\mathbf{D}\,\nabla v)$ — only first derivatives of both $V_m$ and $v$. The boundary integral vanishes because of the no-flux condition (physically: no current flows out of the heart). This matters because first derivatives of piecewise-linear functions are well-defined (they are piecewise constant), while second derivatives are not.

From Integrals to Matrices

Expand the voltage as $V_m(\mathbf{x}, t) = \sum_j V_j(t)\,\varphi_j(\mathbf{x})$, where each $\varphi_j$ is a "hat function" that equals 1 at node $j$ and 0 at all other nodes. Choose $v = \varphi_i$ for each node $i$. The integrals become matrix entries:

(7.4) $$\mathbf{M}\,\dot{\mathbf{V}}_m + \mathbf{K}\,\mathbf{V}_m = \mathbf{F}$$

with two matrices and one vector:

Mass matrix $M_{ij} = \int_\Omega \chi C_m\,\varphi_i\,\varphi_j\,dx$ — comes from the time derivative. Physically, it weights each node by the "volume" it represents. It is symmetric, positive definite, and sparse.

Stiffness matrix $K_{ij} = \int_\Omega \nabla\varphi_i \cdot (\mathbf{D}\,\nabla\varphi_j)\,dx$ — encodes the diffusive coupling. This is the FEM analogue of the FDM matrix we built above. It is symmetric, positive semi-definite (row sums = 0 under Neumann BC), and sparse.

Load vector $F_i = -\int_\Omega \chi\,I_{\text{ion}}\,\varphi_i\,dx + \int_\Omega I_{\text{stim}}\,\varphi_i\,dx$ — contains the ionic current and stimulus. This is where Chapters 5–6 connect: at each node, TTP06 or ORd evaluates $I_{\text{ion}}$, which becomes an entry in $\mathbf{F}$.

Worked Example: 4-Node, 2-Triangle Mesh

Consider the simplest 2D mesh: a unit square $[0,h] \times [0,h]$ divided into two triangles. We use isotropic conductivity $d$ and spacing $h$.

Figure 7.3. A minimal 2D FEM mesh: 4 nodes, 2 triangles ($T_1$ with nodes 1-2-3, $T_2$ with nodes 1-3-4). The diagonal splits the square into two right triangles of area $h^2/2$.

FEM assembles the global stiffness matrix element-by-element. For each triangle, we compute a small element stiffness matrix (3×3 for a triangle with 3 nodes), then "scatter" its entries into the correct positions in the global matrix.

For a triangle with vertices at $\mathbf{x}_a$, $\mathbf{x}_b$, $\mathbf{x}_c$ and area $A$, the gradients of the linear basis functions are constant vectors: $\nabla\varphi_a = \tfrac{1}{2A}(y_b - y_c,\; x_c - x_b)^T$ (and cyclic permutations). The element stiffness entry is:

$$K^e_{ab} = A\,\nabla\varphi_a \cdot (d\,\nabla\varphi_b) = \frac{d}{4A}\bigl[(y_b - y_c)(y_c - y_a) + (x_c - x_b)(x_a - x_c)\bigr]$$

Triangle $T_1$ (nodes 1, 2, 3) with vertices $(0,0)$, $(h,0)$, $(h,h)$, area $= h^2/2$:

$$\mathbf{K}^{T_1} = \frac{d}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \qquad \text{(for nodes 1, 2, 3)}$$

Triangle $T_2$ (nodes 1, 3, 4) with vertices $(0,0)$, $(h,h)$, $(0,h)$, area $= h^2/2$:

$$\mathbf{K}^{T_2} = \frac{d}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & -1 \\ -1 & -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \qquad \text{(for nodes 1, 3, 4)}$$

Global assembly: Add element matrices into the global $4 \times 4$ matrix. Node 1 appears in both triangles, so its diagonal entry gets contributions from both. Node 2 appears only in $T_1$; node 4 only in $T_2$. The result:

$$\mathbf{K}_{\text{global}} = \frac{d}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 & 0 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 0 & -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Check: every row sums to zero (conservation under Neumann BC). The matrix is symmetric and positive semi-definite with a single zero eigenvalue (the constant mode). Physically, each node couples to its connected neighbors with equal weight $-d/2$ — which makes sense because the mesh is symmetric and the conductivity is isotropic.

Notice what happened during assembly: the element stiffness matrices overlap at shared nodes. Node 1's diagonal entry is $d/2 + d/2 = d$ (contributions from both triangles). Node 3's diagonal entry is also $d/2 + d/2 = d$. The off-diagonal entries come from whichever element(s) connect the two nodes. Nodes 2 and 4 are not directly connected by any element edge, so $K_{24} = 0$ — even though they are geometric neighbors across the diagonal.

The Mass Matrix: Consistent vs. Lumped

The mass matrix for a linear triangle with area $A$ has entries:

$$M^e_{ab} = \frac{\chi C_m A}{12}\times\begin{cases} 2 & \text{if } a = b \\ 1 & \text{if } a \neq b \end{cases}$$

For our two-triangle mesh (each with area $h^2/2$), the assembled consistent mass matrix is:

$$\mathbf{M}_{\text{consistent}} = \frac{\chi C_m h^2}{12}\begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 2 & 1 & 4 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

This matrix is full (no zero entries for connected nodes) and couples each node to its neighbors even for the time derivative. This coupling is mathematically correct but computationally inconvenient: for explicit time stepping, you would need to solve $\mathbf{M}\,\dot{\mathbf{V}} = \mathbf{b}$ at every timestep, which requires inverting $\mathbf{M}$.

The lumped mass matrix avoids this by concentrating each row's total mass onto the diagonal:

$$\mathbf{M}_{\text{lumped}} = \frac{\chi C_m h^2}{3}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

The diagonal entries reflect the area "owned" by each node: nodes 1 and 3 (shared by two triangles) each own $2 \times h^2/6 = h^2/3$ of area, while nodes 2 and 4 (each in one triangle) own $h^2/6$. With a lumped mass matrix, explicit time stepping becomes trivial: $\dot{V}_i = b_i / M_{ii}$ — just a pointwise division, no linear solve required.

Mass lumping introduces an $O(h^2)$ error — the same order as the spatial discretization itself. So for P1 elements, lumping does not degrade the overall accuracy order. This is why lumped mass is the default choice in cardiac simulation: it preserves the convergence rate while eliminating the mass-matrix solve. The only case where consistent mass matters is when using higher-order elements (P2 or above), where lumping can reduce the accuracy benefit.

Element Types

For cardiac simulations, P1 elements (linear triangles in 2D, linear tetrahedra in 3D) are the workhorse, providing $O(h^2)$ accuracy with minimal overhead. The gradients are constant within each element, making the integrals trivial. P2 elements (quadratic) achieve $O(h^3)$ accuracy but at $\sim$8× the memory cost. For the monodomain problem — where the ionic model, not the linear solve, is the bottleneck — P1 elements with mesh refinement are generally preferred.

When to use FEM: FEM is the method of choice for realistic anatomical heart geometries. It handles any mesh shape (triangles, tetrahedra, hexahedra), naturally accommodates curved boundaries, and has rigorous mathematical convergence theory. Its main cost is the assembly step (computing element stiffness matrices and scattering into the global matrix) and the need for a mass matrix. For structured Cartesian grids, FDM is faster; for problems dominated by sharp conductivity jumps, FVM may be more appropriate.

Engine V5.4 supports P1 FEM on triangular (2D) and tetrahedral (3D) meshes. The mass matrix can be either consistent or lumped (diagonal). The stiffness matrix $\mathbf{K}$ is assembled element-by-element and stored in CSR (Compressed Sparse Row) format. Both explicit solvers (which need only $\mathbf{K}\mathbf{V}$ products) and implicit solvers (which solve $(\mathbf{M} + \alpha\,\mathbf{K})\mathbf{V} = \mathbf{b}$) operate through the same assembled matrices.

7.7   Finite Volume Method (FVM)

The Conservation Principle

FVM starts from the most physically intuitive formulation: conservation. Around each node $i$, draw a control volume $\Omega_i$ — the region of space "owned" by that node. Integrate the monodomain equation over $\Omega_i$:

$$\chi C_m\,|\Omega_i|\,\frac{dV_i}{dt} = \underbrace{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} F_{ij}}_{\text{net flux from neighbors}} - \chi\,|\Omega_i|\,I_{\text{ion},i}$$

where $|\Omega_i|$ is the volume (or area, in 2D) of the control cell, $\mathcal{N}(i)$ is the set of neighbors sharing a face with cell $i$, and $F_{ij}$ is the flux from cell $j$ into cell $i$. The key insight: the flux leaving cell $i$ into cell $j$ is exactly equal and opposite to the flux entering cell $j$ from cell $i$: $F_{ij} = -F_{ji}$. This ensures that total charge is conserved to machine precision — no charge is created or destroyed at any face.

Two-Point Flux Approximation (TPFA)

The flux between cells $i$ and $j$ is approximated using only the voltages at the two cell centers:

$$F_{ij} = \sigma_{ij}\,\frac{V_j - V_i}{|\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i|}\,A_{ij}$$

where $A_{ij}$ is the area of the shared face and $\sigma_{ij}$ is the effective conductivity at the face. When the two cells have different conductivities ($\sigma_i \neq \sigma_j$), the correct effective value is the harmonic mean:

$$\sigma_{ij} = \frac{2\sigma_i\,\sigma_j}{\sigma_i + \sigma_j}$$

This is the formula for two resistors in series — the face lies between the two cells, so the total resistance is the sum of half-cell resistances. Importantly, if one cell has zero conductivity (scar tissue), $\sigma_{ij} = 0$ regardless of the other cell's conductivity. This makes FVM naturally correct at sharp tissue boundaries.

Worked Example: 1D, 4-Cell FVM

Consider four control volumes in 1D with uniform cell width $h$ and uniform conductivity $\sigma$. Each face has area 1 (unit cross-section). The flux from cell $j$ to cell $i$ is $F_{ij} = \sigma\,(V_j - V_i)/h$.

The balance equation for cell $i$ (interior) is: $h\,\chi C_m\,dV_i/dt = \sigma(V_{i-1} - V_i)/h + \sigma(V_{i+1} - V_i)/h$, which simplifies to:

$$\chi C_m\,\frac{dV_i}{dt} = \frac{\sigma}{h^2}\bigl(V_{i-1} - 2V_i + V_{i+1}\bigr)$$

This is identical to the FDM formula! On a uniform 1D grid with uniform conductivity, FVM and FDM produce the same stiffness matrix. The difference emerges in two situations: (1) non-uniform grids, where FVM correctly accounts for variable cell sizes through the control-volume areas, and (2) discontinuous conductivity, where the harmonic mean gives the physically correct flux while FDM's arithmetic average does not.

When to use FVM: FVM excels when conservation is paramount and when conductivity has sharp jumps (e.g., scar tissue abutting healthy myocardium). The harmonic-mean flux ensures physically correct behavior at material interfaces. The trade-off is that the two-point flux approximation requires the mesh to be "K-orthogonal" (control-volume faces perpendicular to the line connecting cell centers) for full accuracy with anisotropic conductivity. On non-orthogonal meshes, more complex multi-point flux approximations are needed.

7.9   Connecting the Pieces: From PDE to Matrix System

We have spent the last five sections building spatial discretization tools — stiffness matrices from FDM, FEM, and FVM. Now comes the payoff: we will derive, step by step, the central matrix equation that the simulator actually solves. The goal is to show you exactly where every term comes from, so that equation (7.5) below feels inevitable rather than pulled from thin air.

Here is the roadmap. We start from the continuous PDE (equation 7.1), break it into pieces, replace each continuous piece with its discrete counterpart, and collect everything into one matrix equation. By the end, you will see that equation (7.5) is just a compact way of writing "voltage changes at every node because of ion channels (local) plus current from neighbors (spatial)."

Step 1: Start from the Continuous PDE

Recall the monodomain equation (7.1):

$$\chi C_m \frac{\partial V_m}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot (\mathbf{D}\,\nabla V_m) - \chi\,I_{\text{ion}}(V_m, \mathbf{w}) + I_{\text{stim}}$$

This is a continuous equation — it holds at every point $\mathbf{x}$ in the tissue simultaneously. A computer cannot handle "every point" (there are infinitely many), so we pick a finite set of $N$ points (the mesh nodes) and ask: what does this equation look like at node $i$?

Step 2: Sample at Node $i$ (the Semi-Discrete Idea)

Let $V_{m,i}(t)$ denote the voltage at node $i$ as a function of time. The time derivative is simple — it just becomes $dV_{m,i}/dt$, an ordinary derivative because we are tracking a single node's voltage over time. The ionic current is also simple — it only depends on local quantities, so it becomes $I_{\text{ion}}(V_{m,i}, \mathbf{w}_i)$ evaluated entirely at node $i$. The tricky term is the diffusion operator $\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{D}\,\nabla V_m)$, because it involves spatial derivatives — information from neighboring nodes. This is what sections 7.5–7.7 taught us to handle.

Step 3: Replace the Diffusion Operator with a Matrix Product

Every spatial discretization method does the same thing: it replaces the continuous diffusion operator at node $i$ with a weighted sum of voltages at node $i$ and its neighbors:

$$\bigl[\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{D}\,\nabla V_m)\bigr]_i \;\approx\; -\sum_{j=1}^{N} K_{ij}\,V_{m,j}$$

The minus sign is a convention: we define $\mathbf{K}$ so that it is positive semi-definite (the natural sign for a "stiffness" matrix). The entry $K_{ij}$ encodes how strongly node $j$ influences node $i$ through diffusion — this is exactly what we computed in the FDM stencil (Section 7.5), the FEM element assembly (Section 7.6), or the FVM flux balance (Section 7.7). If node $j$ is not a neighbor of node $i$, then $K_{ij} = 0$, which is why $\mathbf{K}$ is sparse.

In vector notation, gathering all $N$ node voltages into $\mathbf{V}_m = (V_{m,1}, V_{m,2}, \ldots, V_{m,N})^T$:

$$\text{Diffusion at all nodes} \;\approx\; -\mathbf{K}\,\mathbf{V}_m$$

Step 4: Handle the Time Derivative (the Mass Matrix)

For FDM and FVM, the time derivative is straightforward. At node $i$, the left-hand side of the PDE is simply $\chi C_m\,dV_{m,i}/dt$, and we can write $\chi C_m\,\dot{\mathbf{V}}_m$ for the whole vector. In matrix language, this is $\mathbf{M}\,\dot{\mathbf{V}}_m$ where $\mathbf{M}$ is a diagonal matrix with $M_{ii} = \chi C_m |\Omega_i|$ and $|\Omega_i|$ is the volume associated with node $i$ (for FDM on a uniform grid, $|\Omega_i| = h^d$, so $\mathbf{M}$ is proportional to the identity).

For FEM, the mass matrix arises naturally from the weak form (Section 7.6). The time-derivative integral $\int_\Omega \chi C_m (\partial V_m / \partial t)\,\varphi_i\,dx$ produces the entry $M_{ij} = \int_\Omega \chi C_m\,\varphi_i\,\varphi_j\,dx$. This is a consistent mass matrix — it is not diagonal because the hat functions of neighboring nodes overlap. In practice, we often lump it (replace each row with its row sum on the diagonal) so that $\mathbf{M}$ becomes diagonal and trivially invertible, just like FDM's.

In all cases, $\mathbf{M}$ has the physical meaning: it weights each node's time derivative by the "volume of tissue" that node represents.

Step 5: Gather the Ionic Current into a Load Vector

The ionic current and stimulus terms are purely local — they don't involve spatial derivatives. At node $i$, they contribute:

$$F_i(t) = -\chi\,|\Omega_i|\,I_{\text{ion}}(V_{m,i},\,\mathbf{w}_i) \;+\; |\Omega_i|\,I_{\text{stim},i}(t)$$

Here $I_{\text{ion}}(V_{m,i},\,\mathbf{w}_i)$ is the total ionic current at node $i$ — this is equation (5.1) for TTP06 (12 currents, 17 state variables) or equation (6.1) for ORd (15 currents, 40 state variables). The state vector $\mathbf{w}_i$ lives entirely at node $i$: none of its components (gates, concentrations, CaMKII states) diffuse. Collecting all $N$ entries gives the load vector $\mathbf{F}(t)$.

The weighting factor $|\Omega_i|$ is the "volume" associated with node $i$: for FDM on a uniform grid, $|\Omega_i| = h^d$; for lumped FEM, it equals the lumped mass diagonal entry divided by $\chi C_m$; for FVM, it is the control-volume size.

Step 6: Assemble the Semi-Discrete System

Now we put steps 3, 4, and 5 together. The continuous PDE at node $i$ was:

$$\underbrace{\chi C_m \frac{dV_{m,i}}{dt}}_{\text{mass} \times \text{time deriv}} \;=\; \underbrace{-\sum_j K_{ij} V_{m,j}}_{\text{diffusion}} \;+\; \underbrace{\bigl(-\chi\,I_{\text{ion},i} + I_{\text{stim},i}\bigr)|\Omega_i|^{-1}\cdot|\Omega_i|}_{\text{ionic + stimulus}}$$

Stacking all $N$ nodes into vectors and matrices:

(7.5) $$\boxed{\mathbf{M}\,\dot{\mathbf{V}}_m = -\mathbf{K}\,\mathbf{V}_m + \mathbf{F}(t)}$$

This is it — the semi-discrete monodomain system. "Semi-discrete" because space has been discretized (the matrices $\mathbf{M}$ and $\mathbf{K}$) but time is still continuous (the time derivative $\dot{\mathbf{V}}_m$). Every term has a clear origin:

TermOriginWhat it encodes
$\mathbf{M}$Time-derivative integral (PDE left-hand side)Volume weight per node; $\mathbf{I}$ for FDM, diagonal for lumped FEM/FVM
$\mathbf{K}$Diffusion operator $\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{D}\nabla V_m)$Spatial coupling: stencil (FDM), element assembly (FEM), flux balance (FVM)
$\mathbf{F}(t)$Ionic current + stimulus (PDE right-hand side)Local chemistry from TTP06/ORd at each node; no spatial derivatives

Equation (7.5) says exactly the same thing as the continuous PDE (7.1), but in a language a computer can execute: "at each node, the voltage rate of change equals the diffusive current arriving from neighbors (encoded by the matrix $-\mathbf{K}\mathbf{V}_m$) plus the ionic current produced locally (encoded by $\mathbf{F}$)." The matrices are just bookkeeping — a structured way to store the neighbor weights that sections 7.5–7.7 computed.

One Full Timestep: How It All Fits Together

To make this concrete, here is what happens during a single timestep $\Delta t$ using Godunov operator splitting (Chapter 8), FDM spatial discretization, and TTP06 ionic model, on a mesh with $N$ nodes:

Step 1 — Reaction (at every node independently): For each node $i = 1, \ldots, N$, evaluate TTP06's 12 currents from the current voltage $V_{m,i}^n$ and state variables $\mathbf{w}_i^n$:

$$I_{\text{ion},i} = I_{\text{Na},i} + I_{\text{CaL},i} + I_{\text{to},i} + \cdots \quad \text{(12 terms from eq. 5.1)}$$

Update the voltage: $V_{m,i}^* = V_{m,i}^n + \Delta t\,(-I_{\text{ion},i} / C_m)$. Update the 17 state variables using Rush–Larsen for gates and Forward Euler for concentrations: $\mathbf{w}_i^{n+1} = \text{advance}(\mathbf{w}_i^n, V_{m,i}^n, \Delta t)$. This step is embarrassingly parallel — each node is independent.

Step 2 — Diffusion (the matrix-vector product): Starting from the reaction-updated voltages $\mathbf{V}_m^*$, solve: $\mathbf{M}\,(\mathbf{V}_m^{n+1} - \mathbf{V}_m^*)/\Delta t = -\mathbf{K}\,\mathbf{V}_m^{n+1}$ (for implicit diffusion) or $\mathbf{V}_m^{n+1} = \mathbf{V}_m^* + (\Delta t / \chi C_m)\,(-\mathbf{K}\,\mathbf{V}_m^*)$ (for explicit diffusion with lumped mass). For FDM, this is just the stencil applied to each grid point — 5 multiplies and adds in 2D.

Advance to next timestep: Set $\mathbf{V}_m^n \leftarrow \mathbf{V}_m^{n+1}$ and repeat. On a mesh with 1 million nodes, this means evaluating TTP06 one million times per timestep (Step 1), then performing one sparse matrix-vector product of size $10^6$ (Step 2). With $\Delta t = 0.02$ ms and a simulation length of 500 ms, this is $25{,}000$ timesteps × $10^6$ ionic evaluations = $2.5 \times 10^{10}$ TTP06 calls. This is why efficient ionic model implementations and spatial discretizations matter.

This separation of spatial and temporal concerns is a deliberate design choice in Engine V5.4. All three discretizations (FDM, FEM, FVM) implement the SpatialDiscretization ABC, which exposes apply_diffusion(V) for explicit solvers and get_diffusion_operators(dt, scheme) for implicit solvers. You can switch from FDM to FEM without changing a single line of the solver code. The ionic model plugs in through the IonicModel interface — switching from TTP06 (18 ionic states + V) to ORd (40 ionic states + V) changes only the reaction step, not the diffusion step. Chapters 9 and 10 describe the time integrators; Chapter 14 shows how this framework extends to the bidomain's 2×2 block system.
