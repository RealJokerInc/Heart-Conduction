Chapter 5

The ten Tusscher–Panfilov 2006 Model

TTP06 is the workhorse model for human ventricular electrophysiology. With 18 state variables — 12 gating variables and 6 ionic concentrations — it captures every phase of the action potential we dissected in Chapter 3, plus the calcium cycling machinery that drives contraction.

5.1   18 State Variables: What and Why

A natural question when encountering TTP06 for the first time is: why 18 variables? The answer lies in the variety of currents and intracellular processes that shape the cardiac AP. Each current requires gates, and each ion species requires a concentration variable to track changes over multiple beats.

The 18 states fall into two natural groups:

5.2   The Complete Current Inventory

TTP06 models 12 distinct membrane currents plus 4 internal calcium fluxes. Every membrane current contributes to the total ionic current:

(5.1) $$I_{\text{ion}} = \underbrace{I_{\text{Na}} + I_{\text{CaL}}}_{\text{depolarizing}} + \underbrace{I_{\text{to}} + I_{\text{Kr}} + I_{\text{Ks}} + I_{\text{K1}} + I_{\text{pK}}}_{\text{repolarizing}} + \underbrace{I_{\text{NaK}} + I_{\text{NaCa}} + I_{\text{pCa}}}_{\text{pumps/exchangers}} + \underbrace{I_{\text{bNa}} + I_{\text{bCa}}}_{\text{background}}$$

This equation is the heart of the model: at every timestep, the simulator evaluates all 12 terms, sums them, and uses the total to update $V_m$. The sign convention follows physiology — positive current is outward (repolarizing), negative current is inward (depolarizing). The table below provides a complete reference, mapping each current to its AP phase, physiological role, and clinical significance.

5.3   Voltage-Gated Depolarizing Currents

Fast Sodium Current ($I_{\text{Na}}$) — Phase 0

The fast sodium current is responsible for the action potential upstroke and determines conduction velocity in tissue. It follows the classical HH formulation with three activation gates ($m$) and two inactivation gates (fast $h$ and slow $j$):

(5.2) $$I_{\text{Na}} = \bar{g}_{\text{Na}}\,m^3\,h\,j\,(V_m - E_{\text{Na}})$$

The maximal conductance $\bar{g}_{\text{Na}} = 14.838$ nS/pF is one of the largest in the model. The $m^3$ activation is ultrafast ($\tau_m \lt 1$ ms), providing the explosive upstroke. The dual inactivation ($h$ fast, $j$ slow) ensures that sodium channels close within a few milliseconds and remain closed throughout the plateau — creating the absolute refractory period. Physiologically, $I_{\text{Na}}$ determines the maximum rate of rise $dV/dt_{\text{max}} \approx 250{-}350$ V/s, which is a direct clinical measure of sodium channel availability. Class I antiarrhythmic drugs (lidocaine, flecainide) work by reducing $\bar{g}_{\text{Na}}$, slowing conduction.

L-Type Calcium Current ($I_{\text{CaL}}$) — Phase 2

The L-type calcium current sustains the plateau and triggers excitation-contraction coupling. Unlike $I_{\text{Na}}$, it uses the Goldman–Hodgkin–Katz (GHK) formulation rather than an Ohmic driving force, because Ca$^{2+}$ has a very low intracellular concentration and a 2+ valence:

(5.3) $$I_{\text{CaL}} = P_{\text{CaL}}\,d\,f\,f_2\,f_{\text{Ca}}\cdot 4\,\frac{F^2}{RT}\,(V_m - 15)\,\frac{0.25\,[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_{\text{ss}}\,e^{2F(V_m-15)/RT} - [\text{Ca}^{2+}]_o}{e^{2F(V_m-15)/RT}-1}$$

Note three features that distinguish this from a simple Ohmic current. First, TTP06 uses a permeability $P_{\text{CaL}} = 3.98 \times 10^{-5}$ cm/s rather than a conductance — the natural parameter for the GHK formulation. Second, the voltage is shifted by $-15$ mV ($V_m - 15$), reflecting the experimental observation that the current–voltage relationship of L-type channels peaks near $+15$ mV rather than $0$ mV. Third, the intracellular calcium carries an activity coefficient of 0.25, while the extracellular side uses bare $[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_o$ — this asymmetry accounts for the different ionic environments on each side. The four gates represent distinct biophysical mechanisms: $d$ is voltage activation ($\tau_d \sim 1{-}5$ ms), $f$ and $f_2$ are voltage-dependent inactivation (slow, $\tau \sim 20{-}300$ ms — this determines how long the plateau lasts), and $f_{\text{Ca}}$ is calcium-dependent inactivation (CDI), which responds to the local subspace calcium $[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_{\text{ss}}$. CDI is the primary negative feedback mechanism that limits calcium entry: as CICR raises subspace calcium, $f_{\text{Ca}}$ inactivates the channel, shutting off the trigger.

The GHK equation replaces the simple $(V_m - E_{\text{Ca}})$ driving force with a nonlinear function of voltage and concentrations on both sides. Why? For ions with large concentration gradients ($[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_o / [\text{Ca}^{2+}]_i \approx 20{,}000$) and multivalent charge, the Ohmic approximation badly overestimates current at large depolarizations. The GHK formula — derived from the Nernst–Planck electrodiffusion equation — accounts for the exponential concentration profile across the membrane and gives physically correct saturation behavior.

5.4   Voltage-Gated Repolarizing Currents

Transient Outward Current ($I_{\text{to}}$) — Phase 1

The transient outward K$^+$ current creates the Phase 1 notch. It activates rapidly during depolarization ($r$ gate, $\tau \sim 1{-}5$ ms) and inactivates within tens of milliseconds ($s$ gate, $\tau \sim 5{-}50$ ms):

(5.4) $$I_{\text{to}} = \bar{g}_{\text{to}}\,r\,s\,(V_m - E_{\text{K}})$$

The maximal conductance $\bar{g}_{\text{to}}$ varies dramatically between cell types: 0.294 nS/pF in epicardial cells (deep notch) vs. 0.073 nS/pF in endocardial cells (minimal notch). This transmural gradient is the single largest source of AP shape variation across the ventricular wall and produces the J-point deflection on the surface ECG. In Brugada syndrome, enhanced $I_{\text{to}}$ in right ventricular epicardium creates an exaggerated notch that can trigger reentrant arrhythmias.

Rapid Delayed Rectifier ($I_{\text{Kr}}$) — Phase 3

$I_{\text{Kr}}$ is the primary repolarization current, carried by hERG (Kv11.1) channels. It has unusual kinetics — fast inactivation at positive voltages, rapid recovery from inactivation during repolarization — that produce a characteristic "resurgent" current during Phase 3:

(5.5) $$I_{\text{Kr}} = \bar{g}_{\text{Kr}}\,\sqrt{[\text{K}^+]_o / 5.4}\,x_{r1}\,x_{r2}\,(V_m - E_{\text{K}})$$

The $\sqrt{[\text{K}^+]_o / 5.4}$ factor captures the experimental observation that hERG conductance scales with the square root of extracellular potassium. The two gating variables $x_{r1}$ (activation, $\tau \sim 10{-}100$ ms) and $x_{r2}$ (inactivation, fast) together create the voltage-dependent rectification that makes $I_{\text{Kr}}$ small during the plateau but large during Phase 3. Clinically, $I_{\text{Kr}}$ is the most important current in cardiac pharmacology: hERG block is the leading cause of drug-induced Long QT syndrome, and the CiPA initiative focuses heavily on predicting hERG interactions in silico.

Slow Delayed Rectifier ($I_{\text{Ks}}$) — Repolarization Reserve

$I_{\text{Ks}}$ activates very slowly ($\tau_{x_s} \sim 100{-}500$ ms) and provides a "safety net" when $I_{\text{Kr}}$ is compromised:

(5.6) $$I_{\text{Ks}} = \bar{g}_{\text{Ks}}\,x_s^2\,(V_m - E_{\text{Ks}})$$

Note that $I_{\text{Ks}}$ uses the potassium–sodium reversal potential $E_{\text{Ks}}$ (computed from the Goldman equation for K$^+$ and Na$^+$ permeability) rather than the pure potassium reversal $E_{\text{K}}$, because the KvLQT1/minK channel complex has slight Na$^+$ permeability. The $x_s^2$ activation gives the current a sigmoid-like voltage dependence with a slower foot than $I_{\text{Kr}}$. Under normal conditions, $I_{\text{Ks}}$ contributes modestly to repolarization. Its importance emerges when $I_{\text{Kr}}$ is blocked — then $I_{\text{Ks}}$ becomes the backup repolarization pathway. This is the concept of repolarization reserve: a healthy heart can tolerate partial block of one K$^+$ channel because others compensate. Loss of repolarization reserve (e.g., combined $I_{\text{Kr}}$ + $I_{\text{Ks}}$ reduction) dramatically increases arrhythmia risk.

Inward Rectifier ($I_{\text{K1}}$) — Phase 4 and Late Phase 3

(5.7) $$I_{\text{K1}} = \bar{g}_{\text{K1}}\,\sqrt{[\text{K}^+]_o / 5.4}\,x_{K1,\infty}(V_m)\,(V_m - E_{\text{K}})$$

$I_{\text{K1}}$ is unique among TTP06 currents: it has no dynamic gating variable. Its rectification is instantaneous, captured entirely by the steady-state function $x_{K1,\infty}(V_m)$, which transitions from $\sim$1 below $-80$ mV to $\sim$0 above $-40$ mV. Physiologically, this reflects the block of Kir2.1 channels by intracellular Mg$^{2+}$ and polyamines at depolarized voltages. The result is a current that behaves as a strong "anchor" at rest (holding $V_m$ near $E_K \approx -88$ mV), vanishes during the plateau (allowing the ICaL/IKr balance to play out), and then reactivates during Phase 3 to accelerate the final phase of repolarization.

Plateau Potassium Current ($I_{\text{pK}}$)

(5.8) $$I_{\text{pK}} = \bar{g}_{\text{pK}}\,\frac{1}{1 + e^{(25 - V_m)/5.98}}\,(V_m - E_{\text{K}})$$

$I_{\text{pK}}$ is a small outward K$^+$ current active during the plateau. It has no dynamic gates — the sigmoidal voltage dependence is instantaneous. Its contribution to total current is minor ($\bar{g}_{\text{pK}} = 0.0146$ nS/pF), but it participates in the repolarization reserve and affects APD at the margins.

5.5   Pumps, Exchangers, and Background Currents

The six voltage-gated currents above shape the AP waveform on a beat-to-beat basis. But the cell must also maintain its ionic gradients over thousands of beats. This is the job of the pumps, exchangers, and background leak currents — a group that is often overlooked but is essential for long-term stability.

Na$^+$/K$^+$ ATPase ($I_{\text{NaK}}$) — The Gradient Keeper

(5.9) $$I_{\text{NaK}} = \frac{P_{\text{NaK}}\,[\text{K}^+]_o\,[\text{Na}^+]_i}{([\text{K}^+]_o + K_{mK})([\text{Na}^+]_i + K_{mNa})}\cdot\frac{1}{1 + 0.1245\,e^{-0.1FV_m/RT} + 0.0353\,e^{-FV_m/RT}}$$

The Na$^+$/K$^+$-ATPase pumps 3 Na$^+$ ions out and 2 K$^+$ ions in per ATP hydrolyzed, generating a net outward current. This pump is what maintains the sodium and potassium gradients that all voltage-gated currents depend on. Without it, gradients would run down within minutes and the cell would depolarize. The current is voltage-dependent (less effective at positive voltages), saturates with intracellular Na$^+$ ($K_{mNa} = 40$ mM) and extracellular K$^+$ ($K_{mK} = 1$ mM), and is the target of cardiac glycosides (digoxin/digitalis): by blocking $I_{\text{NaK}}$, intracellular Na$^+$ rises, which reduces the driving force for the Na$^+$/Ca$^{2+}$ exchanger, indirectly increasing intracellular Ca$^{2+}$ and strengthening contraction (positive inotropy).

Na$^+$/Ca$^{2+}$ Exchanger ($I_{\text{NaCa}}$) — The Bidirectional Bridge

(5.10) $$I_{\text{NaCa}} = \frac{k_{\text{NaCa}}\bigl([\text{Na}^+]_i^3\,[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_o\,e^{\gamma FV_m/RT} - \alpha\,[\text{Na}^+]_o^3\,[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_i\,e^{(\gamma-1)FV_m/RT}\bigr)}{(K_{m,\text{Na}}^3 + [\text{Na}^+]_o^3)(K_{m,\text{Ca}} + [\text{Ca}^{2+}]_o)(1 + k_{\text{sat}}\,e^{(\gamma-1)FV_m/RT})}$$

The NCX exchanges 3 Na$^+$ for 1 Ca$^{2+}$ and can operate in either direction depending on the electrochemical gradients. The numerator contains two terms: the first (forward) drives Na$^+$ in and Ca$^{2+}$ out; the second (reverse, scaled by $\alpha = 2.5$) drives Na$^+$ out and Ca$^{2+}$ in. The factor $\alpha$ sets the relative strength of reverse-mode transport. The denominator has three components: the $K_m$ saturation terms for Na$^+$ and Ca$^{2+}$, and a voltage-dependent saturation factor $1 + k_{\text{sat}}\,e^{(\gamma-1)FV_m/RT}$ that prevents the exchanger from running at unphysically high rates at extreme potentials. In forward mode (dominant during late repolarization and diastole): Na$^+$ enters, Ca$^{2+}$ is extruded → net inward current. This is the primary mechanism for removing calcium from the cytoplasm after each beat (contributing $\sim$28% of calcium removal in human ventricular myocytes, with SERCA handling the remaining $\sim$70%). In reverse mode (brief, during early depolarization): Na$^+$ exits, Ca$^{2+}$ enters → net outward current. The voltage-dependent partition factor $\gamma \approx 0.35$ determines the relative influence of voltage on the forward vs. reverse rates.

$I_{\text{NaCa}}$ is critically important in disease. In heart failure, elevated $[\text{Na}^+]_i$ reduces forward-mode NCX activity, impairing calcium removal and contributing to diastolic dysfunction. In conditions of calcium overload, the inward current generated by forward-mode NCX during diastole can trigger delayed afterdepolarizations (DADs) — a major arrhythmia mechanism (Chapter 4, Section 4.5).

Sarcolemmal Ca$^{2+}$ Pump ($I_{\text{pCa}}$)

(5.11) $$I_{\text{pCa}} = \frac{\bar{g}_{\text{pCa}}\,[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_i}{K_{\text{pCa}} + [\text{Ca}^{2+}]_i}$$

The plasma membrane Ca$^{2+}$-ATPase (PMCA) provides a secondary route for calcium extrusion — slower than NCX but with higher affinity ($K_{\text{pCa}} = 0.0005$ mM). It contributes only $\sim$1–2% of total calcium removal per beat but is important for fine-tuning the diastolic calcium level over many beats.

Background Leak Currents ($I_{\text{bNa}}$, $I_{\text{bCa}}$)

(5.12) $$I_{\text{bNa}} = \bar{g}_{\text{bNa}}\,(V_m - E_{\text{Na}}), \qquad I_{\text{bCa}} = \bar{g}_{\text{bCa}}\,(V_m - E_{\text{Ca}})$$

These small Ohmic currents represent the passive leak of Na$^+$ and Ca$^{2+}$ through non-specific channels. Their conductances are tiny ($\bar{g}_{\text{bNa}} = 2.9 \times 10^{-4}$, $\bar{g}_{\text{bCa}} = 5.92 \times 10^{-4}$ nS/pF), but they serve a critical bookkeeping role: they provide the steady inward Na$^+$ and Ca$^{2+}$ leak that the pumps ($I_{\text{NaK}}$, $I_{\text{pCa}}$, NCX) must counterbalance. Without these background currents, the model would slowly drift to non-physiological concentrations over hundreds of beats.

The balance between background leak currents and pump/exchanger currents is what determines the long-term stability of ionic concentrations. If you modify pump parameters (e.g., reducing $I_{\text{NaK}}$ to simulate ischemia), you must simulate hundreds of beats to reach a new steady state — the first few beats will look normal, but gradual Na$^+$ accumulation will progressively alter the AP shape. This is why cardiac modelers must always run to steady state before collecting data.

All gating variables in TTP06 satisfy the same relaxation ODE we derived in equation (1.3): $dx/dt = (x_\infty - x)/\tau_x$. This uniformity is what makes the Rush–Larsen method (Chapter 8) so powerful: you can integrate every gate with the same exponential formula $x^{n+1} = x_\infty + (x^n - x_\infty)\,e^{-\Delta t/\tau_x}$, regardless of how stiff the kinetics are. The concentration variables ($[\text{Na}^+]_i$, $[\text{K}^+]_i$, etc.) do not follow relaxation kinetics and must be integrated with Forward Euler or a similar method.

5.7   Endo, Epi, and M-Cells: Transmural Heterogeneity

One of TTP06's most important features is its ability to represent three distinct cell types across the ventricular wall, each with a different AP morphology:

Epicardial cells (outer wall): Large $I_{\text{to}}$ → deep Phase 1 notch → shorter AP. Endocardial cells (inner wall): Small $I_{\text{to}}$ → minimal notch → moderate AP duration. M-cells (mid-wall): Small $I_{\text{Ks}}$ → very long plateau → longest AP duration.

These differences arise from changing just a few maximal conductance values ($\bar{g}_{\text{to}}$, $\bar{g}_{\text{Ks}}$) and some kinetic parameters. The fact that the same model structure, with minor parameter adjustments, reproduces three qualitatively different AP shapes is a testament to how well TTP06 captures the underlying biophysics.

In Engine V5.4, the TTP06 implementation lives in the cardiac_sim/ionic/ttp06/ package: model.py (the TTP06Model class), currents.py (all 12 current functions), gating.py (steady-states and time constants), calcium.py (concentration update equations), and parameters.py (the StateIndex enum and TTP06Parameters dataclass). V is stored separately; the 18 ionic states live in a tensor of shape (n_cells, 18). The cell_type parameter (CellType.ENDO, CellType.EPI, CellType.M_CELL) selects endo/epi/M-cell variants by switching maximal conductances and I$_{\text{to}}$ kinetics. A single tissue simulation can represent the full transmural gradient by assigning different cell types to different spatial regions — essential for realistic ECG simulation.

5.8   Calcium Handling and CICR

The calcium subsystem is perhaps the most physiologically important part of TTP06, because calcium is the link between electrical excitation and mechanical contraction. The model tracks calcium through three compartments connected by fluxes:

Figure 4.1. Calcium cycling in TTP06. The L-type current $I_{\text{CaL}}$ brings Ca$^{2+}$ into the subspace, triggering CICR from the SR ($J_{\text{rel}}$). Ca$^{2+}$ diffuses to the cytoplasm ($J_{\text{xfer}}$), drives contraction, then is pumped back into the SR by SERCA ($J_{\text{up}}$).

The CICR mechanism works like a trigger and amplifier:

Trigger: L-type calcium channels open during Phase 2, admitting a small amount of Ca$^{2+}$ into the subspace. This raises $[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_{\text{ss}}$.

Amplification: The elevated $[\text{Ca}^{2+}]_{\text{ss}}$ activates ryanodine receptors (RyR) on the SR membrane, which release a much larger bolus of Ca$^{2+}$ from the SR store into the subspace. This is the "release" flux $J_{\text{rel}}$.

Diffusion and contraction: Ca$^{2+}$ diffuses from the subspace to the bulk cytoplasm ($J_{\text{xfer}}$), where it binds to troponin C and triggers the contractile machinery of the sarcomere.

Recovery: The SERCA pump ($J_{\text{up}}$) actively transports Ca$^{2+}$ from the cytoplasm back into the SR, refilling the store for the next beat. A small leak ($J_{\text{leak}}$) allows some Ca$^{2+}$ to trickle back out.

Getting this calcium balance right is critical: if the SR is overloaded, spontaneous Ca$^{2+}$ release can trigger delayed afterdepolarizations (DADs) and arrhythmias. If the SR is depleted, contractile force drops and the AP shape distorts. The three-compartment model in TTP06 captures these dynamics with reasonable fidelity while remaining computationally tractable for tissue-scale simulations.
