Chapter 1

The Hodgkin–Huxley Revolution

In 1952, Alan Hodgkin and Andrew Huxley did something no one thought possible: they wrote down a mathematical model of the nerve impulse that was so precise it predicted experiments that hadn't been done yet. Every cardiac cell model used today — from the simplest to the most complex — is a direct descendant of their work.

1.1   The Membrane as a Circuit

A cell membrane is, at its core, a thin lipid bilayer — roughly 7 nm of oily molecules separating two conducting salt solutions. This structure has a remarkable electrical consequence: the membrane acts as a capacitor. Like two metal plates separated by an insulator, the membrane stores charge — positive ions lining up on one side, negative on the other.

But the membrane is not a perfect insulator. Embedded within it are ion channels — protein pores that allow specific ions (Na$^+$, K$^+$, Ca$^{2+}$, Cl$^-$) to flow through when open. Each channel type acts like a variable resistor: its conductance changes with voltage, time, and chemical signals. At any instant, the total current flowing across the membrane is the sum of the capacitive current (charging the membrane) and the ionic current (flowing through channels):

(1.1) $$I_m = C_m \frac{dV_m}{dt} + I_{\text{ion}}(V_m, t)$$

Here $C_m \approx 1\;\mu\text{F/cm}^2$ is the specific membrane capacitance (remarkably constant across all cell types), $V_m$ is the transmembrane potential, and $I_{\text{ion}}$ is the total ionic current — the quantity that Hodgkin and Huxley set out to understand.

Figure 1.1. The Hodgkin–Huxley equivalent circuit. The membrane capacitance $C_m$ sits in parallel with voltage-dependent conductances for Na$^+$, K$^+$, and a leak current. Each ionic branch has a variable conductance $g_x$ in series with a Nernst battery $E_x$.

The key insight of Figure 1.1 is that each ionic species contributes its own current $I_x = g_x(V_m - E_x)$, where $E_x$ is the reversal potential — the voltage at which the electrochemical gradient for that ion vanishes. For sodium, $E_{\text{Na}} \approx +65$ mV; for potassium, $E_{\text{K}} \approx -88$ mV. The enormous 150 mV difference between these two batteries is the energy source that powers every action potential.

Think of each ion channel as a door with a bouncer who checks the voltage. At rest ($V_m \approx -85$ mV), the Na$^+$ doors are nearly all shut and the K$^+$ doors are mostly open. The cell sits quietly at a voltage close to $E_{\text{K}}$. But if something pushes $V_m$ above about $-55$ mV, the Na$^+$ doors suddenly swing open — and the positive feedback loop that creates an action potential begins.

1.2   Gating Variables: m, h, and n

Hodgkin and Huxley's genius was not the circuit diagram — that was known. It was their decision to treat each conductance as a product of independent "gates," each opening and closing with its own voltage-dependent kinetics. For the squid axon, they found:

$$g_{\text{Na}} = \bar{g}_{\text{Na}} \cdot m^3 \cdot h, \qquad g_{\text{K}} = \bar{g}_{\text{K}} \cdot n^4$$

Here $\bar{g}_{\text{Na}}$ and $\bar{g}_{\text{K}}$ are the maximal conductances — what you'd measure if every channel were fully open. The gating variables $m$, $h$, and $n$ each range between 0 (all gates closed) and 1 (all gates open). They obey first-order kinetics:

(1.2) $$\frac{dx}{dt} = \alpha_x(V_m)(1 - x) - \beta_x(V_m)\,x, \qquad x \in \{m, h, n\}$$

where $\alpha_x$ and $\beta_x$ are voltage-dependent rate constants that Hodgkin and Huxley measured experimentally from the squid giant axon. This equation says something beautifully simple: each gate transitions between a closed state (population $1-x$) and an open state (population $x$), with forward rate $\alpha_x$ and backward rate $\beta_x$.

It is often more revealing to rewrite equation (1.2) in relaxation form:

(1.3) $$\frac{dx}{dt} = \frac{x_\infty(V_m) - x}{\tau_x(V_m)}$$

where $x_\infty = \alpha_x / (\alpha_x + \beta_x)$ is the steady-state value (where the gate would end up if voltage were held constant forever) and $\tau_x = 1/(\alpha_x + \beta_x)$ is the time constant (how fast it gets there). This form makes the physics transparent: the gate exponentially approaches its target $x_\infty(V_m)$ with time constant $\tau_x(V_m)$.

The relaxation form (1.3) is not just prettier notation — it is the foundation of the Rush–Larsen method, the standard numerical integrator for gating variables in cardiac simulations. We will meet it again in Chapter 8 when we discuss operator splitting.

Figure 1.2. Steady-state gating curves for the HH model. At rest, $m_\infty \approx 0$ (Na$^+$ activation off), $h_\infty \approx 0.6$ (Na$^+$ inactivation partially available), and $n_\infty \approx 0.3$ (K$^+$ partially open). The Na$^+$ activation curve $m_\infty$ is notably steeper than $n_\infty$, explaining why sodium channels open faster than potassium channels — the key to the action potential upstroke.

What do these three gates mean physically? The variable $m$ is the sodium activation gate: it controls how quickly Na$^+$ channels open when the membrane depolarizes. The variable $h$ is the sodium inactivation gate: it shuts Na$^+$ channels back down, even if the voltage stays high. The variable $n$ is the potassium activation gate: it opens K$^+$ channels slowly during depolarization to repolarize the cell.

The powers ($m^3$ and $n^4$) reflect the cooperativity of channel gating — Hodgkin and Huxley interpreted them as the number of independent "particles" that must simultaneously be in the right position for the channel to conduct. Modern structural biology has confirmed this: voltage-gated channels have four voltage-sensing domains, and the sodium channel's fast gate involves three of them.

1.3   The Sodium Hypothesis and Positive Feedback

The action potential is a self-amplifying event, and understanding why it fires requires tracing a feedback loop. Suppose a small stimulus pushes $V_m$ slightly above the threshold of about $-55$ mV. Here is what happens:

Step 1: The small depolarization causes $m_\infty$ to increase (the $m_\infty$ curve in Figure 1.2 is steeply rising near threshold). Gates start opening: $m$ rises toward $m_\infty$.

Step 2: As $m$ increases, the sodium conductance $g_{\text{Na}} = \bar{g}_{\text{Na}} m^3 h$ grows rapidly. Na$^+$ ions rush into the cell (driven by the large electrochemical gradient $V_m - E_{\text{Na}} \approx -55 - 65 = -120$ mV).

Step 3: The inward Na$^+$ current further depolarizes the membrane, which causes even more $m$-gates to open — and the process accelerates explosively.

This is positive feedback: depolarization opens Na$^+$ gates, which cause more depolarization, which opens more gates. It is the biological equivalent of a spark igniting gasoline. Once started, the upstroke is essentially unstoppable — $V_m$ shoots from $-85$ mV toward $E_{\text{Na}} \approx +65$ mV in less than 1 ms. This is the "all-or-nothing" nature of the action potential.

What stops this runaway? Two mechanisms, operating on different timescales:

Fast shutdown — inactivation ($h$): While $m$ opens in under 0.5 ms, the inactivation gate $h$ closes with a time constant of 1–5 ms. As $h \to 0$, the product $m^3 h \to 0$ even though $m$ is large. The Na$^+$ current self-terminates. This is the fundamental asymmetry: $m$ is fast, $h$ is slower.

Slow recovery — potassium ($n$): The K$^+$ gate $n$ opens even more slowly ($\tau_n \approx$ 5–10 ms). As $n^4$ grows, the outward K$^+$ current $I_K = \bar{g}_K n^4 (V_m - E_K)$ overwhelms the now-extinct Na$^+$ current and pulls $V_m$ back toward $E_K$.

1.4   Activation vs. Inactivation: The Refractory Period

After an action potential, the cell enters a refractory period during which it cannot fire again. This is essential for ordered wave propagation — without it, waves could travel backward and the heart would fibrillate. The refractory period has a clear gating-variable explanation:

Immediately after the action potential, $h \approx 0$ (Na$^+$ channels are inactivated) and $n$ is still elevated (K$^+$ channels are open). Even if a new stimulus arrives, there are no Na$^+$ channels available to open — the $h$ gate needs time to recover ($h$ must return toward $h_\infty(V_{\text{rest}}) \approx 0.6$). This recovery takes 5–10 ms in nerve, and 200–300 ms in ventricular cardiac cells.

Figure 1.3. The nerve action potential (squid axon). The upstroke is driven by Na$^+$ influx ($m^3h$ activating), the repolarization by Na$^+$ inactivation ($h \to 0$) and delayed K$^+$ activation ($n^4$ increasing). The refractory period corresponds to the time needed for $h$ to recover.

In Engine V5.4, every ionic model inherits from the IonicModel abstract base class (cardiac_sim/ionic/base.py), which requires: compute_Iion(V, ionic_states) to compute total ionic current, step(V, ionic_states, dt) to advance the full model, and separate methods compute_gate_steady_states() and compute_gate_time_constants() so that the Rush–Larsen solver can update gates externally. V is always stored separately from ionic states. The Hodgkin–Huxley structure — membrane current as sum of conductances times driving forces, gating variables following first-order kinetics — is exactly the pattern this interface encodes. Understanding HH means understanding the API contract of every model in the engine.

The complete Hodgkin–Huxley system is four coupled ordinary differential equations: one for $V_m$ (the circuit equation) and three for the gates ($m$, $h$, $n$). It was the first biophysically detailed cell model and remains the conceptual template for all that followed. But for the heart, we need something quite different — as we'll see in the next chapter.
