Appendix C

Numerical Analysis: The Bridge

Appendix A introduced differential equations — the continuous language of physics. Appendix B introduced linear algebra — the language of computation. This appendix connects them. The central question: how do you take a differential equation that lives in the continuous world and turn it into a matrix problem that a computer can solve? We will build one running example — a 2D grid with a hot spot — and solve it with every method from the simplest (Forward Euler) to the most sophisticated (Chebyshev iteration). By the end, every concept from Appendices A and B will have a job to do.

C.1   The Discretization Leap

In calculus, $u(x,y)$ is a smooth surface — a rule that assigns a number to every point in a region. A computer cannot store infinitely many values. So we make a choice: lay down a grid, sample the function at each grid point, and store only those values.

From film to frames. A movie camera captures a continuous scene by taking 24 frames per second. Each frame is a finite approximation of a continuous reality. Discretization is the same idea: a smooth function becomes a finite list of samples, and the spacing between samples determines what features you can resolve.

1D Warmup: The Three-Point Stencil

On a 1D rod with equally spaced points $x_j = j\,\Delta x$, the second derivative at point $j$ can be approximated by:

(C.1) $$\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}\bigg|_{x_j} \approx \frac{u_{j-1} - 2u_j + u_{j+1}}{\Delta x^2}$$

This is a weighted sum of three values — weights $[1, -2, 1]/\Delta x^2$. That weighted sum is one row of a matrix-vector product (Section B.3). Stacking all rows gives the discrete Laplacian $L$: a tridiagonal matrix where each row encodes the stencil at one grid point.

The Running Example: A 3×3 Grid

Now the real thing. Consider a square domain discretized into a $3 \times 3$ grid of points, with spacing $\Delta x = \Delta y = 1$. We number the 9 nodes using row-major ordering: $k = i + j \cdot 3$ where $i$ is the column index and $j$ is the row index.

Figure C.1. The 3×3 running example grid. Node numbering is row-major: $k = i + 3j$. The 5-point stencil at node 4 (center) connects to its four direct neighbors. Corner and edge nodes have fewer neighbors.

Building the 2D Laplacian Matrix

At the center node (node 4), the 5-point stencil approximates $\nabla^2 u$ using four neighbors:

$$\nabla^2 u\big|_4 \approx \frac{u_1 + u_3 + u_5 + u_7 - 4u_4}{\Delta x^2}$$

This is row 4 of the matrix $L$: coefficients $[0,\,1,\,0,\,1,\,-4,\,1,\,0,\,1,\,0]$.

At boundary nodes, the stencil has fewer neighbors. With Neumann (zero-flux) boundary conditions, we simply skip the missing neighbor — no ghost nodes needed. Node 0 (bottom-left corner) has only two neighbors (1 and 3):

$$\nabla^2 u\big|_0 \approx \frac{u_1 + u_3 - 2u_0}{\Delta x^2}$$

Row 0 of $L$: $[-2,\,1,\,0,\,1,\,0,\,0,\,0,\,0,\,0]$. Edge node 1 (bottom edge) has three neighbors:

$$\nabla^2 u\big|_1 \approx \frac{u_0 + u_2 + u_4 - 3u_1}{\Delta x^2}$$

Assembling all 9 rows (with $\Delta x = 1$) gives the complete $9 \times 9$ Laplacian:

$$L = \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 1 & . & 1 & . & . & . & . & . \\ 1 & -3 & 1 & . & 1 & . & . & . & . \\ . & 1 & -2 & . & . & 1 & . & . & . \\ 1 & . & . & -3 & 1 & . & 1 & . & . \\ . & 1 & . & 1 & -4 & 1 & . & 1 & . \\ . & . & 1 & . & 1 & -3 & . & . & 1 \\ . & . & . & 1 & . & . & -2 & 1 & . \\ . & . & . & . & 1 & . & 1 & -3 & 1 \\ . & . & . & . & . & 1 & . & 1 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Dots represent zeros. Check: every row sums to zero (Neumann BCs conserve total heat). The matrix is sparse (at most 5 nonzeros per row), symmetric (Section B.8), and negative semi-definite.

This is the moment Appendix A meets Appendix B. The differential operator $\nabla^2$ from calculus has become the matrix $L$ from linear algebra. The continuous heat equation $\partial u/\partial t = D\,\nabla^2 u$ becomes the matrix ODE:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{u}}{dt} = D\,L\,\mathbf{u}$$

A partial differential equation is now an ordinary differential equation whose right-hand side is a sparse matrix-vector product. Everything from here is about solving this system efficiently.

Mass matrices. The finite-difference derivation above gives $d\mathbf{u}/dt = DL\mathbf{u}$ directly. For finite-element methods (Chapter 8), the discretization produces $M\,d\mathbf{u}/dt = K\mathbf{u}$, where $M$ is a mass matrix (not the identity). This does not change the conceptual story but adds one more matrix to handle. Chapter 8 covers this in detail.

C.2   Truncation Error: The Price of Approximation

The three-point stencil (C.1) is an approximation. How good is it? Taylor-expand $u(x \pm \Delta x)$ around $x$ and add them:

$$\frac{u(x+\Delta x) - 2u(x) + u(x-\Delta x)}{\Delta x^2} = u''(x) + \frac{\Delta x^2}{12}\,u^{(4)}(x) + \cdots$$

The error is $O(\Delta x^2)$ — second order. Halving $\Delta x$ reduces the error by a factor of 4. This truncation error sets the spatial accuracy.

C.3   Forward Euler: The Simplest Method

We have the matrix ODE $d\mathbf{u}/dt = D\,L\,\mathbf{u}$. The simplest time-stepping approach: approximate the time derivative as a forward difference and evaluate the right-hand side at the current time:

(C.3) $$\mathbf{u}^{n+1} = \mathbf{u}^n + \Delta t\,D\,L\,\mathbf{u}^n = \underbrace{(I + \Delta t\,D\,L)}_{G}\,\mathbf{u}^n$$

This is just a sparse matrix-vector multiply (SpMV). No linear system to solve — given $\mathbf{u}^n$, one SpMV produces $\mathbf{u}^{n+1}$. Cost: $O(N)$ per time step. It does not get simpler than this.

Worked Example: Hot Spot on the 3×3 Grid

Set $D = 1$, $\Delta t = 0.05$. Place all the heat at the center node:

$$\mathbf{u}^0 = [0,\;0,\;0,\;0,\;9,\;0,\;0,\;0,\;0]^T$$

First, compute $L\,\mathbf{u}^0$. Most rows give zero because the only nonzero entry in $\mathbf{u}^0$ is at node 4. The rows that have a 1 in column 4 (nodes 1, 3, 5, 7) pick up that value:

$$L\,\mathbf{u}^0 = [0,\; 9,\; 0,\; 9,\; -36,\; 9,\; 0,\; 9,\; 0]^T$$

Now apply Forward Euler:

$$\mathbf{u}^1 = \mathbf{u}^0 + 0.05 \times L\,\mathbf{u}^0 = [\,0,\;\; 0.45,\;\; 0,\;\; 0.45,\;\; 7.20,\;\; 0.45,\;\; 0,\;\; 0.45,\;\; 0\,]^T$$
Figure C.2. One Forward Euler step. Heat flows from center (9 → 7.2) to the four edge-adjacent neighbors (0 → 0.45). Corners stay at zero — the 5-point stencil does not connect diagonally. Total heat is conserved.

Two things to notice. First, heat only spreads to the four edge-adjacent neighbors, not the four corners — this is because the 5-point stencil has no diagonal connections. Second, the total heat is conserved: $\sum u_j = 9$ before and after. This is guaranteed by the row-sum-zero property of $L$ under Neumann BCs.

C.4   Stability: When Forward Euler Blows Up

Forward Euler worked beautifully with $\Delta t = 0.05$. What happens if we try $\Delta t = 0.5$?

$$\mathbf{u}^1 = \mathbf{u}^0 + 0.5 \times L\,\mathbf{u}^0 = [\,0,\;\; 4.5,\;\; 0,\;\; 4.5,\;\; \mathbf{-9},\;\; 4.5,\;\; 0,\;\; 4.5,\;\; 0\,]^T$$

The center value is $-9$ — negative temperature. This is physically impossible and numerically catastrophic. The next step will amplify the error further, producing wilder oscillations until the solution explodes to infinity. This is numerical instability.

Why It Blows Up: Eigenvalue Analysis

Apply Forward Euler to a single eigenmode. If $\mathbf{v}$ is an eigenvector of $L$ with eigenvalue $\lambda$ (so $L\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$), then:

$$G\mathbf{v} = (I + \Delta t\,D\,L)\,\mathbf{v} = (1 + \Delta t\,D\,\lambda)\,\mathbf{v}$$

The amplification factor is $g = 1 + \Delta t\,D\,\lambda$. For stability, we need $|g| \leq 1$ for every eigenvalue. Since $\lambda < 0$ for the Laplacian:

(C.4) $$\Delta t \leq \frac{2}{D\,|\lambda_{\max}|}$$

This is the CFL condition. For the 2D discrete Laplacian with Neumann BCs, the eigenvalues are:

(C.5) $$\lambda_{p,q} = \frac{2}{\Delta x^2}\!\left(\cos\!\frac{\pi p}{N_x} - 1\right) + \frac{2}{\Delta y^2}\!\left(\cos\!\frac{\pi q}{N_y} - 1\right)$$

For our $3 \times 3$ grid ($N_x = N_y = 3$, $\Delta x = \Delta y = 1$), the most negative eigenvalue is $\lambda_{2,2} = -6$, giving:

$$\Delta t \leq \frac{2}{1 \times 6} = \frac{1}{3} \approx 0.333$$

Our stable step ($\Delta t = 0.05$) is well within the limit. The unstable step ($\Delta t = 0.5$) violates it. In general, the CFL condition for Forward Euler on a 2D grid is:

$$\Delta t \leq \frac{\Delta x^2}{4D} \qquad \text{(2D, square grid)}$$

The quadratic dependence on $\Delta x$ is brutal: halving the grid spacing requires quartering the time step.

Stability Regions

For a general time-stepper, the stability region is the set of complex values $z = \Delta t\,\lambda$ for which $|g(z)| \leq 1$:

  • Forward Euler: A disk of radius 1 centered at $z = -1$. Limited.
  • RK4: A larger region extending further along the negative real axis. Allows $\sim 2.8\times$ larger $\Delta t$.
  • Backward Euler: Everything outside a disk of radius 1 centered at $z = +1$ — includes the entire left half-plane. Unconditionally stable.
  • Crank-Nicolson: The entire left half-plane, but with $|g| = 1$ on the imaginary axis.
Figure C.3. Stability regions in the complex $z = \Delta t\lambda$ plane. All eigenvalues of the Laplacian (black dots) must fall inside the stability region. Backward Euler contains the entire left half-plane, so it is unconditionally stable.

Explicit methods put a speed limit on $\Delta t$; implicit methods remove it. But implicit methods pay a price: instead of a matrix-vector multiply, they require solving a linear system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ at each step. This is the central engineering trade-off of time-stepping.

C.5   Backward Euler: Unconditional Stability

Forward Euler evaluates the right-hand side at the current time. What if we evaluate it at the future time instead?

(C.6) $$\mathbf{u}^{n+1} = \mathbf{u}^n + \Delta t\,D\,L\,\mathbf{u}^{n+1}$$

The unknown $\mathbf{u}^{n+1}$ appears on both sides. Rearranging:

$$\underbrace{(I - \Delta t\,D\,L)}_{A}\;\mathbf{u}^{n+1} = \mathbf{u}^n$$

This is $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ — a linear system. The unknown is $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{u}^{n+1}$, the right-hand side is $\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{u}^n$. We must solve this system at every time step.

This is the climactic moment. The PDE from Appendix A has become a linear system from Appendix B. The matrix $A = I - \Delta t\,D\,L$ inherits structure from the Laplacian: it is sparse (Section B.4), symmetric (Section B.8), and positive definite (because $L$ is negative semi-definite and $I$ pushes all eigenvalues above zero). It is SPD — the gold standard from Section B.8.

Worked Example: Same Grid, Same Hot Spot

With $D = 1$, $\Delta t = 0.05$, the matrix $A = I - 0.05\,L$ for our $3 \times 3$ grid is:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1.1 & -0.05 & . & -0.05 & . & . & . & . & . \\ -0.05 & 1.15 & -0.05 & . & -0.05 & . & . & . & . \\ . & -0.05 & 1.1 & . & . & -0.05 & . & . & . \\ -0.05 & . & . & 1.15 & -0.05 & . & -0.05 & . & . \\ . & -0.05 & . & -0.05 & 1.2 & -0.05 & . & -0.05 & . \\ . & . & -0.05 & . & -0.05 & 1.15 & . & . & -0.05 \\ . & . & . & -0.05 & . & . & 1.1 & -0.05 & . \\ . & . & . & . & -0.05 & . & -0.05 & 1.15 & -0.05 \\ . & . & . & . & . & -0.05 & . & -0.05 & 1.1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Solving $A\,\mathbf{u}^1 = \mathbf{u}^0$ (with $\mathbf{u}^0 = [0,0,0,0,9,0,0,0,0]^T$) gives:

$$\mathbf{u}^1 \approx [\,0.016,\;\; 0.405,\;\; 0.016,\;\; 0.405,\;\; 7.316,\;\; 0.405,\;\; 0.016,\;\; 0.405,\;\; 0.016\,]^T$$

Compare with Forward Euler: the center value is 7.32 vs. 7.20, and the corners now receive a tiny amount of heat (0.016) through the implicit coupling. The result is similar but computed fundamentally differently — by solving a system rather than multiplying a matrix. The payoff: Backward Euler is unconditionally stable. We could use $\Delta t = 0.5$ or $\Delta t = 100$ without blowup.

C.6   Crank-Nicolson: Second-Order Accuracy

Forward Euler (first-order, explicit) and Backward Euler (first-order, implicit) sit at opposite ends of a tradeoff. Can we get the best of both? Crank-Nicolson averages them:

(C.7) $$\left(I - \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\,D\,L\right)\mathbf{u}^{n+1} = \left(I + \tfrac{\Delta t}{2}\,D\,L\right)\mathbf{u}^n$$

Still $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, but now both sides involve matrix operations. The left-hand matrix is SPD; the right-hand side requires one additional SpMV. The reward: second-order accuracy in time while remaining unconditionally stable.

C.7   The Lax Equivalence Theorem

We have now seen three methods. Before adding more, a powerful theorem ties the theory together. Every numerical scheme must have three properties:

  • Consistency: As $\Delta x, \Delta t \to 0$, the discrete scheme approaches the continuous PDE. (Truncation error vanishes.)
  • Stability: Errors do not grow unboundedly. (Amplification factor controlled.)
  • Convergence: The numerical solution approaches the true solution.

Lax Equivalence Theorem (1956): For a consistent linear scheme, stability is equivalent to convergence.

Verify consistency (check truncation error, Section C.2) and stability (check CFL, Section C.4), and convergence is guaranteed. This is the theoretical foundation for Chapters 10 and 11.

C.8   Stiffness and Operator Splitting

A system is stiff when it contains widely separated time scales — some components change very fast, others very slowly. Cardiac electrophysiology is inherently stiff. During the upstroke, sodium channels open in $\sim$1 ms, changing voltage by 100 mV. During the plateau, voltage changes by a few mV over 200 ms.

Stiffness is "rush hour traffic." A highway where some cars travel at 200 km/h and others at 2 km/h. A simulation using one $\Delta t$ for both is like setting the speed limit for the fastest car — the slow ones barely move per step. Operator splitting is separate lanes: treat the fast dynamics (ionic currents) with a small step, and the slow dynamics (diffusion) with a larger step.

The Idea: Divide and Conquer

Instead of solving $\partial u/\partial t = \mathcal{D}(u) + \mathcal{R}(u)$ (diffusion + reaction) all at once, split each time step into sub-problems:

Godunov (Lie) splitting — first order:

1.   Reaction: Solve $\partial u/\partial t = \mathcal{R}(u)$ for one full step $\Delta t$ → gives $u^*$
2.   Diffusion: Solve $\partial u/\partial t = \mathcal{D}(u)$ for one full step $\Delta t$, starting from $u^*$ → gives $u^{n+1}$

Strang splitting — second order:

1.   Half diffusion: Solve $\partial u/\partial t = \mathcal{D}(u)$ for $\Delta t/2$ → gives $u^*$
2.   Full reaction: Solve $\partial u/\partial t = \mathcal{R}(u)$ for $\Delta t$, starting from $u^*$ → gives $u^{**}$
3.   Half diffusion: Solve $\partial u/\partial t = \mathcal{D}(u)$ for $\Delta t/2$, starting from $u^{**}$ → gives $u^{n+1}$

Why This Is Powerful

The reaction sub-problem $\mathcal{R}(u)$ is a system of ODEs at each grid point independently — no spatial coupling, embarrassingly parallel. On our 3×3 grid, this means solving 9 independent ODE systems (one per node), each handling its own ion channel dynamics. Use Rush-Larsen or explicit methods (Chapter 9).

The diffusion sub-problem $\mathcal{D}(u)$ is a linear PDE with no reaction stiffness. It produces exactly the $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ systems from Sections C.5–C.6. The matrix $A$ is SPD, sparse, and the same at every step — we can precompute and reuse.

Each sub-problem is much easier than the combined system. Chapter 9 develops operator splitting in full detail for cardiac simulation.

C.10   Transform Methods: Spectral Solvers

Imagine listening to an orchestra — violins, cellos, flutes all at once. Your ear receives a single pressure wave, but your brain separates it into individual pitches. A transform does the same to a vector: re-expresses it in a new basis where the problem becomes simpler.

The discrete Laplacian has known eigenvectors — sines and cosines. In the eigenvector basis, the matrix becomes diagonal (Section B.7). The system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ becomes $N$ independent scalar divisions. No iteration, no factorization — just divide.

Choosing the Right Basis

Cosines → Neumann BCs. The functions $\cos(k\pi x/L)$ have zero derivative at both endpoints — they automatically satisfy the zero-flux boundary condition. The Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) decomposes a vector into these modes.

Sines → Dirichlet BCs. The functions $\sin(k\pi x/L)$ are zero at both endpoints — they automatically satisfy fixed-value boundary conditions. The Discrete Sine Transform (DST) decomposes a vector into these modes.

The basis function is the boundary condition. Cosines have flat endpoints (zero slope) → Neumann. Sines pass through zero → Dirichlet. No ghost nodes, no special boundary treatment.

Eigenvalues

The 1D Neumann eigenvalues come from equation (C.5) with one dimension. For Dirichlet:

(C.8) $$\lambda_k^{\text{DST}} = \frac{2}{\Delta x^2}\left(\cos\!\frac{\pi k}{N+1} - 1\right), \qquad k = 1, \ldots, N$$

The Neumann eigenvalues include $\lambda_0 = 0$ (the null space — a constant is unchanged by the Laplacian). The Dirichlet eigenvalues have no zero eigenvalue — the boundary condition pins the solution. In 2D, eigenvalues are sums of 1D eigenvalues:

(C.9) $$\lambda_{p,q} = \lambda_p^{(x)} + \lambda_q^{(y)}$$

The Algorithm

To solve $(I - \Delta t\,D\,L)\,\mathbf{u}^{n+1} = \mathbf{u}^n$:

  1. Forward transform: Decompose $\mathbf{u}^n$ into spectral coefficients $\hat{u}_k$ via DCT or DST.
  2. Divide: $\hat{u}_k^{n+1} = \hat{u}_k^n \;/\; (1 - \Delta t\,D\,\lambda_k)$.
  3. Inverse transform: Recover $\mathbf{u}^{n+1}$ from $\hat{u}_k^{n+1}$.

The transforms cost $O(N \log N)$ via FFT. The division is $O(N)$. Total: $O(N \log N)$ per time step — faster than any iterative solver. In 2D, apply the 1D transform along each axis separately (the transforms are separable).

C.11   Introduction to Iterative Methods

When spectral methods cannot be used — irregular geometry, anisotropic conductivity, mixed boundary conditions — we need a general-purpose approach. Iterative methods start from an initial guess $\mathbf{x}_0$ and improve it step by step:

The iterative loop:
1.   Compute residual: $\mathbf{r}_k = \mathbf{b} - A\mathbf{x}_k$
2.   If $\|\mathbf{r}_k\|/\|\mathbf{b}\| < \varepsilon$: stop (converged)
3.   Compute an update direction $\mathbf{p}_k$ and step size $\alpha_k$
4.   $\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k + \alpha_k\,\mathbf{p}_k$
5.   Go to 1

Each iteration costs $O(N)$ — one SpMV to compute $A\mathbf{p}$. The methods differ in how they choose the direction $\mathbf{p}_k$. A good choice converges in few iterations; a bad one zigzags forever.

Warm-starting is free lunch. In time-stepping, we solve $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ at every step, and the solution changes only slightly between steps. Using the previous step's solution as $\mathbf{x}_0$ starts us close to the answer — convergence in 3–8 iterations instead of 20–50.

The three iterative methods below form a progression: CG is the foundation, PCG adds preconditioning for faster convergence, and Chebyshev removes global communication for GPU scalability.

The Running Example for Iterative Methods

For Sections C.12–C.14, we switch to a 4-point 1D rod ($\Delta x = 1$, $D = 1$, Neumann BCs) so we can trace every number by hand. The Backward Euler system with $\Delta t = 0.1$ is:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1.1 & -0.1 & 0 & 0 \\ -0.1 & 1.2 & -0.1 & 0 \\ 0 & -0.1 & 1.2 & -0.1 \\ 0 & 0 & -0.1 & 1.1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 2 \\ 1 \\ 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

$A$ is SPD and tridiagonal. The exact solution is $\mathbf{x}^* \approx [3.82,\; 2.08,\; 1.28,\; 2.82]^T$.

C.12   Conjugate Gradient (CG)

Imagine you are blindfolded in a hilly landscape, trying to find the lowest valley. You can feel the slope beneath your feet. The obvious strategy: always walk downhill. This is gradient descent. But in a long, narrow valley (an ill-conditioned problem — Section B.9), gradient descent zigzags back and forth, making painfully slow progress.

The conjugate gradient method fixes this with a geometric idea from Section B.12: choose search directions that are $A$-conjugate (orthogonal with respect to the bowl shape defined by $A$). Each conjugate direction eliminates error along one dimension permanently, guaranteeing convergence in at most $N$ steps.

The Energy Minimization Viewpoint

When $A$ is SPD, solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is equivalent to minimizing:

(C.10) $$E(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^T A\,\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{b}^T\mathbf{x}$$

The gradient is $\nabla E = A\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{b} = -\mathbf{r}$, where $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{b} - A\mathbf{x}$ is the residual. CG builds conjugate search directions cheaply — each new direction needs only the current residual and the previous direction.

The CG Algorithm

Algorithm C.1: Conjugate Gradient

Input: SPD matrix $A$, RHS $\mathbf{b}$, initial guess $\mathbf{x}_0$, tolerance $\varepsilon$

1.   $\mathbf{r}_0 = \mathbf{b} - A\mathbf{x}_0$,   $\mathbf{p}_0 = \mathbf{r}_0$
2.   for $k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$
3.    $\alpha_k = \dfrac{\mathbf{r}_k^T \mathbf{r}_k}{\mathbf{p}_k^T A\mathbf{p}_k}$   (step size)

4.    $\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k + \alpha_k\,\mathbf{p}_k$   (update solution)
5.    $\mathbf{r}_{k+1} = \mathbf{r}_k - \alpha_k\,A\mathbf{p}_k$   (update residual)
6.    if $\|\mathbf{r}_{k+1}\|/\|\mathbf{b}\| < \varepsilon$: stop
7.    $\beta_k = \dfrac{\mathbf{r}_{k+1}^T \mathbf{r}_{k+1}}{\mathbf{r}_k^T \mathbf{r}_k}$   (conjugacy weight)

8.    $\mathbf{p}_{k+1} = \mathbf{r}_{k+1} + \beta_k\,\mathbf{p}_k$   (new search direction)
9.   end for

Each iteration: one SpMV ($A\mathbf{p}_k$), two inner products ($\mathbf{r}^T\mathbf{r}$ and $\mathbf{p}^T A\mathbf{p}$), and a few vector updates. Cost per iteration: $O(N)$.

Worked Example: CG on the 4-Point Rod

Start with $\mathbf{x}_0 = [0,\,0,\,0,\,0]^T$ (cold start).

Iteration 1. $\mathbf{r}_0 = \mathbf{b} = [4,\,2,\,1,\,3]^T$, $\mathbf{p}_0 = \mathbf{r}_0$.

$$A\mathbf{p}_0 = [4.2,\; 1.9,\; 0.7,\; 3.2]^T$$ $$\alpha_0 = \frac{\mathbf{r}_0^T\mathbf{r}_0}{\mathbf{p}_0^T A\mathbf{p}_0} = \frac{30}{30.9} = 0.971$$ $$\mathbf{x}_1 = 0.971 \times [4,\,2,\,1,\,3]^T = [3.88,\; 1.94,\; 0.97,\; 2.91]^T$$ $$\mathbf{r}_1 = \mathbf{r}_0 - 0.971 \times A\mathbf{p}_0 = [-0.08,\; 0.16,\; 0.32,\; -0.11]^T$$

Iteration 2. $\beta_0 = \|\mathbf{r}_1\|^2/\|\mathbf{r}_0\|^2 = 0.144/30 = 0.0048$.

Convergence Rate

The error after $k$ iterations satisfies:

(C.11) $$\|\mathbf{x}_k - \mathbf{x}^*\|_A \leq 2\left(\frac{\sqrt{\kappa} - 1}{\sqrt{\kappa} + 1}\right)^k \|\mathbf{x}_0 - \mathbf{x}^*\|_A$$

where $\kappa = \lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$ is the condition number (Section B.9). CG needs $O(\sqrt{\kappa})$ iterations. For the Laplacian, $\kappa \propto N$, so unpreconditioned CG needs $O(\sqrt{N})$ iterations — fine for small problems, too many for $N = 90{,}000$.

CG requires $A$ to be SPD. If $A$ is symmetric but indefinite (both positive and negative eigenvalues), CG breaks down. The full bidomain block system (Chapter 13) is symmetric indefinite — use MINRES or GMRES. But the decoupled sub-problems (Chapter 14) are each SPD, so CG works on them individually.

C.13   Preconditioned Conjugate Gradient (PCG)

CG converges in $O(\sqrt{\kappa})$ iterations. For a fine cardiac mesh with $N = 90{,}000$ nodes, $\kappa \propto N$, so CG needs $\sim 300$ iterations — expensive. The solution: preconditioning.

The Idea

A preconditioner $M$ is a matrix that approximates $A$ but is cheap to invert. Instead of solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, we solve $M^{-1}A\mathbf{x} = M^{-1}\mathbf{b}$. If $M \approx A$, then $M^{-1}A \approx I$, so $\kappa(M^{-1}A) \approx 1$ — a round bowl instead of a bathtub. CG on the preconditioned system converges in far fewer iterations.

Preconditioning reshapes the bowl. Recall from Section B.9: an ill-conditioned system looks like an elongated bathtub — gradient descent zigzags across the narrow valley. The preconditioner stretches the bathtub back toward a round bowl, making CG converge in a handful of iterations instead of hundreds.

Jacobi Preconditioning

The simplest preconditioner: $M = \text{diag}(A)$. One line of code, $O(N)$ to apply, and it is often enough to halve the iteration count. For our 4-point rod:

$$M^{-1} = \text{diag}\!\left(\frac{1}{1.1},\; \frac{1}{1.2},\; \frac{1}{1.2},\; \frac{1}{1.1}\right) = \text{diag}(0.909,\; 0.833,\; 0.833,\; 0.909)$$

The PCG Algorithm

Algorithm C.2: Preconditioned Conjugate Gradient

Input: SPD matrix $A$, RHS $\mathbf{b}$, initial guess $\mathbf{x}_0$, preconditioner $M$, tolerance $\varepsilon$

1.   $\mathbf{r}_0 = \mathbf{b} - A\mathbf{x}_0$,   $\mathbf{z}_0 = M^{-1}\mathbf{r}_0$,   $\mathbf{p}_0 = \mathbf{z}_0$
2.   for $k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$
3.    $\alpha_k = \dfrac{\mathbf{r}_k^T\mathbf{z}_k}{\mathbf{p}_k^T A\mathbf{p}_k}$

4.    $\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k + \alpha_k\,\mathbf{p}_k$
5.    $\mathbf{r}_{k+1} = \mathbf{r}_k - \alpha_k\,A\mathbf{p}_k$
6.    if $\|\mathbf{r}_{k+1}\|/\|\mathbf{b}\| < \varepsilon$: stop
7.    $\mathbf{z}_{k+1} = M^{-1}\mathbf{r}_{k+1}$   (apply preconditioner)
8.    $\beta_k = \dfrac{\mathbf{r}_{k+1}^T\mathbf{z}_{k+1}}{\mathbf{r}_k^T\mathbf{z}_k}$

9.    $\mathbf{p}_{k+1} = \mathbf{z}_{k+1} + \beta_k\,\mathbf{p}_k$
10.   end for

The only difference from CG (Algorithm C.1) is: (a) the preconditioner solve $\mathbf{z} = M^{-1}\mathbf{r}$ at line 7, and (b) the inner products use $\mathbf{z}$ instead of $\mathbf{r}$ for the direction computation. When $M = I$, PCG reduces to CG exactly.

Cost vs. Benefit

Each PCG iteration costs slightly more than CG (one extra $M^{-1}$ application). But the iteration count drops:

The payoff appears at scale: on a 90,000-node cardiac mesh, Jacobi preconditioning typically cuts iterations from $O(\sqrt{N})$ to $O(\sqrt{N}/c)$ with $c \approx 3\text{–}5$. More sophisticated preconditioners (incomplete Cholesky, AMG) reduce further.

C.14   Chebyshev Iteration

CG and PCG have a hidden cost: two inner products per iteration (steps 3 and 7/8 in Algorithms C.1/C.2). An inner product $\mathbf{r}^T\mathbf{z}$ requires summing $N$ products across all nodes — a global synchronization where every processor must wait for every other processor before continuing. On a GPU with thousands of cores, these synchronization points become the bottleneck.

The GPU bottleneck. A SpMV is embarrassingly parallel — each row of $A$ can be processed independently. But an inner product requires collecting results from all rows into a single number. On a GPU, this "reduce" operation stalls thousands of cores waiting for one sum. Two reduces per iteration × thousands of time steps = significant overhead.

Chebyshev's Deal

Chebyshev iteration offers a trade: give me the eigenvalue bounds $\lambda_{\min}$ and $\lambda_{\max}$ of $A$, and I will converge at the same rate as CG with zero inner products. The update uses only local operations — SpMV, vector additions, and scalar multiplications:

(C.12) $$\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \omega_{k+1}\!\left(\frac{2}{\gamma}\,M^{-1}\mathbf{r}_k + \mathbf{x}_k - \mathbf{x}_{k-1}\right) + \mathbf{x}_{k-1}$$

where $\gamma = (\lambda_{\max} + \lambda_{\min})/2$ is the spectral center and $\omega_k$ are Chebyshev-derived weights computed from a simple recurrence:

$$\omega_1 = 1, \qquad \omega_2 = \frac{2}{2 - \rho^2}, \qquad \omega_{k+1} = \frac{1}{1 - \rho^2\omega_k/4}$$

with $\rho = (\lambda_{\max} - \lambda_{\min})/(\lambda_{\max} + \lambda_{\min})$.

The Three-Term Recurrence

Chebyshev iteration is a three-term recurrence: each update uses $\mathbf{x}_k$ and $\mathbf{x}_{k-1}$ (the current and previous iterates). This is what enables zero inner products — the algorithm looks ahead using its knowledge of the eigenvalue bounds rather than adapting to the residual at each step.

Algorithm C.3: Preconditioned Chebyshev Iteration

Input: SPD matrix $A$, RHS $\mathbf{b}$, initial guess $\mathbf{x}_0$, preconditioner $M$, eigenvalue bounds $\lambda_{\min}, \lambda_{\max}$, iterations $K$
Setup: $\gamma = (\lambda_{\max} + \lambda_{\min})/2$,   $\delta = (\lambda_{\max} - \lambda_{\min})/2$

1.   $\mathbf{r}_0 = \mathbf{b} - A\mathbf{x}_0$,   $\mathbf{x}_1 = \mathbf{x}_0 + (1/\gamma)\,M^{-1}\mathbf{r}_0$,   $\omega_1 = 1$
2.   for $k = 1, 2, \ldots, K-1$
3.    $\omega_{k+1} = 1\,/\,(1 - \delta^2\omega_k\,/\,(2\gamma^2))$
4.    $\mathbf{r}_k = \mathbf{b} - A\mathbf{x}_k$
5.    $\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \omega_{k+1}\!\left(\tfrac{2}{\gamma}\,M^{-1}\mathbf{r}_k + \mathbf{x}_k - \mathbf{x}_{k-1}\right) + \mathbf{x}_{k-1}$
6.   end for

Every operation is local — no inner products, no global synchronization. This makes Chebyshev iteration ideal for GPU execution.

The Practical Workflow: CG Then Chebyshev

Chebyshev needs eigenvalue bounds, but CG discovers them automatically. The standard approach:

  1. First few time steps: Use PCG. Track the extreme eigenvalue estimates from the CG recurrence coefficients (the Lanczos connection).
  2. Remaining time steps: Switch to Chebyshev using the estimated bounds. Since the matrix $A$ is the same at every step (it depends only on $\Delta t$, $D$, and the mesh), the bounds computed once are valid forever.
