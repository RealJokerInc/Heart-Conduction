Appendix B

Linear Algebra

This appendix builds the linear algebra you need from the ground up, following the philosophy of 3Blue1Brown: start with pictures, develop geometric intuition, and let the formalism emerge naturally. Everything here is abstract — we work with vectors, matrices, and transformations as mathematical objects, independent of any particular application. The connection to differential equations and cardiac simulation is the subject of Appendix C.

B.1   Vectors and Spaces

A vector is an ordered list of numbers — and simultaneously an arrow pointing from the origin to a location in space. In low dimensions, think of the arrow (the 3Blue1Brown viewpoint). In high dimensions ($n = 90{,}000$), the arrow is unvisualizable but the list of numbers works just fine. Both pictures are correct; they emphasize different things:

(B.1) $$\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ \vdots \\ v_n \end{pmatrix}$$

The number of entries is the dimension. A 2-vector lives in $\mathbb{R}^2$ (the plane), a 3-vector in $\mathbb{R}^3$ (3D space), and an $n$-vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$. When $n = 90{,}000$, you cannot visualize the space, but the algebra works identically to $\mathbb{R}^2$.

Two operations define the algebra of vectors:

  • Addition: Add corresponding entries. $\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}$ has entries $u_i + v_i$.
  • Scalar multiplication: Multiply every entry by a number. $c\,\mathbf{v}$ has entries $c \cdot v_i$.

Any combination $c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_k\mathbf{v}_k$ is called a linear combination. The set of all linear combinations of a collection of vectors is their span — the "space" they can reach.

Why "linear"? These two operations — addition and scaling — are the only ones allowed. No squaring entries, no taking square roots, no multiplying entries together. This restriction seems limiting, but it is what makes the entire theory tractable. Linearity means superposition: if $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are solutions, so is $c_1\mathbf{u} + c_2\mathbf{v}$. This single property drives everything in this appendix.

The inner product (dot product) of two vectors measures how aligned they are:

$$\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{v} = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2 + \cdots + u_n v_n$$

The norm (length) of a vector is $\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{\mathbf{v}^T\mathbf{v}}$. A vector with $\|\mathbf{v}\| = 1$ is called a unit vector.

B.2   Matrices as Transformations

A matrix is not just a grid of numbers — it is a machine. You feed it a vector, and it produces a new vector. In the language of 3Blue1Brown: a matrix is a transformation that takes every point in space and moves it to a new location, in a way that keeps straight lines straight and the origin fixed.

Consider a $2 \times 2$ matrix acting on vectors in the plane:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

This matrix stretches every vector by a factor of 2 in the $x$-direction and leaves the $y$-direction unchanged. The unit square becomes a rectangle twice as wide. The matrix is the stretch — it encodes the transformation completely.

Now consider a rotation by $90°$:

$$R = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Every vector gets rotated counterclockwise by a quarter turn. The transformation is encoded in the columns of $R$: the first column $[0,\,1]^T$ tells you where the $x$-axis unit vector lands, and the second column $[-1,\,0]^T$ tells you where the $y$-axis unit vector lands.

Columns are destinations. The $j$-th column of a matrix tells you where the $j$-th basis vector goes under the transformation. Once you know where the basis vectors land, you know where every vector lands, because every vector is a combination of basis vectors. This is the single most important insight in all of linear algebra.

Figure B.1. A matrix transforms the unit square (left) into a parallelogram (right). The columns of $A$ are the destinations of the basis vectors: $\hat{\mathbf{e}}_1 \to (2,0)$ and $\hat{\mathbf{e}}_2 \to (1,1)$. Every other point follows by linearity.

B.3   Matrix-Vector Multiplication: What It Actually Does

If $A$ is an $m \times n$ matrix and $\mathbf{x}$ is an $n$-vector, the product $\mathbf{y} = A\mathbf{x}$ is an $m$-vector whose $i$-th entry is:

(B.2) $$y_i = \sum_{j=1}^{n} A_{ij}\,x_j$$

Each entry of $\mathbf{y}$ is a weighted sum of the entries of $\mathbf{x}$, with weights given by the corresponding row of $A$. There are two complementary ways to think about this.

Row picture (dot products). Each row of $A$ dots with $\mathbf{x}$ to produce one entry of $\mathbf{y}$. Row $i$ defines a "recipe" for computing $y_i$: which entries of $\mathbf{x}$ to combine and with what weights. If most entries in a row are zero, the recipe uses only a few ingredients — this is the basis of sparse computation.

Column picture (linear combination). The product $A\mathbf{x}$ is a linear combination of the columns of $A$:

$$A\mathbf{x} = x_1\,\mathbf{a}_1 + x_2\,\mathbf{a}_2 + \cdots + x_n\,\mathbf{a}_n$$

Each column $\mathbf{a}_j$ gets scaled by the corresponding entry $x_j$, and the results are summed. This is the "columns as destinations" viewpoint from Section B.2.

Computational Cost

Multiplying a dense $N \times N$ matrix by an $N$-vector costs $O(N^2)$ operations. But if the matrix is sparse — with only $k$ nonzeros per row — the cost drops to $O(kN)$. For $k = 5$ (a common case), that is $O(5N) = O(N)$: linear in the problem size. This is why sparse matrix-vector multiplication (SpMV) is the workhorse operation of large-scale linear algebra.

B.4   Special Matrix Structures

Not all matrices are created equal. Recognizing structure is the key to choosing efficient algorithms.

Diagonal Matrices

A diagonal matrix has nonzero entries only on the main diagonal. Multiplying by it simply scales each entry: $(D\mathbf{x})_i = d_i x_i$. Cost: $O(N)$. Inverting: $(D^{-1})_{ii} = 1/d_i$. Diagonal matrices are the easiest possible case.

Tridiagonal Matrices

A tridiagonal matrix has nonzero entries only on the main diagonal and the two adjacent diagonals:

$$T = \begin{pmatrix} b_1 & c_1 & & \\ a_2 & b_2 & c_2 & \\ & a_3 & b_3 & c_3 \\ & & a_4 & b_4 \end{pmatrix}$$

Each row connects an entry to its two neighbors. Tridiagonal systems $T\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ can be solved in $O(N)$ time by the Thomas algorithm — a simplified form of Gaussian elimination.

Banded and Sparse Matrices

A banded matrix has nonzeros within a fixed number of diagonals of the main diagonal. A sparse matrix has the vast majority of entries equal to zero — typically a fixed number of nonzeros per row, regardless of $N$. For example, a matrix with 5 nonzeros per row and $N = 90{,}000$ columns is 99.99% zeros.

Sparse matrices are stored in compressed formats (CSR, COO) that record only the nonzero entries, reducing memory from $O(N^2)$ to $O(\text{nnz})$.

Sparsity reflects locality. A sparse matrix means each equation involves only a few variables — those that are "nearby" in some sense. When a physical system has local interactions (each node couples only to its neighbors), the resulting matrix is sparse. Sparsity is what makes large-scale computation possible.

B.5   The Identity Matrix and Inverses

The identity matrix $I$ is the "do nothing" transformation: $I\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}$ for every vector $\mathbf{x}$. It has ones on the diagonal and zeros everywhere else.

The inverse of a matrix $A$, written $A^{-1}$, is the matrix that "undoes" the transformation: $A^{-1}A = I$. If $A$ stretches by 2, then $A^{-1}$ compresses by $\frac{1}{2}$. Not every matrix has an inverse — a matrix that collapses a dimension cannot be undone.

In principle, solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ means computing $\mathbf{x} = A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$. In practice, we never compute $A^{-1}$ explicitly. For an $N \times N$ matrix, forming $A^{-1}$ costs $O(N^3)$ and produces a dense matrix even when $A$ is sparse. Instead, we use solvers that compute $A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$ without forming $A^{-1}$ itself. When you see $A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$ in this textbook, read it as "the vector $\mathbf{x}$ that solves $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$."

B.6   Linear Systems: $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$

The equation $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is the central problem of computational linear algebra. Given a matrix $A$ (the "rules") and a vector $\mathbf{b}$ (the "data"), find the vector $\mathbf{x}$ (the "answer"). In one sentence: which input $\mathbf{x}$, when transformed by $A$, produces the desired output $\mathbf{b}$?

Three Possible Outcomes

  1. Exactly one solution — when $A$ is invertible (non-singular). The generic, well-behaved case.
  2. No solution — when $\mathbf{b}$ is not in the range (column space) of $A$.
  3. Infinitely many solutions — when $A$ is singular (has a null space). Multiple inputs map to the same output.

The geometry of $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$. Think of $A$ as a transformation that maps "input space" to "output space." The solution $\mathbf{x}$ is the point in input space that maps to $\mathbf{b}$. If $A$ collapses some directions (its null space), multiple inputs map to the same output. If $\mathbf{b}$ points in a direction that $A$ cannot reach (outside its column space), there is no solution.

B.7   Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors: The DNA of a Matrix

Most vectors change direction when you multiply them by a matrix. But some special vectors only get scaled — they point in the same direction (or flip to the opposite direction) after the transformation. These are eigenvectors, and the scaling factors are eigenvalues:

(B.3) $$A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\,\mathbf{v}$$

The matrix $A$ acts on $\mathbf{v}$ by simply multiplying it by $\lambda$. No rotation, no shearing — just scaling. If $\lambda > 1$: stretched. If $0 < \lambda < 1$: compressed. If $\lambda < 0$: flipped and scaled. If $\lambda = 0$: sent to zero (the vector is in the null space).

Eigenvectors are the natural directions of a matrix. They are the directions along which the matrix acts most simply — as pure scaling. Understanding a matrix's eigenvectors and eigenvalues is like finding the "natural coordinate system" in which the matrix becomes diagonal.

Finding Eigenvalues

For a general $N \times N$ matrix, eigenvalues are the roots of the characteristic polynomial $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$, a polynomial of degree $N$. There are exactly $N$ eigenvalues (counting multiplicities, possibly complex). For $N > 4$, there is no closed-form formula — eigenvalues must be computed numerically.

Worked Example: A 2×2 Matrix

Let $A = \begin{pmatrix}4 & 1\\2 & 3\end{pmatrix}$. The characteristic polynomial is:

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = (4-\lambda)(3-\lambda) - 2 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = (\lambda - 5)(\lambda - 2) = 0$$

So $\lambda_1 = 5$ and $\lambda_2 = 2$. For $\lambda_1 = 5$: $(A - 5I)\mathbf{v} = 0$ gives $-v_1 + v_2 = 0$, so $\mathbf{v}_1 = [1,\,1]^T$. For $\lambda_2 = 2$: $2v_1 + v_2 = 0$, so $\mathbf{v}_2 = [1,\,-2]^T$. Along $\mathbf{v}_1$, $A$ stretches by 5; along $\mathbf{v}_2$, by 2. These are the natural axes of $A$.

Diagonalization

If a matrix $A$ has $N$ linearly independent eigenvectors, we can write $A = P\Lambda P^{-1}$, where $P$ is the matrix of eigenvectors and $\Lambda = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_N)$. In this "eigenbasis," $A$ acts as pure scaling along each axis. This is the key to spectral methods: change to the eigenbasis, do simple arithmetic, change back.

B.8   Symmetric and Positive Definite Matrices

A matrix $A$ is symmetric if $A = A^T$: $A_{ij} = A_{ji}$ for all $i,j$. Symmetric matrices have two remarkable properties:

  1. All eigenvalues are real. No complex values — just real scaling.
  2. Eigenvectors can be chosen orthogonal. The matrix diagonalizes as $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$, where $Q$ is orthogonal ($Q^{-1} = Q^T$) and $\Lambda$ is real diagonal.

A symmetric matrix is positive definite (PD) if all eigenvalues are strictly positive: $\lambda_i > 0$ for all $i$. Equivalently:

(B.4) $$\mathbf{x}^T A\,\mathbf{x} > 0 \qquad \text{for all } \mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$$

This quadratic form measures a kind of "energy." Positive definiteness means the energy is always positive — the matrix defines a bowl in $N$-dimensional space with a unique minimum at the origin. The linear system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is equivalent to finding the bottom of this bowl.

Figure B.2. Left: a positive-definite matrix defines a bowl — every direction curves upward, and the minimum is unique. Right: a positive semi-definite matrix defines a trough — flat along the null-space direction, so infinitely many minimizers exist along the valley floor.

SPD = the gold standard. Symmetric positive-definite (SPD) matrices have real positive eigenvalues, are always invertible, and admit the fastest solvers (Cholesky factorization, conjugate gradient). Every desirable property you could want, SPD matrices have.

Semi-definite means allowing $\lambda_i \geq 0$ (some eigenvalues may be zero). A symmetric positive semi-definite (SPSD) matrix satisfies $\mathbf{x}^T A\mathbf{x} \geq 0$, with equality for vectors in the null space. SPSD matrices are singular but "barely so" — only the null-space directions are collapsed.

B.9   The Condition Number: When Matrices Go Bad

Not all SPD systems are equally easy to solve. Consider two bowls: one is nearly round (a soup bowl), and the other is long and narrow (a bathtub). Both have a unique minimum, but finding the minimum of the bathtub is much harder — gradient descent zigzags across the narrow valley.

The condition number quantifies this shape:

(B.5) $$\kappa(A) = \frac{\lambda_{\max}}{\lambda_{\min}}$$

A condition number near 1 means a round bowl (easy). A condition number of $10^6$ means an extremely elongated bowl (hard). Iterative solvers need roughly $O(\sqrt{\kappa})$ iterations.

Figure B.3. Contour plots of $E(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^T A\mathbf{x}$. Left: well-conditioned ($\kappa \approx 1$) — nearly circular contours, gradient descent converges quickly. Right: ill-conditioned ($\kappa \gg 1$) — elongated contours, gradient descent zigzags across the narrow valley.

Condition number measures amplification of error. If you solve $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ with a small perturbation in $\mathbf{b}$, the perturbation in $\mathbf{x}$ is amplified by at most $\kappa(A)$. For $\kappa = 10^6$ and double-precision arithmetic (16 digits), you may lose 6 digits of accuracy. Preconditioning — transforming the system to reduce $\kappa$ — is the standard remedy.

B.10   Null Spaces and Singular Systems

The null space of a matrix $A$ is the set of all vectors that $A$ sends to zero:

(B.6) $$\text{null}(A) = \{\mathbf{v} : A\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\}$$

If the null space contains only $\mathbf{0}$, $A$ is invertible. If it contains nonzero vectors, $A$ is singular: it collapses some directions to zero.

Solving Singular Systems

If $A$ is singular, $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ has solutions only when $\mathbf{b}$ is orthogonal to the null space of $A^T$ (for symmetric $A$, this is the null space of $A$ itself). This is a compatibility condition. When it is satisfied, there are infinitely many solutions: if $\mathbf{x}^*$ is one, then $\mathbf{x}^* + \mathbf{v}$ is another for any $\mathbf{v} \in \text{null}(A)$. To select a unique solution, we impose an extra constraint — typically pinning (fixing one component) or projecting out the null-space component.

B.11   Block Matrices

A block matrix is a matrix partitioned into rectangular sub-matrices (blocks):

(B.7) $$\begin{pmatrix} A_{11} & A_{12} \\ A_{21} & A_{22} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{x}_1 \\ \mathbf{x}_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{b}_1 \\ \mathbf{b}_2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Block structure arises naturally when a system couples multiple variables. The diagonal blocks $A_{11}, A_{22}$ describe how each variable interacts with itself; the off-diagonal blocks $A_{12}, A_{21}$ describe the coupling.

Block Elimination and the Schur Complement

From the first block row: $\mathbf{x}_1 = A_{11}^{-1}(\mathbf{b}_1 - A_{12}\mathbf{x}_2)$. Substituting into the second row gives:

$$(A_{22} - A_{21}A_{11}^{-1}A_{12})\,\mathbf{x}_2 = \mathbf{b}_2 - A_{21}A_{11}^{-1}\mathbf{b}_1$$

The matrix $S = A_{22} - A_{21}A_{11}^{-1}A_{12}$ is the Schur complement. It reduces a $2N \times 2N$ system to an $N \times N$ system in $\mathbf{x}_2$ alone. This is a powerful technique: solve for $\mathbf{x}_2$ first, then back-substitute to find $\mathbf{x}_1$.

Worked Example

Consider the $4\times 4$ block system with $2\times 2$ blocks:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 3 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 5 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \\ x_4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 8 \\ 7 \\ 14 \\ 11 \end{pmatrix}$$

Here $A_{11} = \begin{pmatrix}4&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix}$, $A_{12} = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix} = I$, $A_{21} = I$, $A_{22} = \begin{pmatrix}5&2\\2&4\end{pmatrix}$. Since $A_{12} = A_{21} = I$, the Schur complement simplifies to $S = A_{22} - A_{11}^{-1}$. This reduces a $4\times 4$ problem to a $2\times 2$ problem: solve $S\,\mathbf{x}_2 = \mathbf{b}_2 - A_{11}^{-1}\mathbf{b}_1$ for $\mathbf{x}_2$, then back-substitute for $\mathbf{x}_1$.

If the full block system is symmetric indefinite (not SPD), the individual blocks or the Schur complement may still be SPD — allowing the use of fast solvers on the sub-problems even when the full system resists them.

B.12   Orthogonality and Projections

Two vectors $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are orthogonal if $\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{v} = 0$. Geometrically, they are perpendicular. Orthogonality is the most powerful simplifying concept in linear algebra: orthogonal directions do not interfere with each other.

A projection decomposes a vector into a component along a given direction and a component perpendicular to it:

(B.8) $$\text{proj}_{\mathbf{u}}\,\mathbf{v} = \frac{\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{v}}{\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{u}}\,\mathbf{u}$$

An orthogonal basis is a set of mutually orthogonal vectors that spans the space. In an orthogonal basis, every vector decomposes uniquely into components along each basis direction, and the components are just projections. This is the mathematical foundation of spectral transforms (Appendix C, Section C.10) and the conjugate gradient method (Appendix C, Section C.12).

$A$-orthogonality. Given an SPD matrix $A$, two vectors $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are called $A$-orthogonal (or conjugate) if $\mathbf{u}^T A \mathbf{v} = 0$. This is orthogonality with respect to the "geometry" defined by $A$ — the bowl shape from Section B.8. The conjugate gradient method (Appendix C) exploits $A$-orthogonality to converge in at most $N$ steps.
