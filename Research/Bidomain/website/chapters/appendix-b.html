Appendix B

Linear Algebra for Cardiac Simulation

Every simulation in this textbook — from a single cell to a full bidomain heart — ultimately reduces to operations on vectors and matrices. This appendix builds the linear algebra you need from the ground up, following the philosophy of 3Blue1Brown: start with pictures, develop geometric intuition, and let the formalism emerge naturally. We begin with the question that puzzles every calculus student encountering computational methods for the first time: how does a smooth, continuous function become a column of numbers?

B.1   From Functions to Vectors: The Discretization Leap

In calculus, you learned to think of $y = f(x)$ as a smooth curve — a rule that assigns a number $y$ to every real number $x$ on some interval. You can zoom in arbitrarily close to any point and the curve remains smooth. You can take derivatives and integrals. The function "lives" at infinitely many points simultaneously.

Now imagine you are a computer. You cannot store infinitely many values — you have finite memory. So you must make a choice: pick a finite set of points $x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_{N-1}$, evaluate the function at those points, and store only those values. This is the discretization leap.

From film to frames. A movie camera captures a continuous scene by taking 24 frames per second. Each frame is a snapshot — a finite approximation of a continuous reality. If the scene changes slowly, 24 frames per second is plenty. If something moves fast (a hummingbird's wing), you need more frames. Discretization is exactly the same: a smooth function becomes a finite list of samples, and the spacing between samples determines what features you can resolve.

Consider a temperature profile along a rod: $u(x)$ is a smooth function of position $x$ on the interval $[0, L]$. We lay down $N$ equally spaced grid points:

$$x_j = j \cdot \Delta x, \qquad j = 0, 1, \ldots, N-1, \qquad \Delta x = \frac{L}{N-1}$$

The entire function $u(x)$ — which required infinitely many numbers to describe — is now approximated by $N$ numbers:

(B.1) $$\mathbf{u} = \begin{pmatrix} u_0 \\ u_1 \\ \vdots \\ u_{N-1} \end{pmatrix} \quad \text{where } u_j \approx u(x_j)$$

This column of numbers is a vector. Not the arrow-in-space from physics class — a vector in the sense of linear algebra: an ordered list of numbers that you can add, scale, and combine.

Two Dimensions: From Surfaces to Flattened Grids

In calculus, a function of two variables $z = f(x,y)$ describes a surface — you can picture it as a landscape with hills and valleys hovering above the $xy$-plane. To discretize it, you lay a grid over the plane:

$$x_i = i\,\Delta x, \quad y_j = j\,\Delta y, \qquad i = 0,\ldots,N_x-1, \;\; j = 0,\ldots,N_y-1$$

Now you have $N_x \times N_y$ sample values $u_{i,j} \approx f(x_i, y_j)$. This is a 2D grid of numbers — a matrix, if you like. But to feed it into a linear algebra solver, we must flatten it into a single column vector. We stack the columns (or rows) into one long list:

$$\mathbf{u} = \begin{pmatrix} u_{0,0} \\ u_{1,0} \\ \vdots \\ u_{N_x-1,0} \\ u_{0,1} \\ \vdots \\ u_{N_x-1,N_y-1} \end{pmatrix} \quad \leftarrow \text{$N_x \times N_y$ entries total}$$

The ordering convention (which column comes first) is arbitrary but must be consistent. In this textbook, we use column-major ordering (stack columns left to right), which matches the indexing='ij' convention of our engine code. Each grid point $(i,j)$ maps to the vector index $k = i + j \cdot N_x$.

The key insight. Once you flatten a 2D grid (or 3D volume) into a 1D vector, every operation on that grid becomes a matrix operation on that vector. Taking a derivative? Matrix multiply. Applying a boundary condition? Modifying a row of a matrix. Solving a PDE? Solving a matrix equation. The entire machinery of linear algebra becomes available, and the physics of the original problem is encoded in the structure of the matrix.

This is why linear algebra is the language of scientific computing. The continuous PDE world of calculus must pass through the discretization gate, and on the other side, everything is vectors and matrices. The rest of this appendix teaches you to speak that language.

B.2   Matrices as Transformations

A matrix is not just a grid of numbers — it is a machine. You feed it a vector, and it produces a new vector. In the language of 3Blue1Brown: a matrix is a transformation that takes every point in space and moves it to a new location, in a way that keeps straight lines straight and the origin fixed.

Consider a $2 \times 2$ matrix acting on vectors in the plane:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

This matrix stretches every vector by a factor of 2 in the $x$-direction and leaves the $y$-direction unchanged. The unit square becomes a rectangle twice as wide. The matrix is the stretch — it encodes the transformation completely.

Now consider a rotation by $90°$:

$$R = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Every vector gets rotated counterclockwise by a quarter turn. The transformation is encoded in the columns of $R$: the first column $[0,\,1]^T$ tells you where the $x$-axis unit vector lands, and the second column $[-1,\,0]^T$ tells you where the $y$-axis unit vector lands.

Columns are destinations. The $j$-th column of a matrix tells you where the $j$-th basis vector goes under the transformation. Once you know where the basis vectors land, you know where every vector lands, because every vector is a combination of basis vectors. This is the single most important insight in all of linear algebra.

For cardiac simulation, the matrices we encounter are not $2 \times 2$ — they are $N \times N$ where $N$ may be hundreds of thousands. But the principle is identical. The stiffness matrix $K$ from Chapter 8 transforms a voltage vector $\mathbf{V}$ (voltage values at every grid point) into a vector $K\mathbf{V}$ that encodes the spatial coupling between neighbors (the discrete Laplacian). The matrix is the discrete version of the differential operator $\nabla \cdot (D\,\nabla)$.

B.3   Matrix-Vector Multiplication: What It Actually Does

The definition of matrix-vector multiplication is simple. If $A$ is an $m \times n$ matrix and $\mathbf{x}$ is an $n$-vector, the product $\mathbf{y} = A\mathbf{x}$ is an $m$-vector whose $i$-th entry is:

(B.2) $$y_i = \sum_{j=1}^{n} A_{ij}\,x_j$$

Each entry of $\mathbf{y}$ is a weighted sum of the entries of $\mathbf{x}$, with weights given by the corresponding row of $A$. But there are two complementary ways to think about this operation, and both are essential.

Row picture (dot products). Each row of $A$ dots with $\mathbf{x}$ to produce one entry of $\mathbf{y}$. Row $i$ defines a "recipe" for computing $y_i$: which entries of $\mathbf{x}$ to combine and with what weights. For a tridiagonal matrix (like the 1D Laplacian), row $i$ uses only $x_{i-1}$, $x_i$, and $x_{i+1}$ — the nearest neighbors. The recipe is the stencil.

Column picture (linear combination). The product $A\mathbf{x}$ is a linear combination of the columns of $A$:

$$A\mathbf{x} = x_1\,\mathbf{a}_1 + x_2\,\mathbf{a}_2 + \cdots + x_n\,\mathbf{a}_n$$

Each column $\mathbf{a}_j$ gets scaled by the corresponding entry $x_j$, and the results are summed. This is the "columns as destinations" viewpoint from Section B.2.

For cardiac simulation, the row picture is usually most natural. When you compute $L\mathbf{V}$ (Laplacian times voltage), row $i$ computes the discrete Laplacian at node $i$: it takes a weighted combination of the voltage at node $i$ and its neighbors. This is exactly the stencil operation described in Chapter 8.

Computational Cost

Multiplying a dense $N \times N$ matrix by an $N$-vector costs $O(N^2)$ operations ($N$ entries, each requiring $N$ multiplications and additions). But for the sparse matrices of cardiac simulation, most entries are zero. A tridiagonal matrix has at most 3 nonzeros per row, so the cost is $O(3N) = O(N)$. A 5-point 2D stencil has at most 5 nonzeros per row: still $O(N)$. This is why sparse matrix-vector multiplication (SpMV) is the workhorse operation of every solver in this textbook.

B.4   Special Matrix Structures

Not all matrices are created equal. The matrices that arise from PDE discretization have very specific structures, and recognizing these structures is the key to choosing efficient solvers. Here are the structures you will encounter repeatedly in this textbook.

Diagonal Matrices

A diagonal matrix has nonzero entries only on the main diagonal:

$$D = \begin{pmatrix} d_1 & & \\ & d_2 & \\ & & d_3 \end{pmatrix}$$

Multiplying by $D$ simply scales each entry: $(D\mathbf{x})_i = d_i x_i$. Cost: $O(N)$. Inverting $D$ is trivial: $(D^{-1})_{ii} = 1/d_i$ (as long as no $d_i$ is zero). In Chapter 11, the mass matrix $M$ for FDM is the identity — the simplest possible diagonal matrix.

Tridiagonal Matrices

A tridiagonal matrix has nonzero entries only on the main diagonal and the two adjacent diagonals:

$$T = \begin{pmatrix} b_1 & c_1 & & \\ a_2 & b_2 & c_2 & \\ & a_3 & b_3 & c_3 \\ & & a_4 & b_4 \end{pmatrix}$$

This is the structure of the 1D discrete Laplacian. Each row connects a node to its two neighbors. Tridiagonal systems can be solved in $O(N)$ time by the Thomas algorithm (a simplified form of Gaussian elimination) — no iterative solver needed. This is why 1D problems are "easy."

Banded Matrices

A banded matrix with bandwidth $w$ has nonzero entries within $w$ diagonals of the main diagonal. The 2D five-point Laplacian on an $N_x \times N_y$ grid, after flattening to a 1D vector, produces a matrix with bandwidth $N_x$ — the off-diagonal bands connect grid points that are neighbors in the $y$-direction. The matrix is "mostly zeros" but cannot be solved by the Thomas algorithm because $w$ is large.

Sparse Matrices

A matrix is sparse if the vast majority of its entries are zero. The 2D Laplacian is sparse: each row has at most 5 nonzero entries out of $N$ total columns. For $N = 90{,}000$ (a $300 \times 300$ grid), that is 5 nonzeros versus 90,000 columns — 99.99% zeros. Storing only the nonzero entries (using formats like CSR, COO, or PyTorch's sparse_coo_tensor — see Appendix D, Section D.7) reduces memory from $O(N^2)$ to $O(\text{nnz})$, where $\text{nnz}$ is the number of nonzeros. For the 5-point Laplacian, $\text{nnz} \approx 5N$.

Sparsity reflects locality. A sparse matrix means each equation involves only a few variables — those that are "nearby" in some sense. For cardiac simulation, "nearby" means spatially adjacent on the tissue grid. A cell's voltage at the next time step depends on its own voltage and the voltages of its immediate neighbors — not on cells millimeters away. This physical locality produces mathematical sparsity, and sparsity is what makes large-scale simulation possible.

B.5   The Identity Matrix and Matrix Inverses

The identity matrix $I$ is the "do nothing" transformation: $I\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}$ for every vector $\mathbf{x}$. It has ones on the diagonal and zeros everywhere else. It is the matrix equivalent of multiplying by 1.

The inverse of a matrix $A$, written $A^{-1}$, is the matrix that "undoes" the transformation: $A^{-1}A = I$. If $A$ stretches space by a factor of 2 in the $x$-direction, then $A^{-1}$ compresses it back. Not every matrix has an inverse — a matrix that collapses a dimension (sends a 2D plane to a line, for instance) cannot be undone.

In principle, solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ means computing $\mathbf{x} = A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$. But in practice, we never compute $A^{-1}$ explicitly. For an $N \times N$ matrix, forming $A^{-1}$ costs $O(N^3)$ and produces a dense matrix even when $A$ is sparse. Instead, we use solvers (direct or iterative) that compute the action of $A^{-1}$ on $\mathbf{b}$ without forming $A^{-1}$ itself. When you see $A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$ in this textbook, mentally read it as "the vector $\mathbf{x}$ that solves $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$."

This distinction is crucial for the bidomain solver in Chapter 15. The parabolic and elliptic sub-problems each require "inverting" a sparse matrix at every time step. PCG and spectral methods (Appendix C) compute $A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$ in $O(N)$ to $O(N\log N)$ time — without ever forming the $O(N^2)$-sized inverse.

B.6   Linear Systems: $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$

The equation $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is the central problem of computational science. Given a matrix $A$ (the "rules") and a vector $\mathbf{b}$ (the "data"), find the vector $\mathbf{x}$ (the "answer"). In one sentence: which input $\mathbf{x}$, when transformed by $A$, produces the desired output $\mathbf{b}$?

Every implicit time-stepper in this textbook requires solving a linear system. When we write, for example, the Backward Euler step (Chapter 11, equation 11.2):

$$(I - \Delta t\,L)\,\mathbf{V}^{n+1} = \mathbf{V}^n$$

we are asking: given the current voltage $\mathbf{V}^n$ (that is $\mathbf{b}$), what is the next voltage $\mathbf{V}^{n+1}$ (that is $\mathbf{x}$) such that the matrix $A = I - \Delta t\,L$ maps it to $\mathbf{V}^n$?

Three Possible Outcomes

A linear system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ can have:

  1. Exactly one solution — when $A$ is invertible (non-singular). This is the normal case for time-stepping systems in cardiac simulation.
  2. No solution — when $\mathbf{b}$ is not in the range of $A$. This does not arise in well-posed physical problems.
  3. Infinitely many solutions — when $A$ is singular (has a null space). This does arise: the bidomain elliptic equation (Chapter 12, equation 12.4) has a singular matrix because the extracellular potential $\phi_e$ is only defined up to a constant. See Section B.10.

The geometry of $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$. Think of $A$ as a transformation that maps "input space" to "output space." The solution $\mathbf{x}$ is the point in input space that maps to $\mathbf{b}$. If $A$ collapses some directions (its null space), multiple inputs map to the same output, giving infinitely many solutions. If $\mathbf{b}$ points in a direction that $A$ cannot reach (outside its range), there is no solution.

B.7   Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors: The DNA of a Matrix

Most vectors change direction when you multiply them by a matrix. But some special vectors only get scaled — they point in the same direction (or flip to the opposite direction) after the transformation. These special vectors are called eigenvectors, and the scaling factors are called eigenvalues:

(B.3) $$A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\,\mathbf{v}$$

Here $\mathbf{v}$ is an eigenvector and $\lambda$ is its corresponding eigenvalue. The matrix $A$ acts on $\mathbf{v}$ by simply multiplying it by $\lambda$. No rotation, no shearing — just scaling. If $\lambda > 1$, the vector gets stretched. If $0 < \lambda < 1$, it gets compressed. If $\lambda < 0$, it gets flipped and scaled. If $\lambda = 0$, it gets sent to zero — the vector is in the null space.

Eigenvectors are the natural directions of a matrix. They are the directions along which the matrix acts most simply — as pure scaling. Every other direction is a mix of stretching, rotating, and shearing. Understanding a matrix's eigenvectors and eigenvalues is like finding the "natural coordinate system" in which the matrix becomes diagonal.

Why Eigenvalues Matter for Cardiac Simulation

Eigenvalues appear in almost every chapter of this textbook:

  • Stability (Chapter 10): The CFL condition $\Delta t \leq 2/|\lambda_{\max}|$ ensures that explicit time-stepping does not blow up. The largest eigenvalue of the Laplacian determines the maximum stable time step.
  • Convergence (Chapter 11, Appendix C): The number of iterations PCG needs depends on the ratio $\kappa = \lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$ (the condition number).
  • Spectral solvers (Appendix C): The DCT/DST diagonalize the Laplacian — they find the eigenvectors. In the eigenvector basis, solving the linear system becomes trivial division.
  • Null space (Chapter 15): The bidomain elliptic operator has $\lambda = 0$ as an eigenvalue. The corresponding eigenvector (the constant vector) is the null space that must be handled carefully.

Finding Eigenvalues

For a general $N \times N$ matrix, eigenvalues are the roots of the characteristic polynomial $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$. This is a polynomial of degree $N$, so there are exactly $N$ eigenvalues (counting multiplicities, possibly complex). For $N > 4$, there is no closed-form formula — eigenvalues must be computed numerically.

But for structured matrices from PDE discretization, the eigenvalues often have known formulas. The discrete Laplacian on a 1D grid with $N$ points and Neumann BCs has eigenvalues (see Appendix C, Section C.8):

(B.4) $$\lambda_k = \frac{2}{\Delta x^2}\left(\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi k}{N}\right) - 1\right), \qquad k = 0, 1, \ldots, N-1$$

All eigenvalues are $\leq 0$ (the Laplacian is negative semi-definite). The largest in magnitude is $\lambda_{N-1} \approx -4/\Delta x^2$, which directly gives the CFL bound from Chapter 10.

B.8   Symmetric and Positive Definite Matrices

A matrix $A$ is symmetric if $A = A^T$ — that is, reflecting across the main diagonal leaves the matrix unchanged: $A_{ij} = A_{ji}$ for all $i,j$. In the language of transformations, symmetry means "the transformation treats all directions fairly" — it has no preferred handedness or rotation.

Symmetric matrices have a remarkable property: all their eigenvalues are real numbers. No complex values, no oscillatory behavior — just real scaling. Furthermore, the eigenvectors of a symmetric matrix can always be chosen to be orthogonal (perpendicular to each other). This means a symmetric matrix can always be diagonalized as $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$, where $Q$ is an orthogonal matrix of eigenvectors and $\Lambda$ is the diagonal matrix of eigenvalues.

A symmetric matrix is positive definite (PD) if all its eigenvalues are strictly positive: $\lambda_i > 0$ for all $i$. Equivalently:

(B.5) $$\mathbf{x}^T A\,\mathbf{x} > 0 \qquad \text{for all } \mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$$

This quadratic form $\mathbf{x}^T A\mathbf{x}$ measures a kind of "energy." Positive definiteness means the energy is always positive — the matrix defines a bowl (paraboloid) in $N$-dimensional space with a unique minimum at the origin. The linear system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is equivalent to finding the bottom of this bowl offset by $\mathbf{b}$.

SPD = the gold standard. Symmetric positive-definite (SPD) matrices are the best-behaved matrices in numerical computing. They have real, positive eigenvalues. They are invertible. The conjugate gradient method converges provably. Cholesky factorization (a fast variant of LU) works. Every property you could want, SPD matrices have. This is why so much effort in solver design goes into reformulating problems to produce SPD systems.

Semi-definite means allowing $\lambda_i \geq 0$ (some eigenvalues may be zero). A symmetric positive semi-definite (SPSD) matrix satisfies $\mathbf{x}^T A\mathbf{x} \geq 0$, with equality for vectors in the null space. The Neumann Laplacian is SPSD — it has one zero eigenvalue (the constant mode) and all other eigenvalues are positive.

B.9   The Condition Number: When Matrices Go Bad

Not all SPD systems are equally easy to solve. Consider two bowls: one is nearly round (like a soup bowl), and the other is long and narrow (like a bathtub). Both have a unique minimum, but finding the minimum of the bathtub is much harder — gradient descent zigzags across the narrow valley, making painfully slow progress toward the minimum.

The condition number quantifies this "shape" of the bowl:

(B.6) $$\kappa(A) = \frac{\lambda_{\max}}{\lambda_{\min}}$$

where $\lambda_{\max}$ and $\lambda_{\min}$ are the largest and smallest eigenvalues of $A$. A condition number near 1 means a round bowl (easy to solve). A condition number of $10^6$ means an extremely elongated bowl (hard to solve). The number of iterations that CG needs scales as $O(\sqrt{\kappa})$.

For the discrete Laplacian on a grid with $N$ points and spacing $\Delta x$:

$$\lambda_{\max} \sim \frac{4}{\Delta x^2}, \qquad \lambda_{\min} \sim \frac{\pi^2}{L^2}$$

so $\kappa \propto (L/\Delta x)^2 \propto N$ (in 1D). Refining the grid makes the condition number worse! This is the fundamental challenge of large-scale simulation, and it is why preconditioning — transforming the system to reduce $\kappa$ — is not optional for production solvers.

Condition number measures amplification of error. If you solve $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ with a small error in $\mathbf{b}$, the error in $\mathbf{x}$ is amplified by at most a factor of $\kappa(A)$. For $\kappa = 10^6$ and double-precision arithmetic (about 16 significant digits), you may lose 6 digits of accuracy — leaving only 10 correct digits in the solution.

B.10   Null Spaces and Singular Systems

The null space of a matrix $A$ is the set of all vectors that $A$ sends to zero:

(B.7) $$\text{null}(A) = \{\mathbf{v} : A\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\}$$

If the null space contains only the zero vector, $A$ is invertible and $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ has a unique solution. If the null space is nontrivial (contains nonzero vectors), $A$ is singular: it collapses some directions to zero and cannot be inverted.

This is not a theoretical curiosity — it happens in this textbook. The bidomain elliptic operator $-(L_i + L_e)$ has a one-dimensional null space spanned by the constant vector $\mathbf{1} = [1, 1, \ldots, 1]^T$. Physically, this means the extracellular potential $\phi_e$ is defined only up to an additive constant — like a voltage reference that can be placed anywhere.

Solving Singular Systems

If $A$ is singular, $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ has solutions only when $\mathbf{b}$ is orthogonal to the null space of $A^T$ (which equals the null space of $A$ when $A$ is symmetric). For the bidomain elliptic equation, this means the right-hand side must satisfy a compatibility condition: its components must sum to zero. Physically, this means the total current injected must equal the total current extracted — conservation of charge.

Once compatibility is satisfied, there are infinitely many solutions: if $\mathbf{x}^*$ is one solution, then $\mathbf{x}^* + c\,\mathbf{1}$ is also a solution for any constant $c$. In practice, we pick the unique solution by pinning: fixing $\phi_e$ at one node to zero, which removes the null space ambiguity. Chapter 15 (Section 15.2) discusses the two main approaches — row-zeroing (modify the matrix) and mean subtraction (project out the null component during iteration).

B.11   Block Matrices and Coupled Systems

A block matrix is a matrix partitioned into rectangular sub-matrices (blocks). This is not just a notational convenience — it reflects the physical structure of coupled systems.

The bidomain model couples two unknowns: the transmembrane potential $V_m$ and the extracellular potential $\phi_e$. After discretization, the system has the block form (Chapter 13, equation 13.1):

(B.8) $$\begin{pmatrix} A_{11} & A_{12} \\ A_{21} & A_{22} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{V}_m \\ \boldsymbol{\phi}_e \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{b}_1 \\ \mathbf{b}_2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Each block is an $N \times N$ matrix (where $N$ is the number of grid nodes). The diagonal blocks $A_{11}$ and $A_{22}$ describe how each variable interacts with itself; the off-diagonal blocks $A_{12}$ and $A_{21}$ describe how the two variables couple to each other.

Why Not Just Solve the Full System?

The full block system is $2N \times 2N$. Solving it monolithically requires a solver that handles $2N$ unknowns simultaneously. For a $300 \times 300$ grid, that is $2 \times 90{,}000 = 180{,}000$ unknowns — doable, but the matrix is symmetric indefinite (not SPD), so CG cannot be used. You would need GMRES, which requires more memory and has weaker convergence guarantees.

The insight of Chapter 14 is that the block structure allows decoupling. We solve for $\mathbf{V}_m$ and $\boldsymbol{\phi}_e$ separately using the individual blocks, iterating between them if needed. Each sub-solve is an $N \times N$ system that is SPD, so CG works. This is the Gauss-Seidel decoupled approach of Algorithm 14.1.

The Schur Complement

The formal way to decouple a block system is the Schur complement. From the first block row: $A_{11}\mathbf{V}_m + A_{12}\boldsymbol{\phi}_e = \mathbf{b}_1$, giving $\mathbf{V}_m = A_{11}^{-1}(\mathbf{b}_1 - A_{12}\boldsymbol{\phi}_e)$. Substituting into the second row:

$$(A_{22} - A_{21}A_{11}^{-1}A_{12})\,\boldsymbol{\phi}_e = \mathbf{b}_2 - A_{21}A_{11}^{-1}\mathbf{b}_1$$

The matrix $S = A_{22} - A_{21}A_{11}^{-1}A_{12}$ is the Schur complement. It reduces the $2N \times 2N$ system to an $N \times N$ system in $\boldsymbol{\phi}_e$ alone. If $S$ is SPD (which it is for the bidomain problem under appropriate conditions), CG can be used. The decoupled strategies of Chapter 14 can be viewed as approximate Schur complement methods.

B.12   Orthogonality and Projections

Two vectors $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are orthogonal if their inner product is zero: $\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{v} = 0$. Geometrically, they are perpendicular. Orthogonality is the most powerful simplifying concept in linear algebra, because orthogonal directions do not interfere with each other.

A projection takes a vector and decomposes it into a component along a given direction (or subspace) and a component perpendicular to it. The projection of $\mathbf{v}$ onto the direction $\mathbf{u}$ is:

(B.9) $$\text{proj}_{\mathbf{u}}\,\mathbf{v} = \frac{\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{v}}{\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{u}}\,\mathbf{u}$$

This projects $\mathbf{v}$ onto the line defined by $\mathbf{u}$ — the "shadow" of $\mathbf{v}$ in the $\mathbf{u}$ direction.

Where Projections Appear in Cardiac Simulation

  • Null space handling (Chapter 15): After each CG iteration on the elliptic system, we project out the null-space component by subtracting the mean: $\boldsymbol{\phi}_e \leftarrow \boldsymbol{\phi}_e - \bar{\phi}_e \cdot \mathbf{1}$. This is a projection onto the subspace orthogonal to the constant vector.
  • Conjugate gradient (Appendix C): The CG algorithm builds search directions that are $A$-orthogonal: $\mathbf{p}_i^T A \mathbf{p}_j = 0$ for $i \neq j$. This is orthogonality with respect to the inner product defined by $A$ (the "energy inner product"). Each iteration projects out one more dimension of error.
  • Spectral transforms (Appendix C): The DCT and DST express a vector as a sum of orthogonal basis functions (cosines or sines). The transform coefficients are inner products of the data with each basis function — projections onto the frequency directions.
