Chapter 2

From Neurons to Heart Cells

The nerve action potential is a brief spark — over in 2 milliseconds. The cardiac action potential is a sustained flame, lasting 200–400 ms. This chapter explains why the heart demands such a profoundly different electrical signature, and what new biophysics makes it possible.

2.1   Why the Heart Needs a Longer Action Potential

A nerve fiber has one job: transmit a binary signal from point A to point B as fast as possible. It fires and recovers quickly so it can fire again. But a cardiac myocyte has a very different job: it must contract, and contraction takes time. The heart needs the electrical signal to outlast the mechanical event so that every cell stays activated long enough to squeeze in synchrony.

If the cardiac AP were as short as a nerve spike, the cell would repolarize before it finished contracting. Worse, a new electrical impulse could arrive and re-activate parts of the heart while other parts were still contracting — leading to chaotic, uncoordinated contraction. The long AP duration serves as a built-in safety mechanism: by keeping the cell depolarized for hundreds of milliseconds, it prevents premature re-excitation.

Think of it this way: the nerve AP is like a camera flash — bright, instantaneous, done. The cardiac AP is like holding a light switch on for a deliberate period. The heart needs this "held" state because mechanical contraction is slow compared to electrical activation. The electrical plateau is the heart's way of saying "stay contracted until I tell you to relax."

2.2   The L-type Calcium Current and the Plateau

So how does the heart stretch a 2 ms spike into a 300 ms plateau? The answer is a current that neurons barely use: the L-type calcium current, $I_{\text{CaL}}$. This inward current activates during depolarization (like Na$^+$), but it inactivates much more slowly — on a timescale of 50–200 ms rather than 1–2 ms.

During the plateau phase (Phase 2 of the cardiac AP), the membrane sits at roughly 0 to +20 mV because two opposing forces are nearly balanced:

$$\underbrace{I_{\text{CaL}}}_{\text{inward, depolarizing}} \approx -\underbrace{I_{\text{Kr}} + I_{\text{Ks}}}_{\text{outward, repolarizing}}$$

The L-type Ca$^{2+}$ current holds the voltage up, while slowly activating delayed-rectifier K$^+$ currents ($I_{\text{Kr}}$ and $I_{\text{Ks}}$) try to pull it down. This tug-of-war creates a nearly flat plateau that can last over 200 ms. Eventually, as $I_{\text{CaL}}$ inactivates (both via voltage and via rising intracellular Ca$^{2+}$), the K$^+$ currents win and repolarization accelerates.

The plateau is a fragile equilibrium. A small change in any current during Phase 2 can dramatically alter AP duration. This is why drug-induced QT prolongation (blocking $I_{\text{Kr}}$) is so dangerous — it tips the balance toward inward current, lengthens the plateau, and can trigger the lethal arrhythmia torsades de pointes. Understanding this balance is central to computational drug safety testing.

The L-type calcium current also serves a second critical role: it triggers the release of a much larger amount of calcium from the sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) — the cell's internal calcium store. This process, called calcium-induced calcium release (CICR), amplifies the Ca$^{2+}$ signal roughly 10-fold and drives actual muscle contraction. We will see this calcium handling machinery in detail when we study the TTP06 model in Chapter 5.

2.3   Minimal Cardiac Models: FitzHugh–Nagumo to Fenton–Karma

Before diving into the full complexity of modern ionic models, it is instructive to see how the cardiac AP can be captured with just 2–3 variables. These minimal models sacrifice biophysical detail for mathematical tractability, but they capture the essential shape.

The FitzHugh–Nagumo Model (2 variables)

In 1961, Richard FitzHugh realized that the HH equations could be distilled to their essence. The key insight: the fast variable (Na$^+$ activation) and the slow variable (recovery) are what matter most. He proposed:

(2.1) $$\frac{dv}{dt} = v - \tfrac{v^3}{3} - w + I_{\text{ext}}, \qquad \frac{dw}{dt} = \epsilon(v + a - bw)$$

Here $v$ is a rescaled voltage, $w$ is a slow recovery variable (combining $h$ and $n$ into a single "how recovered is the cell" variable), and $\epsilon \ll 1$ ensures that $w$ moves much slower than $v$. The cubic nonlinearity $v - v^3/3$ creates the bistability needed for excitation: $v$ has two stable branches (resting and excited) and the system jumps between them.

FitzHugh–Nagumo captures excitability, threshold behavior, and refractoriness — but it produces a triangular AP, not the flat plateau of a cardiac cell.

The Fenton–Karma Model (3 variables)

In 1998, Flavio Fenton and Alain Karma introduced a three-variable model specifically designed for cardiac tissue. The key addition: a slow inward current that produces the plateau.

(2.2) $$\frac{dV}{dt} = -\bigl(I_{\text{fi}}(V,v) + I_{\text{so}}(V) + I_{\text{si}}(V,w)\bigr)$$

The three currents map directly to cardiac physiology: $I_{\text{fi}}$ is a fast inward current (like Na$^+$ creating the upstroke), $I_{\text{so}}$ is a slow outward current (like K$^+$ causing repolarization), and $I_{\text{si}}$ is a slow inward current (like Ca$^{2+}$ sustaining the plateau). With just three variables ($V$, $v$, $w$), the Fenton–Karma model reproduces realistic AP shapes, spiral waves, and even alternans — all phenomena seen in real cardiac tissue.

Minimal models like Fenton–Karma are not toys. In Engine V5.4, they serve as essential validation benchmarks: if the monodomain solver produces the correct spiral-wave dynamics with a 3-variable model, you can have confidence in the diffusion numerics before scaling up to TTP06 (18 variables) or ORd (40 variables). They are also used for parameter sweeps and machine-learning studies where millions of simulations are needed.

The progression from HH (4 variables, nerve) → FitzHugh–Nagumo (2 variables, generic excitable) → Fenton–Karma (3 variables, cardiac) → full ionic models (18–40+ variables, human ventricle) is the story of matching model complexity to the scientific question being asked.
