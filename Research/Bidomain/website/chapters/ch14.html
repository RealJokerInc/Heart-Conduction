Chapter 14

Solving the Coupled System

We now have two coupled PDEs and a 2×2 block matrix. How do we actually solve this at each time step? This is the heart of the bidomain implementation. The key insight — which makes everything tractable — is that we do not solve the full 2N×2N system. Instead, we split it into two separate N×N solves. This chapter presents the complete algorithm and five concrete strategies for executing it.

14.1   Why Fully Explicit Methods Fail

Before building the solution, let us understand the constraint we are working under. A natural first instinct is: "Can we just march everything forward explicitly, like we did with RK4 for the monodomain?" The answer is no, for a fundamental structural reason.

The parabolic equation (for $V_m$) has a $\partial V_m / \partial t$ term, so in principle it can be marched forward in time. But the elliptic equation — $0 = \nabla\cdot((\mathbf{D}_i + \mathbf{D}_e)\,\nabla\phi_e) + \nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla V_m)$ — is a constraint. There is no time derivative to march. At every instant, $\phi_e$ must satisfy this equation exactly. That means solving a linear system:

(14.1) $$-(\mathbf{L}_i + \mathbf{L}_e)\,\boldsymbol{\phi}_e = \mathbf{L}_i\,\mathbf{V}_m$$

This is the single most important distinction between bidomain and monodomain: at least one implicit linear solve per time step is unavoidable.

Think of it like a puppet on strings. $V_m$ is the hand — it moves freely (parabolic, has momentum). $\phi_e$ is the puppet — it has no momentum of its own, and must instantly adjust to wherever the hand moves. You cannot "predict" where the puppet will be next; you must solve for its position given the current hand position. This instant adjustment is the elliptic solve.

Even if we accept the mandatory elliptic solve, can the parabolic part be treated explicitly? Yes — but at a severe cost. The CFL condition for forward Euler on the bidomain parabolic equation gives:

(14.2) $$\Delta t \leq \frac{\Delta x^2}{2\,d\,D_{\max}}$$

For typical cardiac parameters with $\Delta x = 0.1$ mm in 2D, this gives $\Delta t_{\text{explicit}} \lesssim 0.5$ $\mu$s. Semi-implicit methods allow $\Delta t \approx 50$ $\mu$s — a factor of 100× larger.

14.2   The Decoupled Approach: Divide and Conquer

The 2×2 block system (13.1) has $2N$ unknowns. Solving it monolithically would require a specialized solver for symmetric indefinite systems — MINRES, GMRES, or similar (see the sidebar in Section 14.5). But there is a much simpler approach: solve the two equations one at a time.

The idea is exactly like Gauss-Seidel iteration for a system of equations. First, update $V_m$ using the current (or previous) $\phi_e$. Then, update $\phi_e$ using the new $V_m$. Each sub-problem is N×N and SPD — solvable by the familiar tools from Part II (PCG, Chebyshev, spectral methods).

Imagine untangling two knotted ropes. Trying to straighten both simultaneously (monolithic solve) is hard — you have to coordinate every move. But if you straighten one rope first, then use the straightened rope as a guide to straighten the other, the problem becomes two easy steps instead of one hard one. That is the decoupled approach.

The complete bidomain time step, including the ionic update from operator splitting (Chapter 9), is shown in Algorithm 14.1.

Algorithm 14.1 — Decoupled Gauss-Seidel Bidomain Step

Given: $\mathbf{V}_m^n$, $\boldsymbol{\phi}_e^n$, ionic states at time $t^n$

Stage 1 — Ionic update (same as monodomain, Section 9.4):

$\mathbf{V}_m^* \leftarrow \text{Rush–Larsen}(\mathbf{V}_m^n,\;\text{ionic\_states},\;\Delta t)$

Stage 2 — Parabolic solve (update $V_m$, using lagged $\phi_e^n$):

$$\underbrace{\left(\frac{1}{\Delta t}\,\mathbf{I} - \theta\,\mathbf{L}_i\right)}_{\mathbf{A}_{\text{para}}}\;\mathbf{V}_m^{n+1} = \underbrace{\left(\frac{1}{\Delta t}\,\mathbf{I} - (1-\theta)\,\mathbf{L}_i\right)}_{\mathbf{B}_{\text{para}}}\;\mathbf{V}_m^* + \mathbf{L}_i\,\boldsymbol{\phi}_e^n$$

[$N \times N$, SPD — solve with PCG, Chebyshev, or spectral method]

Stage 3 — Elliptic solve (update $\phi_e$, using new $V_m^{n+1}$):

$$\underbrace{-(\mathbf{L}_i + \mathbf{L}_e)}_{\mathbf{A}_{\text{ellip}}}\;\boldsymbol{\phi}_e^{n+1} = \mathbf{L}_i\,\mathbf{V}_m^{n+1}$$

[$N \times N$, SPD after pinning — solve with spectral or PCG]

Three key observations about this algorithm:

  1. Stage 1 is identical to monodomain. The ionic model does not know or care about $\phi_e$. It sees only $V_m$ and the gating/concentration states.
  2. Both linear systems are N×N and SPD. We never form or solve the full 2N×2N system. Every solver tool from Part II (PCG, Chebyshev, DCT) applies directly.
  3. The coupling is sequential: $\phi_e^n$ feeds into the parabolic RHS, and the resulting $V_m^{n+1}$ feeds into the elliptic RHS. This is why it is called "Gauss-Seidel" — each unknown is updated using the most recent value of the other.

The parameter $\theta$ controls the time-stepping scheme. $\theta = 0.5$ gives Crank–Nicolson (second-order, A-stable). $\theta = 1.0$ gives backward Euler (first-order, L-stable — damps spurious oscillations near steep wavefronts). Both are unconditionally stable, meaning there is no CFL restriction on $\Delta t$.

14.3   Four More Strategies

Algorithm 14.1 is the baseline — simple, robust, and the most common choice in the literature. But there are good reasons to consider alternatives: GPU parallelism, higher temporal accuracy, or avoiding the parabolic linear solve entirely. The Engine V1 codebase implements five strategies, all sharing the same structure — ionic step, then parabolic step, then elliptic step — but differing in how each step is executed.

Strategy 2: Semi-Implicit (Forward Euler + Implicit Elliptic)

What if we skip the parabolic linear solve entirely? Replace Stage 2 with a single explicit forward Euler step:

$$\mathbf{V}_m^{n+1} = \mathbf{V}_m^* + \Delta t\;\mathbf{L}_i\,(\mathbf{V}_m^* + \boldsymbol{\phi}_e^n)$$

This is just one sparse matrix-vector product — no linear solve. Stage 3 (elliptic) remains implicit, because it must (Section 14.1). The cost drops from two linear solves to one SpMV + one linear solve.

The catch: this is CFL-constrained. The stability condition is $\Delta t < \Delta x^2 / (4\,D_i)$. For $\Delta x = 0.025$ cm and $D_i = 1.214 \times 10^{-3}$: $\Delta t_{\max} \approx 0.126$ ms. This is workable — and on GPUs, the cheap per-step cost can more than compensate for the smaller time step.

Strategy 3: Jacobi (Fully Parallel)

The Gauss-Seidel strategy has a sequential dependency: Stage 3 needs $V_m^{n+1}$ from Stage 2. The Jacobi variant breaks this dependency by using old values for both:

  • Stage 2 (parabolic): same as GS — uses $\phi_e^n$
  • Stage 3 (elliptic): uses $V_m^{\mathbf{n}}$ instead of $V_m^{n+1}$

Now both solves depend only on time-$n$ data. They can run simultaneously on two GPU streams. The price: slightly weaker coupling (the elliptic solve lags the parabolic by one step). Fernandez & Zemzemi (2010) proved this variant is still energy-stable.

Strategy 4: IMEX-SBDF2 (Second-Order, L-Stable)

Both GS and Jacobi with $\theta = 0.5$ (Crank–Nicolson) are second-order in time, but CN can produce spurious oscillations near steep wavefronts. The SBDF2 (semi-implicit BDF2) method achieves second-order accuracy with L-stability — it damps high-frequency errors rather than preserving them.

SBDF2 uses values from two previous time steps. The parabolic stage becomes:

(14.3) $$\left(\frac{3}{2\,\Delta t}\,\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{L}_i\right)\mathbf{V}_m^{n+1} = \frac{2}{\Delta t}\,\mathbf{V}_m^n - \frac{1}{2\,\Delta t}\,\mathbf{V}_m^{n-1} + \mathbf{L}_i\,\boldsymbol{\phi}_e^n$$

Because SBDF2 requires two previous solutions, it cannot self-start. The first step uses backward Euler ($\theta = 1.0$), then all subsequent steps use the BDF2 formula. The cost is identical to GS (two linear solves per step), but the temporal accuracy is higher and the L-stability prevents oscillation artifacts.

Strategy 5: Explicit RKC (Chebyshev-Stabilized)

Runge–Kutta–Chebyshev (RKC) is the most creative approach. Like the semi-implicit strategy, it avoids the parabolic linear solve. But instead of forward Euler (1 stage, tiny stability region), it uses $s$ stages of a Chebyshev polynomial recurrence, extending the stability region quadratically:

$$\text{Stability limit} \approx 0.65\,s^2 \times \Delta t_{\text{FE}}$$

With $s = 20$ stages, the stability limit is $\approx 260 \times$ that of forward Euler. Each stage requires only a sparse matrix-vector product (cheap, parallelizable). The total cost is $s$ SpMV + 1 elliptic solve.

The Chebyshev stages use a 3-term recurrence: $W_0 = V_m^*$, $W_1 = W_0 + \omega_1\,\Delta t\;\mathbf{L}_i(W_0 + \phi_e^n)$, and for $j = 2, \ldots, s$: $W_j = 2\,\omega_0\,W_{j-1} - W_{j-2} + 2\,\omega_1\,\Delta t\;\mathbf{L}_i(W_{j-1} + \phi_e^n)$, where $\omega_0$, $\omega_1$ are derived from Chebyshev polynomial properties. The final $V_m^{n+1} = W_s$ (after normalization). The elliptic solve is identical to all other strategies.

The spectrum of explicitness. These five strategies span a continuum. At one end, GS with $\theta = 1.0$ treats everything implicitly (two linear solves, no CFL). At the other end, semi-implicit uses forward Euler for the parabolic part (one SpMV, CFL-constrained). RKC sits in between — explicit stages with an extended stability region. SBDF2 is fully implicit but optimized for accuracy. Jacobi is identical to GS in cost, but parallel.

14.5   Worked Example: One Decoupled Step on a 5-Node Cable

Let us continue the 5-node cable from Section 13.5 and advance one time step using the decoupled GS strategy with $\theta = 0.5$, $\Delta t = 0.02$ ms.

Initial conditions: $V_m^0 = [-85, -85, -20, -85, -85]^T$ mV (a single node depolarized), $\phi_e^0 = [0, 0, 0, 0, 0]^T$ mV.

Stage 1 — Ionic update. Rush–Larsen produces $V_m^* \approx [-85, -85, -19.5, -85, -85]^T$ (the depolarized node changes slightly due to ionic currents; exact value depends on the model).

Stage 2 — Parabolic solve.

Build the RHS: $\mathbf{b}_{\text{para}} = \mathbf{B}_{\text{para}}\,V_m^* + \mathbf{L}_i\,\phi_e^0$. Since $\phi_e^0 = \mathbf{0}$, the coupling term vanishes on the first step. The parabolic solve produces $V_m^1$ — the depolarized region begins to spread to neighbors due to intracellular diffusion: $V_m^1 \approx [-84.75, -83.74, -20.52, -83.74, -84.75]^T$ mV.

Stage 3 — Elliptic solve.

RHS: $\mathbf{b}_{\text{ellip}} = \mathbf{L}_i\,V_m^1$. The Laplacian $\mathbf{L}_i$ applied to $V_m^1$ produces a vector that is nonzero primarily near the depolarized region (where gradients are large). After solving $-(\mathbf{L}_i + \mathbf{L}_e)\,\phi_e^1 = \mathbf{b}_{\text{ellip}}$ with pinning ($\phi_{e,0} = 0$), we get $\phi_e^1 \approx [0, -0.18, 1.36, -0.18, 0]^T$ mV. The extracellular potential peaks near the depolarized cell — current is leaving the intracellular space and returning through the extracellular space, creating a dipole-like pattern.

The extracellular signal is the "shadow" of the wavefront. Wherever $V_m$ has a steep gradient, intracellular current is flowing out of cells and returning through the extracellular space, creating the $\phi_e$ signal. This is exactly the signal picked up by an ECG electrode. The elliptic solve computes this shadow at every time step.

14.6   Operator Splitting: The Outer Loop

The strategies above handle the diffusion sub-step of operator splitting. The full bidomain simulation uses the same Godunov or Strang splitting from Chapter 9:

  • Godunov: ionic step (full $\Delta t$) → diffusion step (full $\Delta t$). First-order splitting error.
  • Strang: ionic step ($\Delta t/2$) → diffusion step ($\Delta t$) → ionic step ($\Delta t/2$). Second-order splitting error.

The splitting error comes from separating reaction from diffusion, not from the parabolic–elliptic coupling. The elliptic equation has no time derivative and no memory (Section 12.6): its solution depends only on the current $V_m$, so it "comes along for the ride" within the diffusion sub-step without introducing additional splitting error.

Monodomain splitting vs. bidomain splitting. In both cases, operator splitting separates reaction (local ionic ODEs) from diffusion (global PDE). The fact that the bidomain diffusion sub-step involves two coupled equations does not introduce extra splitting error — both are solved together within the diffusion sub-step.

Sidebar: The monolithic alternative. In the research literature, you may encounter solvers that assemble the full 2N×2N block system and solve it with GMRES or MINRES using block preconditioners (block LDU, Schur complement approximations, AMG sub-solves). This monolithic approach has theoretical advantages (tighter coupling, potential for fewer iterations) but is much more complex to implement and requires specialized indefinite-system solvers. The decoupled approach described above is simpler, produces N×N SPD sub-problems solvable by standard tools, and is the dominant approach in modern production codes. Engine V1 uses decoupled strategies exclusively.
