Chapter 9

Operator Splitting: Divide and Conquer

The monodomain equation couples a stiff, nonlinear ODE system (ion channels) with a linear diffusion PDE. Trying to solve them simultaneously would be nightmarish. Operator splitting breaks the problem into two simpler subproblems — and it works remarkably well.

9.1   Why Split? The Stiffness Argument

Imagine you are cooking a stir-fry. The vegetables need to sizzle at high heat for 30 seconds (fast process), while the rice simmers gently for 20 minutes (slow process). Trying to cook both in the same pot at the same temperature is hopeless — you either burn the rice or undercook the vegetables. The smart approach: cook them in separate pots, each at the right temperature, then combine at the end. Operator splitting does exactly this for the monodomain equation: the ionic channels (fast, local, nonlinear) get their own "pot," and the diffusion (slow, global, linear) gets another.

The fundamental problem is timescale separation. During the upstroke (Phase 0), the sodium gate $m$ changes with a time constant of $\sim 0.1$ ms, while the diffusion length scale requires timesteps of $\sim 0.01$ ms for stability. But during the plateau (Phase 2), everything moves slowly — $\tau \sim 50{-}200$ ms. Any method that tries to handle both regimes in a single system must either use tiny timesteps everywhere (wasting computation during the plateau) or use implicit methods for the full coupled system (requiring the solution of a massive nonlinear system at each step).

Operator splitting exploits the structure of the problem. Write equation (7.1) as:

$$\frac{\partial V_m}{\partial t} = \underbrace{\frac{1}{\chi C_m}\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{D}\nabla V_m)}_{\mathcal{D}(V_m)} + \underbrace{\frac{-I_{\text{ion}}(V_m, \mathbf{w})}{ C_m}}_{\mathcal{R}(V_m, \mathbf{w})}$$

Instead of solving $\partial V_m/\partial t = \mathcal{D} + \mathcal{R}$ as one equation, we solve them sequentially: first advance the reaction $\mathcal{R}$ (purely local ODE at each point), then advance the diffusion $\mathcal{D}$ (linear PDE, no ionic coupling).

9.2   Godunov (First-Order) Splitting

The simplest splitting method advances from $t^n$ to $t^{n+1} = t^n + \Delta t$ in two stages:

Stage 1 — Reaction: At every spatial point, solve the ODE system $dV_m/dt = \mathcal{R}(V_m, \mathbf{w})$ and $d\mathbf{w}/dt = \mathbf{g}(V_m, \mathbf{w})$ from $t^n$ to $t^{n+1}$, using $V_m^n$ as the initial condition.

Stage 2 — Diffusion: Starting from the updated $V_m^*$ (output of Stage 1), solve $\partial V_m/\partial t = \mathcal{D}(V_m)$ from $t^n$ to $t^{n+1}$.

Godunov splitting is first-order accurate in time: the splitting error is $O(\Delta t)$. For cardiac simulations with $\Delta t = 0.01{-}0.05$ ms, this error is generally acceptable because the spatial and temporal discretization errors are of similar magnitude.

9.3   Strang (Second-Order) Splitting

For higher accuracy, Strang splitting uses a symmetric sequence:

Half-step reaction ($\Delta t/2$) → Full-step diffusion ($\Delta t$) → Half-step reaction ($\Delta t/2$).

The symmetry cancels the leading-order splitting error, yielding $O(\Delta t^2)$ accuracy. The cost is modestly higher (two reaction solves instead of one, though each is half as long), but the improved accuracy can allow larger timesteps for the same error tolerance.

Strang splitting is second-order because of a symmetry argument. If the error of $\mathcal{R}\to\mathcal{D}$ is $+c\,\Delta t^2$ for some constant $c$, then the error of $\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{R}$ is $-c\,\Delta t^2$ (the error changes sign when you swap the order). By sandwiching — $\mathcal{R}(\Delta t/2)\to\mathcal{D}(\Delta t)\to\mathcal{R}(\Delta t/2)$ — the first-order errors cancel, leaving $O(\Delta t^3)$ local error and $O(\Delta t^2)$ global error.

9.4   The Rush–Larsen Method for Ionic Variables

During the reaction substep, we must integrate the gating ODEs $dx/dt = (x_\infty - x)/\tau_x$. Forward Euler would require very small substeps for gates with fast kinetics (small $\tau_x$). The Rush–Larsen method sidesteps this entirely by noting that for fixed voltage, the gating ODE has an exact exponential solution:

(9.1) $$x^{n+1} = x_\infty(V_m^n) + \bigl(x^n - x_\infty(V_m^n)\bigr)\,e^{-\Delta t / \tau_x(V_m^n)}$$

This is unconditionally stable for any $\Delta t > 0$: even if $\tau_x = 0.01$ ms (the fastest Na$^+$ gate during the upstroke), the exponential simply drives $x$ to $x_\infty$ without oscillation. For slow gates ($\tau_x \gg \Delta t$), the exponential reduces to Forward Euler, so no accuracy is lost.

Rush–Larsen is like solving the "easy part" of the ODE exactly and only approximating the "hard part" (the voltage dependence of $x_\infty$ and $\tau_x$). Since these parameters change slowly compared to the gate dynamics during an upstroke, the approximation is excellent. It transforms the stiffest part of the problem — fast gating — from a numerical headache into a single exponential evaluation per gate per timestep.

In Engine V5.4, the splitting and ionic integration are cleanly separated. The SplittingStrategy ABC (solver/splitting/base.py) owns an IonicSolver and a DiffusionSolver and calls them in the appropriate order. GodunovSplitting calls ionic_solver.step(state, dt) then diffusion_solver.step(state, dt). StrangSplitting calls ionic at dt/2, diffusion at dt, ionic at dt/2.

The RushLarsenSolver (solver/ionic_time_stepping/rush_larsen.py) is a subclass of the IonicSolver ABC. Its step() method follows a specific order: (1) compute $I_{\text{ion}}$ from the current state, (2) compute $x_\infty$ and $\tau_x$ from the old voltage, (3) Forward Euler on voltage, (4) Rush–Larsen exponential update on gates, (5) Forward Euler on concentrations. A separate ForwardEulerIonicSolver is available for debugging. Concentration variables always use Forward Euler because they do not follow relaxation kinetics.
