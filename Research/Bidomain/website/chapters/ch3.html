Chapter 3

Anatomy of the Cardiac Action Potential

The ventricular action potential is traditionally divided into five phases (0 through 4). Each phase has a dominant current, a clear biophysical story, and direct consequences for the heart's electrical and mechanical function. Understanding these phases is the Rosetta Stone for reading any cardiac cell model. Chapter 5 provides the complete mathematical formulations for all 12 membrane currents in the TTP06 model; here we build the qualitative intuition first.
Figure 3.1. The five phases of the human ventricular action potential with dominant currents. Phase 0: Na$^+$ upstroke. Phase 1: transient outward K$^+$ (notch). Phase 2: Ca$^{2+}$-K$^+$ plateau. Phase 3: K$^+$-driven repolarization. Phase 4: resting potential maintained by $I_{\text{K1}}$.

3.1   Phase 0: The Sodium Upstroke

Phase 0 is the fastest event in cardiac electrophysiology. When an arriving wavefront brings $V_m$ above threshold ($\approx -65$ mV), sodium channels undergo the same explosive positive feedback we saw in the HH model: $m$-gates open rapidly, Na$^+$ floods inward, and $V_m$ rockets from $-85$ mV to about $+40$ mV in roughly 1–2 ms.

The maximum rate of rise, $dV/dt_{\text{max}}$, is a clinical measure of sodium channel availability. In healthy ventricular tissue, $dV/dt_{\text{max}} \approx 200{-}300$ V/s. This is also what determines conduction velocity — the faster the upstroke, the larger the local current source that can depolarize neighboring cells, and the faster the wave propagates. In the monodomain equation (Chapter 7), this translates directly to the source term.

3.2   Phase 1: Early Repolarization ($I_{\text{to}}$)

Immediately after the peak, a brief notch appears — a small dip in voltage before the plateau. This is caused by the transient outward potassium current $I_{\text{to}}$, which activates rapidly during depolarization and inactivates within tens of milliseconds.

Phase 1 may look like a minor detail, but it has major consequences. The depth of the notch varies across the ventricular wall: epicardial cells (outer surface) have large $I_{\text{to}}$ and a deep notch, while endocardial cells (inner surface) have little $I_{\text{to}}$ and almost no notch. This transmural gradient in AP morphology creates voltage differences across the wall that are visible on the ECG — the J-wave and T-wave both depend on it.

3.3   Phase 2: The Plateau — A Delicate Balance

The plateau is the heart's signature. For 150–250 ms, the membrane voltage hovers between $-10$ and $+20$ mV, sustained by the near-cancellation of two opposing current groups:

Inward (depolarizing): The L-type calcium current $I_{\text{CaL}}$ is the dominant plateau current. Ca$^{2+}$ ions enter through slowly inactivating channels, holding the voltage up. A smaller late sodium current ($I_{\text{NaL}}$) also contributes, especially in disease states.

Outward (repolarizing): Two delayed-rectifier potassium currents slowly activate: $I_{\text{Kr}}$ (rapid component, carried by hERG channels) and $I_{\text{Ks}}$ (slow component). The inward-rectifier $I_{\text{K1}}$ is largely blocked at plateau voltages due to its voltage dependence — it will become important again in Phase 3–4.

The plateau balance explains why the cardiac AP duration (APD) is so sensitive to perturbations. Block 30% of $I_{\text{Kr}}$ (as many common drugs do), and the plateau suddenly lacks enough repolarizing current. APD extends, the QT interval on the ECG lengthens, and the risk of arrhythmia rises. This is why in silico drug testing with models like TTP06 and ORd has become a regulatory focus (the CiPA initiative).

3.4   Phase 3: Repolarization — The Potassium Takeover

As $I_{\text{CaL}}$ gradually inactivates (driven both by voltage-dependent inactivation and by rising intracellular Ca$^{2+}$ concentration), the balance tips irreversibly toward outward current. The K$^+$ currents $I_{\text{Kr}}$ and $I_{\text{Ks}}$, which have been slowly building, now dominate. As the voltage falls, $I_{\text{K1}}$ — the strong inward rectifier that was blocked at plateau voltages — begins to unblock and adds its large outward conductance to the repolarization.

Phase 3 repolarization is regenerative: as $V_m$ falls, more $I_{\text{K1}}$ unblocks, which pulls $V_m$ down further, which unblocks more $I_{\text{K1}}$. This positive feedback (now outward) ensures that repolarization, once started, proceeds rapidly to completion. The membrane returns to its resting potential of about $-85$ mV.

3.5   Phase 4: Rest and the Role of $I_{\text{K1}}$

At rest, the membrane potential sits near $E_K \approx -88$ mV because $I_{\text{K1}}$ dominates the conductance. The "inward rectifier" name refers to a peculiar property: $I_{\text{K1}}$ passes large outward current at voltages just below $E_K$, but essentially zero current at plateau voltages. This voltage-dependent block (caused by intracellular magnesium and polyamines plugging the channel pore) is what allows the plateau to exist — without it, K$^+$ would short-circuit the plateau and the AP would collapse.

Think of $I_{\text{K1}}$ as a two-state switch. At rest, it is "on" — a strong anchor holding $V_m$ near $E_K$. During the plateau, it switches "off" — stepping aside to let the delicate Ca$^{2+}$/K$^+$ balance play out. Then in Phase 3, it switches back "on" to slam the door shut and restore the resting potential. No other current has this dual role.

With the five phases clearly in mind, we now turn to the single most important ion in cardiac physiology: calcium. The next chapter is devoted entirely to the intracellular calcium machinery — from the nanoscale architecture of the dyadic cleft to the pathological remodeling that drives heart failure and arrhythmia.
