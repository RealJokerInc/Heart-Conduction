Chapter 19

Lattice-Boltzmann Methods for Monodomain

Chapter 17 built the LBM machinery from first principles: distribution functions, lattice geometry, collision and streaming, the equilibrium distribution born from Gauss-Hermite quadrature, and the moment-space decomposition that reveals the physical content of the collision operator. Now we put that machinery to work on the cardiac monodomain equation. The key additions are surprisingly mechanical: an ionic source term injected into the collision step, operator splitting to separate reaction from diffusion, and bounce-back boundary conditions to handle tissue edges. For anisotropic tissue, we unlock the moment-space relaxation rates derived in Section 18.5 — but now we must connect them quantitatively to the cardiac conductivity tensor. This chapter derives that connection, presents the complete algorithm, and discusses the implementation choices that make LBM fast on GPUs.

19.1   The Monodomain Equation as a Target PDE

Our target is equation (7.1), the monodomain PDE that governs wave propagation in cardiac tissue:

$$\chi C_m \frac{\partial V_m}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot (\mathbf{D}\,\nabla V_m) \;-\; \chi\,I_{\text{ion}}(V_m, \mathbf{w}) \;+\; I_{\text{stim}}$$

The left-hand side is the rate of change of the transmembrane potential $V_m$, scaled by the surface-to-volume ratio $\chi$ and membrane capacitance $C_m$. On the right, three terms compete: diffusion ($\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}\nabla V_m)$) spreads the voltage through tissue, ionic currents ($I_{\text{ion}}$) from the cell membrane shape the action potential, and stimulus current ($I_{\text{stim}}$) initiates activation. Chapters 5 and 6 detailed the ionic models (TTP06 and ORd) that compute $I_{\text{ion}}$ from $V_m$ and the gating variables $\mathbf{w}$. Chapters 9 and 10 solved the diffusion side with explicit and implicit finite-difference methods. Now we solve it with LBM.

The diffusion tensor $\mathbf{D}$ encodes the anisotropy of cardiac tissue. In a 2D slab with fiber direction $\mathbf{a}_l = (\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$, the tensor takes the form:

(19.1) $$\mathbf{D} = D_l \,\mathbf{a}_l \mathbf{a}_l^T + D_t\,(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{a}_l \mathbf{a}_l^T)$$

where $D_l$ is the along-fiber diffusivity and $D_t$ is the cross-fiber diffusivity. Typical values are $D_l \approx 0.001$ cm$^2$/ms and $D_t \approx 0.00025$ cm$^2$/ms (a 4:1 ratio). In component form:

$D_{xx} = D_l\cos^2\theta + D_t\sin^2\theta$,   $D_{yy} = D_l\sin^2\theta + D_t\cos^2\theta$,   $D_{xy} = (D_l - D_t)\sin\theta\cos\theta$

For isotropic tissue ($D_l = D_t = D$), the tensor reduces to $D\,\mathbf{I}$ and the off-diagonal term vanishes. For aligned fibers ($\theta = 0$), we get $D_{xx} = D_l$, $D_{yy} = D_t$, $D_{xy} = 0$. The general case ($\theta \neq 0$, $D_l \neq D_t$) produces a full tensor with off-diagonal coupling.

The two-part structure. The monodomain PDE has a clear two-part structure: diffusion (the divergence term, which spreads voltage through tissue) and reaction (the ionic and stimulus terms, which generate and shape the action potential locally). These two parts operate on vastly different scales — diffusion is a spatial coupling between neighboring nodes, while reaction is purely local (each node's ionic currents depend only on that node's voltage and gating variables). This separation is exactly what operator splitting exploits, and it maps perfectly onto the LBM framework: collision handles diffusion, and the ionic ODE handles reaction.

19.2   Operator Splitting: Reaction Then Diffusion

Following the Godunov splitting framework of Chapter 8, we solve the monodomain equation in two sequential substeps per timestep. The reaction substep is purely local — it updates $V_m$ and $\mathbf{w}$ at each node independently. The diffusion substep couples neighbors through LBM collision and streaming.

Substep 1 — Reaction (ionic ODE): At each node, hold the diffusion term frozen and solve:

$$\chi\,C_m \frac{dV_m}{dt} = -\chi\,I_{\text{ion}}(V_m, \mathbf{w}) + I_{\text{stim}}, \qquad \frac{d\mathbf{w}}{dt} = \mathbf{g}(V_m, \mathbf{w})$$

This is an ODE system at each node — identical to what we solve in Chapters 5 and 6. The Rush-Larsen method (equation 8.1) handles the gating variables $\mathbf{w}$, exploiting their exponential relaxation structure for accuracy and stability. After this substep, we have updated gating variables $\mathbf{w}^{n+1}$ and an intermediate voltage $V_m^*$ that includes the ionic contribution but not yet the diffusion.

Substep 2 — Diffusion (LBM): Now solve the diffusion part $\partial V_m / \partial t = \nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}\nabla V_m)/(\chi C_m)$ using the LBM collision-streaming cycle. The collision operator splits cleanly into two pieces that mirror the two-part physics of the monodomain equation:

  • $\Omega^{NR}$ (non-reactive collision) — drives distributions toward equilibrium, producing diffusive flux. This is the LBM mechanism for the divergence term $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}\nabla V_m)$.
  • $\Omega^{R}$ (reactive collision) — injects the ionic source into the distribution. This is how the reaction substep’s output couples back into the lattice populations.

The reactive collision needs a source term $S$ that encapsulates the net effect of ionic currents and external stimulus:

(19.2) $$S = -\frac{I_{\text{ion}}(V_m^n, \mathbf{w}^n) + I_{\text{stim}}}{\chi\,C_m}$$

This source has units of voltage per unit time (mV/ms). The sign convention follows equation (7.1): inward current (depolarizing) makes $S > 0$, outward current (repolarizing) makes $S < 0$. The precise definitions of $\Omega^{NR}$ and $\Omega^{R}$ — and the physical reasons they take the forms they do — are derived in Section 19.3 (BGK) and Section 19.4 (MRT).

Splitting order matters. Godunov splitting (reaction first, then diffusion) is first-order accurate in time. Strang splitting (half-reaction, full diffusion, half-reaction) is second-order but requires two ionic evaluations per timestep. For cardiac LBM, first-order Godunov splitting is standard because the timestep $\Delta t$ is already small (typically 0.005–0.02 ms) due to the lattice resolution requirements, and the splitting error is dominated by the spatial discretization error. See Chapter 8 for the full analysis.

19.3   BGK Collision with Source Term

The diffusion substep is where LBM does its work. Recall from Chapter 17 that each lattice node carries $Q$ distribution functions $f_i$ (five for D2Q5, seven for D3Q7). The macroscopic voltage is their sum: $V_m = \sum_i f_i$. The equilibrium distribution for a pure diffusion problem, derived from Gauss-Hermite quadrature in Section 18.4, is:

$$f_i^{\text{eq}} = w_i \, V_m$$

This is equation (18.27) with the scalar field $\phi$ replaced by the transmembrane potential $V_m$, and no advection ($\mathbf{u} = 0$). The weights $w_i$ are the Gauss-Hermite quadrature weights from Section 18.4: for D2Q5, $w_0 = 1/3$ and $w_{1,2,3,4} = 1/6$.

The BGK collision with ionic source combines two physically distinct operations. We define each as a rate (per unit time), consistent with the continuous collision operator $\Omega(f)$ from equation (18.11). The non-reactive collision rate $\Omega_i^{NR}$ is the BGK relaxation toward equilibrium:

$$\Omega_i^{NR} = -\frac{1}{\tau}\bigl(f_i - w_i\,V_m\bigr)$$

This is a direct transcription of equation (18.11) with $f^{\text{eq}} = w_i V_m$. Physically, $\Omega_i^{NR}$ drives each distribution toward the local equilibrium at rate $1/\tau$. When $f_i > w_i V_m$ (more particles than equilibrium in direction $i$), the rate is negative — particles are removed. When $f_i < w_i V_m$, particles are added. This redistribution of particles among directions is diffusion: non-equilibrium structure in the distribution function corresponds to flux, and relaxing that structure spreads voltage through tissue. The $\tau$–$D$ relationship (equation 17.26) quantifies exactly how the relaxation rate maps to the macroscopic diffusion coefficient.

The reactive collision rate $\Omega_i^{R}$ injects the ionic source into the distribution:

$$\Omega_i^{R} = w_i\,S$$

This is a pure source injection: the reaction term $S$ (equation 18.2) — which encapsulates all ionic currents and external stimulus — is distributed among lattice directions with weights $w_i$. Since $\sum_i w_i = 1$, the total injected rate is $\sum_i \Omega_i^R = S$, preserving the correct total source. The weights ensure isotropy: the source does not preferentially inject particles in any direction, because ionic currents have no spatial directionality — they flow across the cell membrane, not along any lattice vector.

The full collision update discretizes these rates with timestep $\Delta t$:

(19.3) $$f_i^* = f_i + \Delta t\bigl(\Omega_i^{NR} + \Omega_i^{R}\bigr) = f_i - \frac{\Delta t}{\tau}\bigl(f_i - w_i\,V_m\bigr) + \Delta t\,w_i\,S$$

The separation is physically exact, not merely algebraic convenience. $\Omega^{NR}$ handles transport — the spatial coupling between neighboring nodes that produces the $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}\nabla V_m)$ term in the monodomain PDE. $\Omega^R$ handles reaction — the purely local ionic and stimulus currents that produce the $-\chi\,I_{\text{ion}} + I_{\text{stim}}$ terms. This mirrors the operator-splitting structure of Section 19.2, but now at the level of the collision operator itself. The decomposition carries directly into the MRT formulation (Section 19.4), where $\Omega^{NR}$ gains direction-dependent relaxation rates to model anisotropic diffusion, while $\Omega^R$ remains unchanged — because the ionic physics is isotropic.

The relaxation time $\tau$ is set by the $\tau$-$D$ relationship (equation 17.26):

$$\tau = \frac{\Delta t}{2} + \frac{3D\,\Delta t}{\Delta x^2}$$

For isotropic tissue with $D = 0.001$ cm$^2$/ms, $\Delta x = 0.025$ cm, $\Delta t = 0.01$ ms: $\tau = 0.005 + 0.048 = 0.053$ ms. The collision rate is $\omega = \Delta t/\tau \approx 0.189$. Stability requires $\tau > \Delta t/2$, which holds for any positive diffusivity.

After collision, streaming (equation 17.15) shifts each post-collision distribution one lattice spacing in its designated direction:

$$f_i(\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{e}_i \Delta x, \; t + \Delta t) = f_i^*(\mathbf{x}, t)$$

No new mathematical machinery is required beyond what Chapter 17 established. The cardiac monodomain is solved by the same collide-and-stream cycle that solves any diffusion equation — the ionic model simply provides the source term.

Physical interpretation. Think of the source $S$ as a faucet (or drain) at each lattice node. When a stimulus depolarizes a cell ($S > 0$), it adds voltage to the local particle population — distributed equally among all directions via the weights $w_i$. The collision then relaxes the distribution toward equilibrium (spreading the perturbation), and streaming carries the information to neighboring nodes. This is how a depolarization wave propagates through the lattice: source injection → collision → streaming → recovery, repeated every timestep.

19.4   MRT for Anisotropic Tissue

Why BGK Fails for Anisotropy

Cardiac tissue conducts three to four times faster along fibers than across them. This anisotropy is encoded in the diffusion tensor $\mathbf{D}$ (equation 18.1), which has different eigenvalues along and perpendicular to the fiber direction. Can BGK handle this?

No. Recall from Section 18.5 that BGK uses a single relaxation rate $\omega = \Delta t/\tau$ for all moments. When we decoded the collision into moment space, we saw that the $x$-flux and $y$-flux moments both relax at the same rate $s_1 = s_2 = \omega$. Since the diffusion coefficient in each direction is determined by the corresponding flux relaxation rate, BGK forces $D_x = D_y$. It can only produce isotropic diffusion. For anisotropic cardiac tissue, we need to unlock those rates — relax the $x$-flux and $y$-flux at different speeds. This is exactly what MRT (Multiple Relaxation Time) does.

From the Conductivity Tensor to Relaxation Rates

Section 18.5 showed that MRT relaxes each moment independently: the conserved moment (density) at rate $s_0 = 0$, the flux moments at rates $s_1, s_2$, and the ghost moments at freely chosen rates. The question is: what values of $s_1$ and $s_2$ reproduce a given anisotropic diffusion tensor?

Start from the scalar $\tau$-$D$ relationship (equation 17.24), which Chapman-Enskog analysis derives for the BGK operator:

$$D = c_s^2\left(\tau - \frac{\Delta t}{2}\right)$$

This relates a single diffusivity $D$ to a single relaxation time $\tau$. For anisotropic diffusion, we need a tensor generalization. The Chapman-Enskog expansion applied to the MRT collision operator (equation 17.40) shows that each component of the diffusion tensor maps to a corresponding relaxation parameter:

(19.4) $$D_{\alpha\beta} = c_s^2\left(\tau_{\alpha\beta} - \frac{\Delta t}{2}\,\delta_{\alpha\beta}\right)$$

where $\alpha, \beta \in \{x, y\}$ (or $\{x, y, z\}$ in 3D). This is the tensor $\tau$-$D$ relationship — the natural extension of equation (18.24) from a scalar to a matrix. Rearranging for the relaxation parameters:

(19.5) $$\tau_{\alpha\beta} = \frac{\Delta t}{2}\,\delta_{\alpha\beta} + \frac{D_{\alpha\beta}}{c_s^2}$$

Substituting $c_s^2 = \Delta x^2/(3\Delta t^2)$:

(19.6) $$\tau_{\alpha\beta} = \frac{\Delta t}{2}\,\delta_{\alpha\beta} + \frac{3\,D_{\alpha\beta}\,\Delta t}{\Delta x^2}$$

This is the tensor generalization of equation (18.26). Each entry of the $\tau$ matrix is determined by the corresponding entry of the diffusion tensor. The Kronecker delta $\delta_{\alpha\beta}$ ensures that the $\Delta t/2$ offset only appears on the diagonal.

For D2Q5 with axis-aligned fibers ($\theta = 0$), the diffusion tensor is diagonal ($D_{xx} = D_l$, $D_{yy} = D_t$, $D_{xy} = 0$), and the relaxation parameters simplify to:

$\tau_{xx} = \frac{\Delta t}{2} + \frac{3D_l\Delta t}{\Delta x^2}$,   $\tau_{yy} = \frac{\Delta t}{2} + \frac{3D_t\Delta t}{\Delta x^2}$,   $\tau_{xy} = 0$

These become the flux relaxation rates: $s_1 = \Delta t/\tau_{xx}$ controls the $x$-flux decay (and hence $D_{xx}$), while $s_2 = \Delta t/\tau_{yy}$ controls the $y$-flux decay (and hence $D_{yy}$). Since $D_l > D_t$, we have $\tau_{xx} > \tau_{yy}$, so the $x$-flux relaxes more slowly — it takes longer to reach equilibrium, which means more information persists in the $x$-direction, producing faster diffusion along the fiber.

Why slower relaxation means faster diffusion. This seems backwards at first. A larger $\tau$ means less relaxation per timestep — the distribution retains more of its non-equilibrium structure. But non-equilibrium structure is the flux. A large departure from equilibrium means a large gradient, which drives faster diffusion. If $\tau$ were very small (rapid relaxation), the distribution would snap to equilibrium immediately — zero flux, zero diffusion. This is the lattice-Boltzmann version of "a stiff spring returns to rest quickly but doesn't transmit waves far."

The Off-Diagonal Problem: Rotated Fibers

When fibers are not aligned with the lattice axes ($\theta \neq 0$), the diffusion tensor has off-diagonal entries ($D_{xy} \neq 0$). Equation (19.6) then gives $\tau_{xy} \neq 0$, which means the relaxation matrix $\mathbf{S}$ in equation (18.40) is no longer diagonal in the Cartesian moment basis. There are two standard approaches:

Approach 1 — Rotate the moment basis. Construct $\mathbf{M}$ using the fiber-aligned coordinate system instead of the lattice axes. The flux moments then align with the fiber and cross-fiber directions, where $\mathbf{D}$ is diagonal, and $\mathbf{S}$ stays diagonal. This requires a different $\mathbf{M}$ at each node (since fiber angles vary spatially), which adds computational cost.

Approach 2 — Modify the equilibrium distribution. Keep the standard $\mathbf{M}$ (equation 17.36) but replace the isotropic equilibrium $f_i^{\text{eq}} = w_i V_m$ with an anisotropic equilibrium that encodes the full diffusion tensor:

(19.7) $$f_i^{\text{eq}} = w_i \, V_m \left(1 + \frac{e_{i\alpha}\,e_{i\beta}\,D_{\alpha\beta}}{2\,c_s^2\,D_0} - \frac{1}{2}\frac{\text{tr}(\mathbf{D})}{D_0 \cdot d}\right)$$

where $D_0$ is a reference diffusivity and summation over $\alpha, \beta$ is implied. This approach absorbs the anisotropy into the equilibrium target rather than the relaxation rates, allowing a single scalar $\tau$ to be used. The implementation is simpler (no per-node $\mathbf{M}$ matrices) but the equilibrium is more complex.

For the remainder of this chapter, we use Approach 1 (the MRT formulation with diagonal $\mathbf{S}$ in the principal-axis frame), which makes the connection to Chapter 17's moment-space machinery most transparent.

The Full MRT Collision with Source

Assembling the pieces from Section 18.5 (equations 17.38–17.41), the MRT collision with ionic source uses the same $\Omega^{NR} + \Omega^R$ decomposition from Section 19.3, but with the non-reactive part now operating in moment space. Defining the rate-based collision operators:

$$\boldsymbol{\Omega}^{NR} = -\frac{1}{\Delta t}\,\mathbf{M}^{-1}\,\mathbf{S}\,\bigl(\mathbf{M}\mathbf{f} - \mathbf{M}\mathbf{f}^{\text{eq}}\bigr), \qquad \boldsymbol{\Omega}^{R} = \mathbf{w}\,S$$

where $\mathbf{S} = \Delta t \cdot \boldsymbol{\Lambda}$ is the dimensionless relaxation matrix (with entries $s_k = \Delta t / \tau_k$), the factor $1/\Delta t$ converts the finite increment back to a rate. The full MRT update is then:

(19.8) $$\boxed{\mathbf{f}^* = \mathbf{f} + \Delta t\bigl(\underbrace{\boldsymbol{\Omega}^{NR}}_{\text{diffusion}} + \underbrace{\boldsymbol{\Omega}^{R}}_{\text{reaction}}\bigr) = \mathbf{f} - \mathbf{M}^{-1}\,\mathbf{S}\,\bigl(\mathbf{M}\mathbf{f} - \mathbf{M}\mathbf{f}^{\text{eq}}\bigr) + \Delta t\,\mathbf{w}\,S}$$

The expanded right-hand side is identical to the standard MRT form — the $1/\Delta t$ in $\boldsymbol{\Omega}^{NR}$ cancels with $\Delta t$ in the update. But the conceptual decomposition is preserved: $\boldsymbol{\Omega}^{NR}$ is the rate at which moment-space relaxation produces diffusive flux (anisotropic, direction-dependent), while $\boldsymbol{\Omega}^{R}$ is the rate at which ionic source is injected (isotropic, identical to the BGK case).

Here $\mathbf{M}$ is the transformation matrix (equation 17.36 for D2Q5, equation 17.37 for D3Q7), $\mathbf{S}$ is the diagonal relaxation matrix, $\mathbf{w} = (w_0, w_1, \ldots, w_{Q-1})^T$ is the weight vector, and $S$ is the ionic source (equation 18.2). The relaxation matrix for D2Q5 is:

(19.9) $$\mathbf{S}_{\text{D2Q5}} = \text{diag}\!\left(0, \;\frac{\Delta t}{\tau_{xx}}, \;\frac{\Delta t}{\tau_{yy}}, \;s_3, \;s_4\right)$$

The first entry is zero (density is conserved — never relaxed). Entries 1 and 2 are the flux relaxation rates that control $D_{xx}$ and $D_{yy}$ through equation (19.6). Entries 3 and 4 are the ghost-moment rates — the free parameters discussed in Section 18.5.

Ghost-Moment Rates and the Gram-Schmidt Construction

Section 18.5 stated that the ghost rows of $\mathbf{M}$ "just need to be linearly independent" from the physical rows to make the matrix invertible. But how do you construct them in practice?

The standard procedure is Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization. Start with the physical rows — the density row $(1, 1, 1, 1, 1)$ and the flux rows $(0, 1, -1, 0, 0)$, $(0, 0, 0, 1, -1)$ — which are dictated by conservation laws and Fick's law. Then construct the remaining rows by taking higher-order velocity products ($|\mathbf{e}_i|^2$, $e_{ix}^2 - e_{iy}^2$, etc.) and orthogonalizing them against the physical rows using the Gram-Schmidt process. This yields a well-conditioned $\mathbf{M}$ whose inverse is numerically stable.

For D2Q5, the two ghost rows in equation (18.36) correspond to the "energy" moment $\sum_i |\mathbf{e}_i|^2 f_i$ and the "energy difference" moment $\sum_i (e_{ix}^2 - e_{iy}^2) f_i$. These are second-order velocity products that are linearly independent of the zeroth- and first-order physical rows. The Gram-Schmidt process ensures they are also orthogonal, which makes $\mathbf{M}$ well-conditioned. For D3Q7 (equation 17.37), the same procedure generates four ghost rows from second-order products in three dimensions.

What about the ghost relaxation rates $s_3$ and $s_4$? Since no macroscopic conservation law constrains them, they are free parameters. The Chapman-Enskog analysis shows that they do not appear in the recovered PDE at leading order — they affect only the truncation error and numerical stability. In practice, the standard choice is:

$s_3 = s_4 = 1.0$   (full relaxation to equilibrium each timestep)

Setting a ghost rate to 1.0 means the corresponding moment is driven entirely to its equilibrium value in a single collision step — maximum damping of non-physical modes. This is the most stable choice. Some authors optimize ghost rates for specific accuracy targets (for example, setting $s_3 = s_4 = 8(2 - s_1)/(8 - s_1)$ to cancel certain third-order error terms), but for cardiac simulations where stability matters more than squeezing out extra order of accuracy, $s_3 = s_4 = 1.0$ is the standard.

Why ghost rates affect stability but not the PDE. The Chapman-Enskog expansion recovers the macroscopic PDE from the lattice-Boltzmann equation order by order in the Knudsen number $\epsilon = \Delta x / L$ (where $L$ is a macroscopic length scale). The zeroth-order recovery gives mass conservation. The first-order recovery gives the diffusion equation, with $D$ determined by the flux relaxation rates $s_1, s_2$. The ghost rates $s_3, s_4$ first appear at second order, in the truncation error. They modify the coefficient of the $O(\Delta x^2)$ error term but leave the leading-order PDE untouched. This is why different textbooks use different ghost rows and different ghost rates yet recover the same diffusion equation — they differ only in their numerical error properties.

Worked Example: MRT Collision with Anisotropic Tissue

Consider D2Q5 with $D_l = 0.001$ cm$^2$/ms, $D_t = 0.00025$ cm$^2$/ms, fiber angle $\theta = 0$ (aligned with $x$), $\Delta x = 0.025$ cm, $\Delta t = 0.01$ ms.

From equation (19.6):

$\tau_{xx} = 0.005 + \frac{3 \times 0.001 \times 0.01}{0.000625} = 0.005 + 0.048 = 0.053$ ms
$\tau_{yy} = 0.005 + \frac{3 \times 0.00025 \times 0.01}{0.000625} = 0.005 + 0.012 = 0.017$ ms

The relaxation rates:

$s_1 = \Delta t / \tau_{xx} = 0.01/0.053 = 0.189$,   $s_2 = \Delta t / \tau_{yy} = 0.01/0.017 = 0.588$

As expected, the cross-fiber direction ($y$) relaxes faster ($s_2 > s_1$), producing slower diffusion. The full relaxation matrix is:

$\mathbf{S} = \text{diag}(0, \; 0.189, \; 0.588, \; 1.0, \; 1.0)$

Now suppose $V_m = -85$ mV, with the same non-equilibrium distributions from the Chapter 17 worked example: $\mathbf{f} = (0.340, \; 0.175, \; 0.155, \; 0.168, \; 0.162)^T \times (-85)$ (scaled to voltage), and a stimulus source $S = 0.0371$ mV/ms. The MRT collision proceeds as:

1. Decode: $\mathbf{m} = \mathbf{M}\mathbf{f}$ — extract density, $x$-flux, $y$-flux, and ghost moments.
2. Relax: $m_0^* = m_0$ (conserved), $m_1^* = m_1 - 0.189(m_1 - 0)$, $m_2^* = m_2 - 0.588(m_2 - 0)$, $m_3^* = 0$ (full relaxation), $m_4^* = 0$ (full relaxation).
3. Re-encode: $\mathbf{f}^* = \mathbf{M}^{-1}\mathbf{m}^*$.
4. Add source: $f_i^* \leftarrow f_i^* + \Delta t \, w_i \, S$.

The $x$-flux decays by 18.9% (slow — high diffusivity along fibers), while the $y$-flux decays by 58.8% (fast — low diffusivity across fibers). The ghost moments snap entirely to equilibrium. This is the same collision from the Chapter 17 worked example, but now with the $x$ and $y$ dials set to different values.

19.5   Boundary Conditions for LBM Electrophysiology

In classical methods (FEM, FDM), boundary conditions are applied by modifying the global stiffness matrix or by constraining node values. In LBM, boundaries are handled at the distribution level — you modify individual $f_i$ at boundary nodes, one direction at a time. This local, direction-by-direction approach is one of LBM’s most elegant features: no global matrix is ever touched, and complex geometries require no special mesh generation.

For cardiac electrophysiology, the dominant boundary condition is no-flux Neumann: no transmembrane current leaves the tissue domain. Physically, the heart is electrically insulated — ionic currents flow within the myocardium but cannot escape through the epicardial surface. Mathematically, this is $\nabla V_m \cdot \hat{n} = 0$ at every tissue boundary, where $\hat{n}$ is the outward normal.

19.5.1   Full-Way Bounce-Back

Imagine a ball thrown at a wall: it bounces straight back. Full-way bounce-back works the same way. When a distribution $f_i$ at a boundary node would stream into a solid (non-tissue) region, it reverses direction — it becomes $f_{\bar{i}}$, where $\bar{i}$ denotes the opposite direction ($\mathbf{e}_{\bar{i}} = -\mathbf{e}_i$).

Concretely, at a boundary node $\mathbf{x}_b$, after the collision step produces $f_i^*(\mathbf{x}_b)$, instead of streaming $f_i^*$ to the neighbor $\mathbf{x}_b + \mathbf{e}_i \Delta x$ (which lies outside the domain), we apply:

(19.10) $$f_{\bar{i}}(\mathbf{x}_b, \, t + \Delta t) = f_i^*(\mathbf{x}_b, \, t)$$

The post-collision distribution that would have left the domain in direction $i$ is redirected to arrive at the same node in the opposite direction $\bar{i}$. Every unit of “flux” heading outward is perfectly reflected inward, so the net flux at the boundary is zero — exactly the no-flux Neumann condition.

For the D2Q9 lattice used in this chapter, the opposite pairs are:

Type Direction $i$ Velocity $\mathbf{e}_i$ Opposite $\bar{i}$ Velocity $\mathbf{e}_{\bar{i}}$
Axial1$(+1, 0)$3$(-1, 0)$
2$(0, +1)$4$(0, -1)$
Diagonal5$(+1, +1)$7$(-1, -1)$
6$(-1, +1)$8$(+1, -1)$
Rest0$(0, 0)$No partner — never streamed
Corner nodes. At a corner where two boundaries meet (say, the bottom-left corner of a rectangular domain), bounce-back applies to both boundary directions independently. If $\mathbf{e}_3 = (-1,0)$ and $\mathbf{e}_4 = (0,-1)$ would stream out of the domain, both are reflected: $f_1 \leftarrow f_3^*$ and $f_2 \leftarrow f_4^*$. The diagonal direction $\mathbf{e}_7 = (-1,-1)$ is also reflected: $f_5 \leftarrow f_7^*$. No special corner treatment is needed — bounce-back handles it automatically, direction by direction.

19.5.2   Half-Way Bounce-Back

Full-way bounce-back places the effective wall at the boundary node itself. Half-way bounce-back places the wall halfway between the last fluid node and the first solid node. This seemingly small shift has an important consequence: the boundary condition becomes second-order accurate in space, matching the second-order accuracy of the interior LBM scheme.

The rule is almost identical to full-way bounce-back, but the timing changes. Instead of reflecting at the same timestep, the reflected distribution arrives one timestep later:

(19.10b) $$f_{\bar{i}}(\mathbf{x}_b, \, t + \Delta t) = f_i^*(\mathbf{x}_b, \, t)$$

The formula looks the same as full-way bounce-back (eq. 18.10), but the interpretation differs: the wall is now located at $\mathbf{x}_b + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{e}_i \Delta x$, not at $\mathbf{x}_b$. On a uniform Cartesian grid, half-way bounce-back is the standard choice because the wall sits exactly at the cell face — which is where finite-difference methods also place the boundary.

Why second-order? The key insight is that the streaming step in LBM is exact — distributions travel exactly one lattice spacing per timestep. When the wall is at the half-way point, the round-trip distance (fluid node → wall → fluid node) is exactly $\Delta x$, which takes exactly $\Delta t$. This geometric coincidence eliminates the first-order error that arises when the wall does not sit at a half-integer position. For regular Cartesian grids (as used in cardiac LBM), this condition is automatically satisfied.

19.5.3   Identifying Boundary Nodes

Before applying bounce-back, we must know which nodes are boundary nodes and which directions need reflection. In a cardiac simulation on a regular grid, the tissue geometry is typically defined by a binary mask: each grid point is either “tissue” (active) or “non-tissue” (inactive).

A fluid node $\mathbf{x}$ is a boundary node if at least one of its lattice neighbors $\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{e}_i \Delta x$ is non-tissue. The specific directions $i$ that point into non-tissue are the boundary links — these are the directions where bounce-back must be applied. In practice, this is precomputed once at initialization: 

for each fluid node x:
    for each direction i = 1, ..., Q-1:
        neighbor = x + e_i * dx
        if neighbor is outside domain or non-tissue:
            mark (x, i) as a boundary link

At runtime, the streaming step skips boundary links (no data is sent to non-tissue nodes), and the bounce-back rule (eq. 18.10) is applied instead. This keeps the boundary treatment local and branchless — ideal for GPU execution.

19.5.4   Handling Irregular Tissue Geometries

Real cardiac tissue is not rectangular. The left ventricle is roughly ellipsoidal, the atria have complex curved surfaces, and scar tissue creates irregular internal boundaries. On a regular Cartesian grid, these curved boundaries are approximated by a staircase — the tissue mask follows the grid lines, creating jagged edges where the true boundary is smooth.

For cardiac electrophysiology, staircase boundaries are generally acceptable for two reasons:

  1. Diffusion dominates geometry. The monodomain equation is a reaction-diffusion PDE, not a fluid-flow problem. Small geometric errors at the boundary (on the order of $\Delta x$) produce errors in the diffusion flux that are $O(\Delta x)$ — the same order as the spatial discretization error. Refining the grid improves both simultaneously.
  2. No pressure or velocity fields. In fluid LBM, staircase boundaries create spurious pressure oscillations and inaccurate drag forces. These problems do not arise in diffusion-only LBM because there is no momentum equation — only a scalar field $V_m$ that diffuses smoothly.
When staircases fail. If you need sub-grid accuracy at the boundary (e.g., modeling the exact endocardial surface for defibrillation electrode placement), interpolated bounce-back methods exist. The simplest is linear interpolation bounce-back, which uses the fractional distance $q$ from the fluid node to the actual wall: $f_{\bar{i}}(\mathbf{x}_b, t+\Delta t) = 2q\,f_i^*(\mathbf{x}_b, t) + (1-2q)\,f_i^*(\mathbf{x}_b - \mathbf{e}_i \Delta x, t)$ for $q < 1/2$, with a complementary formula for $q \geq 1/2$. This restores second-order accuracy on curved boundaries but adds implementation complexity. For most cardiac EP applications, standard bounce-back on a sufficiently fine grid ($\Delta x \leq 0.2$ mm) is adequate.

19.6   The Complete LBM-EP Algorithm

Assembling all the pieces — operator splitting (Section 19.2), BGK or MRT collision with source (Sections 19.3–18.4), streaming (Chapter 17), and bounce-back boundaries (Section 19.5) — the complete algorithm for one timestep is:

(19.11)

Complete LBM-EP step (BGK or MRT):

$$\begin{aligned} &\textbf{1. Ionic:} &&I_{\text{ion}} = \text{IonicModel.compute\_Iion}(V_m, \mathbf{w}); \quad \text{update } \mathbf{w} \text{ via Rush-Larsen} \\[4pt] &\textbf{2. Source:} &&S = -\frac{I_{\text{ion}} + I_{\text{stim}}}{\chi\,C_m} \\[4pt] &\textbf{3. Collide:} &&f_i^* = f_i + \Delta t\bigl(\Omega_i^{NR} + \Omega_i^{R}\bigr) \quad \text{(BGK eq. 18.3, or MRT eq. 18.8)} \\[4pt] &\textbf{4. Stream:} &&f_i(\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{e}_i \Delta x) \leftarrow f_i^*(\mathbf{x}) \quad \forall\, i \\[4pt] &\textbf{5. Bounce-back:} &&f_{\bar{i}}(\mathbf{x}_b) \leftarrow f_i^*(\mathbf{x}_b) \quad \text{at boundary nodes} \\[4pt] &\textbf{6. Recover:} &&V_m^{n+1} = \textstyle\sum_i f_i \end{aligned}$$

Steps 3–6 constitute the diffusion solver. Note the complete absence of matrix assembly, matrix-vector products, or iterative convergence checks. Every operation is either purely local (collision, source injection, recovery) or a nearest-neighbor shift (streaming). This is why LBM achieves its speed advantage on GPUs.

Worked example. For cardiac tissue with $D = 0.001$ cm$^2$/ms, $\Delta x = 0.025$ cm, $\Delta t = 0.01$ ms: $\tau = 0.053$ ms, so $\omega = \Delta t/\tau = 0.189$. Consider a node with $V_m = -85$ mV, all distributions at equilibrium, and a stimulus $I_{\text{stim}} = -52\;\mu$A/$\mu$F (with $I_{\text{ion}} = 0$, $\chi = 1400$ cm$^{-1}$, $C_m = 1\;\mu$F/cm$^2$).
Then $S = 52 / 1400 = 0.0371$ mV/ms.
After collision: $f_0^* = f_0 + 0.01 \times \frac{1}{3} \times 0.0371 \approx f_0 + 1.24 \times 10^{-4}$.
After streaming and recovery: $V_m^{n+1} = \sum f_i \approx -85 + 0.01 \times 0.0371 = -84.9996$ mV. The stimulus produces a tiny depolarization in one 0.01 ms step, as expected.

19.7   Implementation: Memory Layout and GPU Acceleration

LBM is a textbook example of SIMD parallelism: every node performs the same collision and streaming operations in lockstep. There are no global data dependencies, no reduce operations, no convergence criteria — each GPU thread is completely independent. On modern GPUs, this translates to near-peak memory bandwidth utilization and minimal synchronization overhead.

The Swap-Streaming Optimization

The naive implementation of Algorithm (19.11) uses two arrays: a pre-collision array $\mathbf{f}$ and a post-collision array $\mathbf{f}^*$. After streaming populates $\mathbf{f}^*$, the arrays are swapped. This doubles memory usage. The swap-streaming (or "AA-pattern") optimization fuses collision and streaming into a single pass:

On even timesteps, read $f_i(\mathbf{x})$ from the source node and write $f_i^*$ directly to the destination node $\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{e}_i\Delta x$ (scatter pattern). On odd timesteps, reverse: read $f_i$ from the neighbor $\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{e}_i\Delta x$ and write the post-collision value to $\mathbf{x}$ (gather pattern). By alternating read/write patterns every other timestep, a single array suffices — halving memory and improving cache performance.

Memory Layout: Structure of Arrays

For GPU kernels, the distributions are stored in Structure of Arrays (SoA) format: one contiguous array per direction $i$, each of size $N_x \times N_y$ (or $N_x \times N_y \times N_z$ in 3D). The node at position $(x, y)$ has its $f_i$ stored at index $i \times N_x N_y + y \times N_x + x$. This layout ensures that when a GPU warp processes adjacent nodes, it accesses contiguous memory locations within each direction — maximizing memory coalescing.

Sparse/Indirect Addressing for Irregular Geometries

Cardiac tissue is not rectangular. A ventricular slab, a cross-section of the septum, or a full biventricular geometry all have irregular boundaries. Storing distributions on a full Cartesian grid wastes memory on nodes outside the tissue. The solution is indirect addressing: an adjacency array maps each active node to its neighbors. Instead of computing neighbor positions from $(x \pm 1, y \pm 1)$, the streaming step looks up the neighbor index from a precomputed table. This adds one integer load per direction per node but eliminates wasted storage on inactive nodes.

In Engine V5.4, the LBM monodomain solver is in the cardiac_sim.simulation.lbm package. The lattice definitions (D2Q5, D3Q7) are frozen dataclasses holding weights, velocity vectors, and the $\tau$-$D$ conversion formula. Collision operators (BGKCollision, MRTCollision) inherit from CollisionOperator(ABC) and implement the update step (equation 18.3 or 18.8). The LBMState container holds the distribution tensor $\mathbf{f}$ (shape $Q \times N_x \times N_y$ for 2D), voltage $V_m$, ionic states $\mathbf{w}$, and the domain mask. The LBMSimulation class orchestrates the six-step loop (19.11): call collision.collide(f, V, source, dt), apply state.stream() (one torch.roll per direction), state.apply_bounce_back(), and finally state.recover_voltage() to compute $V_m = \sum f_i$. The ionic substep is identical to the classical path — only the diffusion step changes. Factory functions create_isotropic_bgk(D, dx, dt) and create_anisotropic_mrt(D_fiber, D_cross, fiber_angle, dx, dt) handle initialization.

19.8   LBM vs. Classical Methods: When to Choose What

LBM, explicit methods (Chapter 9), and implicit methods (Chapter 10) each have distinct trade-offs:

When to use LBM: GPU hardware, Cartesian grids, and when throughput (simulations per unit wall-clock time) matters more than memory. The total absence of global synchronization makes LBM ideal for multi-GPU scaling and massive parameter sweeps. When to use classical methods: unstructured meshes (patient-specific geometries from MRI), problems where memory is the bottleneck (very large 3D domains), or when existing FEM infrastructure is already validated and optimized.

With the monodomain LBM now complete, we turn to its greatest challenge: the bidomain system. The parabolic intracellular equation can be solved by LBM, but the elliptic extracellular equation has no time dependence — it is a constraint that must be satisfied at every instant. Chapter 19 develops three strategies to overcome this fundamental obstacle: pseudo-time relaxation, hybrid LBM–classical solvers, and the dual-lattice architecture.
