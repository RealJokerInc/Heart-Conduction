Chapter 12

The Bidomain Model

Before we write equations, we build a physical picture of why two "domains" are needed. Then we derive the bidomain equations step by step, from conservation laws to the coupled parabolic–elliptic system that Engine V5.4 will solve.

12.1   Currents in a Living Heart

Cardiac tissue is not a simple conducting wire. It is a dense, three-dimensional mesh of individual cells (myocytes), each wrapped in a lipid bilayer membrane that acts as both a capacitor and a nonlinear switch. Between the cells lies a bath of salty fluid — the extracellular space. Inside each cell, the cytoplasm forms another conducting medium — the intracellular space. The two spaces are connected electrically by thousands of gap junctions that stitch neighboring cells together.

When a wave of electrical activation sweeps through the heart, current flows simultaneously in both spaces:

  • Intracellular current $\mathbf{J}_i$ travels from cell to cell through gap junctions, following the fiber direction preferentially (anisotropy ratio $\approx$ 10:1).
  • Extracellular current $\mathbf{J}_e$ flows through the interstitial fluid, also anisotropic but with a milder ratio ($\approx$ 2.5:1).
  • Transmembrane current $I_m$ crosses the cell membrane, carried by ion channels, pumps, and membrane capacitance.

The crucial observation is that these anisotropy ratios are not equal. Current takes different preferred paths in the two spaces. This mismatch is what the monodomain model cannot capture — and what the bidomain model was invented to describe.

12.2   Divergence and Conservation: The Language of Current Flow

To build the bidomain equations, we need one fundamental physical law: conservation of charge. In a region of tissue, charge cannot appear or disappear — it can only flow in or out. The mathematical way to say "what flows out must equal what is produced inside" is the divergence.

What is divergence, intuitively? Imagine placing a tiny balloon at a point in a fluid. If the fluid is flowing outward from that point, the balloon inflates — the divergence is positive. If the fluid converges inward, the balloon deflates — the divergence is negative. For electric current, $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}$ measures how much current is "created" at each point (positive) or "absorbed" (negative).

Now apply this to our two domains. In the intracellular space, current $\mathbf{J}_i$ flows from cell to cell. At any point, some of that current may leak out of the intracellular space across the membrane into the extracellular space. That leaking current is the transmembrane current $I_m$. Conservation says:

(12.1) $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}_i = -\chi \, I_m$$

The negative sign tells us: if transmembrane current flows outward (from inside the cell to outside), it is a sink for intracellular current — current is leaving the intracellular space.

Similarly, in the extracellular space, that same transmembrane current is now a source — current arriving from inside:

(12.2) $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}_e = +\chi \, I_m$$

Here $\chi$ is the surface-to-volume ratio of the cells (how much membrane area per unit volume of tissue — typically 1000–1500 cm$^{-1}$). It converts a current per unit membrane area ($I_m$ in $\mu$A/cm$^2$) into a current per unit volume.

The conservation identity. If you add equations (12.1) and (12.2), the transmembrane currents cancel exactly: $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}_i + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}_e = 0$$ This is simply the statement that total current is conserved: every charge that leaves the intracellular space enters the extracellular space, and vice versa. No charge is created or destroyed inside the tissue. This identity is the backbone of the entire bidomain framework.

12.3   From One Domain to Two

In the monodomain model, we make a simplifying assumption: the ratio of intracellular to extracellular conductivity is the same in every direction. Under this assumption, the extracellular potential $\phi_e$ becomes spatially uniform (or can be set to zero), and we need to track only one unknown — the transmembrane potential $V_m$. One PDE, one unknown. Simple and fast.

But real cardiac tissue violates this assumption. The intracellular space is strongly anisotropic (current flows about 10 times faster along fibers than across them), while the extracellular space is only mildly anisotropic ($\approx$ 2.5:1). This unequal anisotropy ratio means the extracellular potential is not uniform — it develops complex spatial patterns of its own.

This matters in practice whenever we need to model:

  • Defibrillation — an external electric field interacts with the tissue through the extracellular space.
  • Virtual electrode polarization — field-induced regions of depolarization and hyperpolarization that cannot exist in the monodomain picture.
  • ECG generation — body-surface potentials are driven by extracellular currents.
  • External stimulation — electrode stimuli enter through $\phi_e$.

To capture these phenomena, we must solve for both $V_m$ and $\phi_e$. We need the bidomain model: two coupled equations, two unknowns, one beautiful (and computationally expensive) system.

12.4   The Monodomain Equation (Review)

The monodomain equation, which Engine V5.4 solves, describes how the transmembrane potential $V_m$ evolves in time:

(12.3) $$\chi \, C_m \frac{\partial V_m}{\partial t} = -\chi \, I_{\text{ion}}(V_m, \mathbf{w}) + \nabla \cdot \bigl(\mathbf{D}\,\nabla V_m\bigr) + I_{\text{stim}}$$

Reading each term from left to right:

  • Left-hand side: rate of change of membrane charge ($\chi C_m\, \partial V_m/\partial t$). The membrane is a capacitor.
  • $-\chi\,I_{\text{ion}}$: ionic currents through membrane channels — these are nonlinear functions of $V_m$ and gating variables $\mathbf{w}$.
  • $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}\,\nabla V_m)$: diffusion of voltage through the tissue. The tensor $\mathbf{D}$ encodes the effective conductivity — a blend of intracellular and extracellular contributions.
  • $I_{\text{stim}}$: externally applied stimulus current.

This is a single parabolic PDE (it has a time derivative and a second-order spatial derivative). It is well-suited to standard diffusion solvers, LBM, or finite-element methods.

12.5   Building the Bidomain System

Let us now be more precise. In each domain, Ohm's law gives us the current density:

$$\mathbf{J}_i = -\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla \phi_i, \qquad \mathbf{J}_e = -\mathbf{D}_e\,\nabla \phi_e$$

where $\phi_i$ and $\phi_e$ are the intracellular and extracellular potentials, and $\mathbf{D}_i$, $\mathbf{D}_e$ are their respective conductivity tensors.

The transmembrane potential is defined as their difference:

$$V_m = \phi_i - \phi_e$$

The transmembrane current per unit volume is the sum of capacitive and ionic components:

$$\chi\, I_m = \chi\,C_m\,\frac{\partial V_m}{\partial t} + \chi\, I_{\text{ion}}(V_m, \mathbf{w})$$

Now substitute into the conservation laws from Chapter 12. From the intracellular conservation $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{J}_i = -\chi I_m$:

$$\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\phi_i) = \chi\,C_m\,\frac{\partial V_m}{\partial t} + \chi\,I_{\text{ion}}$$

Since $\phi_i = V_m + \phi_e$, we can rewrite $\nabla\phi_i = \nabla V_m + \nabla\phi_e$, giving us:

(12.4) $$\chi\,C_m\,\frac{\partial V_m}{\partial t} = -\chi\,I_{\text{ion}}(V_m,\mathbf{w}) + \nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla V_m) + \nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\phi_e) + I_{\text{stim}}^i$$

This is the parabolic equation of the bidomain system. Compare it to the monodomain equation (12.3): the key difference is the extra coupling term $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\phi_e)$, which accounts for how the extracellular potential gradient drives intracellular current.

For the second equation, add both conservation laws: $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{J}_i + \nabla\cdot\mathbf{J}_e = 0$. Expanding:

(12.5) $$0 = \nabla\cdot\bigl(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla V_m\bigr) + \nabla\cdot\bigl[(\mathbf{D}_i + \mathbf{D}_e)\,\nabla\phi_e\bigr] + I_{\text{stim}}^e$$

This is the elliptic equation. It has no time derivative — it is a constraint that must hold at every instant. Think of it as saying: "at each moment, the total current flowing into and out of every point in the tissue must balance."

Why is the second equation elliptic? Because the extracellular space has no "memory" — it equilibrates instantaneously (on the timescale of cardiac propagation). There is no capacitor in the extracellular space storing charge over time. The extracellular potential adjusts immediately to satisfy current conservation. This makes $\phi_e$ a slave variable: it is completely determined by the current state of $V_m$ at each instant.

12.6   The Parabolic–Elliptic Couple

All of Part II (Chapters 7–10) dealt with a single parabolic PDE — the monodomain equation. "Parabolic" means the equation has a first-order time derivative, and its solutions spread out and smooth over time, like heat diffusing through a rod. We learned to discretize it in space (Chapter 7), split reaction from diffusion (Chapter 8), integrate the reaction ODEs (Chapter 9), and solve the implicit diffusion step (Chapter 10). The entire solution strategy was: march forward in time, one step at a time.

Now we face something fundamentally different. The bidomain model has two equations, and they are not the same type. One is parabolic (it evolves in time), and the other is elliptic (it has no time derivative at all). An elliptic equation is like a rubber sheet stretched over a frame (Appendix A.5) — if you push on one part, the entire sheet adjusts instantaneously to find a new equilibrium. There is no "marching forward" for an elliptic equation; it must be solved as a global boundary-value problem at every instant in time.

This parabolic–elliptic pairing changes everything about how we solve the system. Let us examine the structure:

Equation Type Unknown Character
(12.4) — Membrane dynamics Parabolic $V_m$ (primary) Evolves in time; has $\partial V_m/\partial t$
(12.5) — Current conservation Elliptic $\phi_e$ (constraint) No time derivative; instantaneous balance

The two equations are bidirectionally coupled: the parabolic equation depends on $\phi_e$ (through $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\phi_e)$), and the elliptic equation depends on $V_m$ (through $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla V_m)$). Neither can be solved independently.

Why the elliptic equation cannot be marched in time. The parabolic equation (12.4) has $\partial V_m/\partial t$ on the left — it tells us the rate of change of $V_m$, so we can step forward. The elliptic equation (12.5) has no time derivative — it is a constraint that must be satisfied at every instant, like requiring that the forces on a bridge sum to zero at all times. You cannot "advance" a force-balance equation; you can only solve it. This is why the bidomain diffusion step requires solving a linear system (Chapter 15), not just evaluating a time derivative.

The good news is that the elliptic equation has no memory — its solution at time $t$ depends only on $V_m(t)$, not on $\phi_e$ at any earlier time. This means that if we know $V_m^{n+1}$ (or an approximation of it), we can solve the elliptic equation for $\phi_e^{n+1}$ without any information about $\phi_e^n$. This property is the key to operator splitting (Chapter 14): advance $V_m$ first, then solve for $\phi_e$.

Additionally, the gating variables $\mathbf{w}$ evolve according to a system of ODEs:

(12.6) $$\frac{d\mathbf{w}}{dt} = \mathbf{h}(V_m, \mathbf{w})$$

These ODEs are local — they depend only on $V_m$ and $\mathbf{w}$ at each point, not on spatial derivatives. This is good news: the ionic solver from Engine V5.4 (e.g., Rush–Larsen exponential integrator) can be reused without modification.

12.7   When Bidomain Reduces to Monodomain

Under the equal anisotropy ratio assumption — meaning $\mathbf{D}_e = \lambda\,\mathbf{D}_i$ for some positive scalar $\lambda$ — the elliptic equation (12.5) degenerates. With this assumption, the combined conductivity $(\mathbf{D}_i + \mathbf{D}_e) = (1+\lambda)\,\mathbf{D}_i$, and the elliptic equation becomes $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla[V_m + (1+\lambda)\phi_e]) = 0$, which implies $\phi_e$ is essentially constant (or zero with appropriate boundary conditions). The parabolic equation then reduces to the monodomain form with an effective conductivity:

$$\mathbf{D}_{\text{eff}} = \frac{\mathbf{D}_i \, \mathbf{D}_e}{\mathbf{D}_i + \mathbf{D}_e}$$

In real cardiac tissue, however, the anisotropy ratios are markedly different:

Domain$\sigma_L$ (S/cm)$\sigma_T$ (S/cm)Ratio $\sigma_L/\sigma_T$
Intracellular0.3 – 0.50.03 – 0.05$\approx$ 10:1
Extracellular0.3 – 0.60.15 – 0.3$\approx$ 2.5:1

Because 10:1 $\neq$ 2.5:1, the equal anisotropy assumption fails, and the full bidomain system is needed.

12.8   Conductivity Tensors and Fiber Architecture

In each domain, the conductivity tensor is expressed in local fiber coordinates as a diagonal matrix:

$$\mathbf{D}_d^{\text{local}} = \begin{pmatrix}\sigma_d^L & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \sigma_d^T & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \sigma_d^T\end{pmatrix}, \quad d \in \{i, e\}$$

where $\sigma_d^L$ and $\sigma_d^T$ are the longitudinal (along-fiber) and transverse (cross-fiber) conductivities. In an anatomically realistic heart model, the fibers rotate transmurally (about 120° from epicardium to endocardium), so the local tensor must be rotated into global coordinates via:

$$\mathbf{D}_d^{\text{global}}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{R}(\theta(\mathbf{x}))\;\mathbf{D}_d^{\text{local}}\;\mathbf{R}(\theta(\mathbf{x}))^T$$

This makes the conductivity a spatially varying tensor field — one of the major implementation challenges for any bidomain solver.

Engine V5.4 already handles fiber-aligned conductivity tensors for the monodomain model. For bidomain, the same rotation machinery can be reused — we simply need to maintain two tensor fields ($\mathbf{D}_i$ and $\mathbf{D}_e$) instead of one effective $\mathbf{D}$.

12.9   Boundary Conditions: Two Domains, Two Choices

In the monodomain model (Section 7.4), we needed one boundary condition for one unknown. The bidomain model has two unknowns — the intracellular potential $\phi_i$ and the extracellular potential $\phi_e$ — so we need two boundary conditions at every point on the tissue surface. This doubles the number of choices and creates physical situations that have no monodomain analogue.

To build intuition, think of a piece of cardiac tissue sitting in a glass dish. The intracellular space (inside the cells) is sealed at the tissue surface — cell membranes continue right up to the edge, and no intracellular current can escape. This is always Neumann. But the extracellular space (between the cells) is open — it may be in contact with a conductive bath (saline solution) or exposed to air. What happens at the boundary depends on what is outside.

There are three canonical physical scenarios:

  • Isolated tissue (Neumann on both): No current leaves through either domain. This is the most realistic for an intact heart — but it introduces a null space in the elliptic equation, because $\phi_e$ is determined only up to an additive constant.
  • Grounded bath (Neumann on $\phi_i$, Dirichlet on $\phi_e$): Cells are sealed, but the extracellular space opens into a bath at a reference potential. This is the standard Langendorff preparation. The Dirichlet condition removes the null space.
  • Current injection (Dirichlet on $\phi_i$ at electrode): An intracellular electrode clamps $\phi_i$ locally. Rare experimentally but important for theoretical stimulation studies.

The null space is physics, not a bug. When the entire boundary has Neumann conditions on $\phi_e$, the extracellular potential is defined only relative to some reference point — just like altitude is defined relative to sea level. You can add any constant to $\phi_e$ without changing any current flow. This is a real physical degeneracy: only differences in extracellular potential matter, not absolute values.

The concrete mathematical formulations — how each BC type modifies the stiffness matrices and the block system — are developed in Chapter 13 (Section 13.4). The strategies for handling the null space in iterative solvers (pinning, deflation, DCT zero-mode removal) are covered in Chapter 16 (Section 16.5).
