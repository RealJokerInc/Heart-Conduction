Chapter 12

The Bidomain Model

Before we write equations, we build a physical picture of why two "domains" are needed. Then we derive the bidomain equations step by step, from conservation laws to the coupled parabolic–elliptic system that Engine V1 will solve.

Two notations for the same physics. The bidomain literature uses two equivalent formulations:

  • Conductivity form ($\sigma$-form): Uses conductivities $\sigma_i$, $\sigma_e$ with explicit $\chi\,C_m$ in the time derivative. Standard in journal papers and in the derivations below.
  • Diffusivity form ($D$-form): Uses $D_i = \sigma_i / (\chi\,C_m)$, $D_e = \sigma_e / (\chi\,C_m)$, absorbing $\chi\,C_m$ so the time derivative becomes simply $\partial V_m / \partial t$. Used in the Engine V1 code.

This chapter derives equations in $\sigma$-form (matching the literature). All discrete equations in Chapters 13–15 and the Engine connection boxes use $D$-form. To convert: replace $\chi\,C_m\,\partial V_m/\partial t$ with $\partial V_m/\partial t$, and replace $\sigma$ with $D \cdot \chi\,C_m$.

12.1   Currents in a Living Heart

Cardiac tissue is not a simple conducting wire. It is a dense, three-dimensional mesh of individual cells (myocytes), each wrapped in a lipid bilayer membrane that acts as both a capacitor and a nonlinear switch. Between the cells lies a bath of salty fluid — the extracellular space. Inside each cell, the cytoplasm forms another conducting medium — the intracellular space. The two spaces are connected electrically by thousands of gap junctions that stitch neighboring cells together.

When a wave of electrical activation sweeps through the heart, current flows simultaneously in both spaces:

  • Intracellular current $\mathbf{J}_i$ travels from cell to cell through gap junctions, following the fiber direction preferentially (anisotropy ratio $\approx$ 10:1).
  • Extracellular current $\mathbf{J}_e$ flows through the interstitial fluid, also anisotropic but with a milder ratio ($\approx$ 2.5:1).
  • Transmembrane current $I_m$ crosses the cell membrane, carried by ion channels, pumps, and membrane capacitance.

The crucial observation is that these anisotropy ratios are not equal. Current takes different preferred paths in the two spaces. This mismatch is what the monodomain model cannot capture — and what the bidomain model was invented to describe.

12.2   Divergence and Conservation: The Language of Current Flow

To build the bidomain equations, we need one fundamental physical law: conservation of charge. In a region of tissue, charge cannot appear or disappear — it can only flow in or out. The mathematical way to say "what flows out must equal what is produced inside" is the divergence.

What is divergence, intuitively? Imagine placing a tiny balloon at a point in a fluid. If the fluid is flowing outward from that point, the balloon inflates — the divergence is positive. If the fluid converges inward, the balloon deflates — the divergence is negative. For electric current, $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}$ measures how much current is "created" at each point (positive) or "absorbed" (negative).

Now apply this to our two domains. In the intracellular space, current $\mathbf{J}_i$ flows from cell to cell. At any point, some of that current may leak out of the intracellular space across the membrane into the extracellular space. That leaking current is the transmembrane current $I_m$. Conservation says:

(12.1) $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}_i = -\chi \, I_m$$

The negative sign tells us: if transmembrane current flows outward (from inside the cell to outside), it is a sink for intracellular current — current is leaving the intracellular space.

Similarly, in the extracellular space, that same transmembrane current is now a source — current arriving from inside:

(12.2) $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}_e = +\chi \, I_m$$

Here $\chi$ is the surface-to-volume ratio of the cells (how much membrane area per unit volume of tissue — typically 1000–1500 cm$^{-1}$). It converts a current per unit membrane area ($I_m$ in $\mu$A/cm$^2$) into a current per unit volume.

The conservation identity. If you add equations (12.1) and (12.2), the transmembrane currents cancel exactly: $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}_i + \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J}_e = 0$$ This is simply the statement that total current is conserved: every charge that leaves the intracellular space enters the extracellular space, and vice versa. No charge is created or destroyed inside the tissue. This identity is the backbone of the entire bidomain framework.

12.3   From One Domain to Two

In the monodomain model, we make a simplifying assumption: the ratio of intracellular to extracellular conductivity is the same in every direction. Under this assumption, the extracellular potential $\phi_e$ becomes spatially uniform (or can be set to zero), and we need to track only one unknown — the transmembrane potential $V_m$. One PDE, one unknown. Simple and fast.

But real cardiac tissue violates this assumption. The intracellular space is strongly anisotropic (current flows about 10 times faster along fibers than across them), while the extracellular space is only mildly anisotropic ($\approx$ 2.5:1). This unequal anisotropy ratio means the extracellular potential is not uniform — it develops complex spatial patterns of its own.

This matters in practice whenever we need to model:

  • Defibrillation — an external electric field interacts with the tissue through the extracellular space.
  • Virtual electrode polarization — field-induced regions of depolarization and hyperpolarization that cannot exist in the monodomain picture.
  • ECG generation — body-surface potentials are driven by extracellular currents.
  • External stimulation — electrode stimuli enter through $\phi_e$.

To capture these phenomena, we must solve for both $V_m$ and $\phi_e$. We need the bidomain model: two coupled equations, two unknowns, one beautiful (and computationally expensive) system.

12.4   The Monodomain Equation (Review)

The monodomain equation (Part II) describes how the transmembrane potential $V_m$ evolves in time:

(12.3) $$\chi \, C_m \frac{\partial V_m}{\partial t} = -\chi \, I_{\text{ion}}(V_m, \mathbf{w}) + \nabla \cdot \bigl(\mathbf{D}\,\nabla V_m\bigr) + I_{\text{stim}}$$

Reading each term from left to right:

  • Left-hand side: rate of change of membrane charge ($\chi C_m\, \partial V_m/\partial t$). The membrane is a capacitor.
  • $-\chi\,I_{\text{ion}}$: ionic currents through membrane channels — these are nonlinear functions of $V_m$ and gating variables $\mathbf{w}$.
  • $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}\,\nabla V_m)$: diffusion of voltage through the tissue. The tensor $\mathbf{D}$ encodes the effective conductivity — a blend of intracellular and extracellular contributions.
  • $I_{\text{stim}}$: externally applied stimulus current.

This is a single parabolic PDE (it has a time derivative and a second-order spatial derivative). It is well-suited to standard diffusion solvers, LBM, or finite-element methods.

12.5   Building the Bidomain System

Let us now be precise. In each domain, Ohm's law gives us the current density:

$$\mathbf{J}_i = -\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla \phi_i, \qquad \mathbf{J}_e = -\mathbf{D}_e\,\nabla \phi_e$$

where $\phi_i$ and $\phi_e$ are the intracellular and extracellular potentials, and $\mathbf{D}_i$, $\mathbf{D}_e$ are their respective conductivity tensors. The transmembrane potential is the difference:

$$V_m = \phi_i - \phi_e$$

The transmembrane current per unit volume has two components — capacitive charging and ionic flow:

$$\chi\, I_m = \chi\,C_m\,\frac{\partial V_m}{\partial t} + \chi\, I_{\text{ion}}(V_m, \mathbf{w})$$

The Parabolic Equation (Membrane Dynamics)

Start with the intracellular conservation law (12.1): $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{J}_i = -\chi I_m$. Substitute Ohm's law $\mathbf{J}_i = -\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\phi_i$:

$$\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\phi_i) = \chi\,C_m\,\frac{\partial V_m}{\partial t} + \chi\,I_{\text{ion}}$$

Now comes the key substitution. Since $\phi_i = V_m + \phi_e$, we have $\nabla\phi_i = \nabla V_m + \nabla\phi_e$. Substituting and using the linearity of divergence:

$$\nabla\cdot\bigl(\mathbf{D}_i\,(\nabla V_m + \nabla\phi_e)\bigr) = \nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla V_m) + \nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\phi_e)$$

This is where the monodomain's single diffusion term splits into two distinct physical processes. Rearranging to isolate $\partial V_m/\partial t$:

(12.4) $$\chi\,C_m\,\frac{\partial V_m}{\partial t} = -\chi\,I_{\text{ion}}(V_m,\mathbf{w}) + \underbrace{\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla V_m)}_{\text{propagation}} + \underbrace{\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\phi_e)}_{\text{coupling}} + I_{\text{stim}}^i$$

Let us read each term as a physical current — what is it actually doing?

  • $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla V_m)$ — the propagation term. This is intracellular current driven by transmembrane potential gradients. Where $V_m$ is high (depolarized tissue) and neighbors are low (resting tissue), intracellular current flows from depolarized to resting regions, spreading the action potential. This is exactly the same term as in the monodomain equation (7.1) — it is why the electrical wave propagates through the heart.
  • $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\phi_e)$ — the coupling term. This is the new term that did not appear in the monodomain. It represents intracellular current driven by extracellular potential gradients. Why does extracellular potential affect intracellular current? Because $\phi_i = V_m + \phi_e$: if the extracellular potential varies in space (say, because of a defibrillation electrode), that shifts the intracellular potential everywhere, which changes the intracellular current flow. In the monodomain model, $\phi_e$ is assumed uniform, so $\nabla\phi_e = 0$ and this term vanishes. This coupling term is the entire reason the bidomain model exists.

Comparing monodomain to bidomain. The monodomain equation (12.3) has a single diffusion term $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}\,\nabla V_m)$ with an effective conductivity $\mathbf{D}$ that blends both domains. The bidomain parabolic equation (12.4) replaces this with two separate terms: propagation via $\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla V_m$ and coupling via $\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\phi_e$. Both use the intracellular conductivity $\mathbf{D}_i$ — because this equation describes what happens inside the cells.

The Elliptic Equation (Current Conservation)

For the second equation, recall the conservation identity from Section 12.2: $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{J}_i + \nabla\cdot\mathbf{J}_e = 0$ — total current is conserved. Apply Ohm's law to both terms:

$$\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\phi_i) + \nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_e\,\nabla\phi_e) = 0$$

Now substitute $\phi_i = V_m + \phi_e$ into the first term, using the same splitting as before:

$$\underbrace{\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla V_m)}_{\text{from } \phi_i = V_m + \phi_e} + \underbrace{\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\phi_e)}_{\text{from } \phi_i = V_m + \phi_e} + \nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_e\,\nabla\phi_e) = 0$$

Collect the two terms involving $\nabla\phi_e$:

(12.5) $$0 = \underbrace{\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla V_m)}_{\text{source}} + \underbrace{\nabla\cdot\bigl[(\mathbf{D}_i + \mathbf{D}_e)\,\nabla\phi_e\bigr]}_{\text{response}} + I_{\text{stim}}^e$$

This is the elliptic equation. It has no time derivative — $\phi_e$ does not evolve; it adjusts instantaneously. Read the two terms as a source-response balance:

  • $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla V_m)$ — the source. The transmembrane potential $V_m$ varies in space (especially at the wavefront, where it changes from $-85$ mV to $+30$ mV over a few tenths of a millimeter). This gradient drives intracellular current, which crosses the membrane and enters the extracellular space. The divergence of this current tells you how much charge is being "dumped" into the extracellular space at each point. At the wavefront, this dump is massive.
  • $\nabla\cdot\bigl[(\mathbf{D}_i + \mathbf{D}_e)\,\nabla\phi_e\bigr]$ — the response. The extracellular potential $\phi_e$ adjusts so that the combined current in both domains carries away all the charge that was dumped. The combined conductivity $(\mathbf{D}_i + \mathbf{D}_e)$ appears because current in both domains responds to extracellular potential gradients. This term acts like diffusion — it smooths $\phi_e$ across the tissue. The stronger the combined conductivity, the faster the smoothing, and the gentler the extracellular potential gradients.

The steady-state heat analogy. If you have studied heat transfer, you know this structure: a steady-state temperature field adjusts so that heat production (source) equals heat diffusion (response) everywhere. The elliptic equation is exactly that — $V_m$ is the "heat source" and $\phi_e$ is the "temperature." There is no thermal mass, no transient — just an instant equilibrium. The extracellular potential $\phi_e$ is a slave variable: it is completely determined by the current $V_m$ at each instant, with no memory of the past.

Why is there no time derivative? The extracellular space has no capacitor — no mechanism to store charge over time. The intracellular space stores charge on the membrane capacitor (that is the $C_m\,\partial V_m/\partial t$ term). But the extracellular space simply conducts: charge enters at one point and leaves at another, with no accumulation. Mathematically, this means the extracellular conservation law produces an equation with spatial derivatives only — no $\partial\phi_e/\partial t$. This is what makes it elliptic rather than parabolic.

What the Numbers Look Like

To make these terms concrete, let us estimate their magnitudes at a cardiac wavefront using typical human ventricular parameters:

ParameterValueSource
$D_{i,L}$ (intracellular, along fibers)$0.0017$ cm$^2$/msClerc 1976
$D_{i,T}$ (intracellular, across fibers)$0.00019$ cm$^2$/msClerc 1976

At the wavefront, the action potential rises from $-85$ to $+30$ mV over roughly $\Delta x \approx 0.3$ mm along fibers. A crude estimate of the spatial curvature gives $\partial^2 V_m/\partial x^2 \approx -4000$ V/cm$^2$ (negative because $V_m$ has a peak at the front). Along fibers:

  • Propagation term: $D_{i,L} \cdot \partial^2 V_m / \partial x^2 \approx 0.0017 \times (-4000) = -6.8$ mV/ms
  • Coupling term: $D_{i,L} \cdot \partial^2\phi_e / \partial x^2$. Extracellular potential gradients are smaller — typically $|\phi_e|$ varies by $\sim$1–5 mV at the wavefront, giving $\partial^2\phi_e/\partial x^2 \approx 200$ V/cm$^2$. So the coupling contributes $\approx 0.0017 \times 200 = 0.34$ mV/ms — roughly 5% of the propagation term.
  • Elliptic balance: The source term $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla V_m)$ dumps charge at $\sim$6.8 mV/ms, and the response $\nabla\cdot[(\mathbf{D}_i + \mathbf{D}_e)\,\nabla\phi_e]$ must absorb all of it: $(0.0017 + 0.0062) \times \partial^2\phi_e/\partial x^2 \approx 6.8$ mV/ms. This is why $\phi_e$ develops its characteristic spatial pattern — it must generate gradients large enough to carry away all the source current.

The coupling is small but essential. The coupling term ($\sim$5%) has a modest direct effect on propagation speed. But it is responsible for all of the phenomena that distinguish bidomain from monodomain: virtual electrode polarization during defibrillation, the body-surface ECG, and the response to extracellular stimulation. A 5% correction in the PDE creates qualitatively different physics.

12.6   The Parabolic–Elliptic Couple: A New Kind of Problem

All of Part II dealt with a single parabolic PDE — the monodomain equation. "Parabolic" means the equation has a time derivative, and its solutions spread and smooth over time, like heat diffusing through a metal rod. We learned to discretize it (Chapter 8), split reaction from diffusion (Chapter 9), and march it forward in time one step at a time (Chapters 10–11).

Now we face something fundamentally different: two coupled equations of different types.

Equation Type Unknown Character Analogy
(12.4) — Membrane dynamics Parabolic $V_m$ Evolves in time; has $\partial V_m/\partial t$ A pot of water heating up
(12.5) — Current conservation Elliptic $\phi_e$ No time derivative; instantaneous balance A rubber sheet finding its shape

If you have seen coupled differential equations before — say, $dx/dt = f(x,y)$ and $dy/dt = g(x,y)$ — the bidomain is harder. In a standard coupled system, both unknowns evolve in time and you can march them both forward. Here, only $V_m$ evolves. The equation for $\phi_e$ is not $d\phi_e/dt = \ldots$ but $0 = \ldots$ — a constraint that must be satisfied at every instant. You cannot "march" a constraint equation forward in time. You can only solve it.

The two equations are bidirectionally coupled: the parabolic equation (12.4) depends on $\phi_e$ through the coupling term $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla\phi_e)$, and the elliptic equation (12.5) depends on $V_m$ through the source term $\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{D}_i\,\nabla V_m)$. Neither can be solved in isolation.

Picture the coupling. Imagine a room with a heater (the action potential wavefront) and an air conditioning system (the extracellular space). The heater warms the air locally — this is the parabolic equation evolving $V_m$ in time. The air conditioning system instantly adjusts the airflow pattern to maintain the right temperature everywhere else — this is the elliptic equation determining $\phi_e$. But the heater's output depends on the ambient air temperature (from the AC), and the AC's required output depends on the heater. They influence each other continuously.

This coupling creates the central computational challenge of the bidomain model: how do you advance a system where one variable evolves and the other is a constraint?

There are three fundamental strategies, each with a different trade-off:

Strategy Idea Trade-off
Solve together (monolithic) Combine both equations into one big $2N \times 2N$ system and solve simultaneously Exact coupling, but the system is indefinite (not SPD) — needs specialized, expensive solvers
Advance then equilibrate (decoupled sequential) Advance $V_m$ one step using the old $\phi_e$, then solve the elliptic equation for the new $\phi_e$ using the new $V_m$ Two smaller $N \times N$ SPD solves (cheap!), but the one-step lag in $\phi_e$ introduces a small error
Advance explicitly, equilibrate implicitly (semi-implicit) Advance $V_m$ with an explicit step (no linear solve), then solve the elliptic for $\phi_e$ Only one linear solve per step, but CFL-limited — must use small $\Delta t$

The key insight that makes decoupled strategies work is that the elliptic equation has no memory. Its solution at time $t$ depends only on $V_m(t)$, not on $\phi_e$ at any earlier time. So once we have $V_m^{n+1}$ (or an approximation of it), we can solve for $\phi_e^{n+1}$ as a standalone problem — a fresh boundary-value problem with no history to track.

Chapter 14 develops these strategies in detail, starting with the simplest (decoupled sequential) and building toward more sophisticated variants. But first, we must turn these continuous equations into matrix equations — the subject of Chapter 13.

Additionally, the gating variables $\mathbf{w}$ evolve according to a system of local ODEs:

(12.6) $$\frac{d\mathbf{w}}{dt} = \mathbf{h}(V_m, \mathbf{w})$$

These depend only on $V_m$ and $\mathbf{w}$ at each point — no spatial derivatives. The ionic solver from Part II (Rush–Larsen exponential integrator, Section 9.4) can be reused without modification. This is the same operator-splitting approach as in the monodomain: handle the local ionic kinetics first, then tackle the spatial diffusion problem.

Sidebar: When does the bidomain collapse to monodomain? If the two domains happen to have the same anisotropy ratio — meaning $\mathbf{D}_e = \lambda\,\mathbf{D}_i$ for some scalar $\lambda$ — the elliptic equation degenerates. The combined conductivity becomes $(1+\lambda)\,\mathbf{D}_i$, and the elliptic equation forces $\phi_e$ to be essentially constant. The parabolic equation then reduces to the monodomain form with an effective diffusivity $\mathbf{D}_{\text{eff}} = \mathbf{D}_i\,\mathbf{D}_e / (\mathbf{D}_i + \mathbf{D}_e)$. In real cardiac tissue, however, the intracellular anisotropy ratio is $\approx$10:1 while the extracellular ratio is $\approx$2.5:1 — so the equal-anisotropy assumption fails, and the full bidomain system is needed.

Sidebar: Fiber architecture and conductivity tensors. In each domain, the conductivity tensor is diagonal in local fiber coordinates: $\mathbf{D}_d^{\text{local}} = \text{diag}(\sigma_d^L,\;\sigma_d^T,\;\sigma_d^T)$ where $L$ = along-fiber and $T$ = cross-fiber. Because cardiac fibers rotate transmurally ($\approx$120° from epicardium to endocardium), the local tensor must be rotated into global coordinates: $\mathbf{D}_d^{\text{global}}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{R}(\theta)\,\mathbf{D}_d^{\text{local}}\,\mathbf{R}(\theta)^T$. This makes the conductivity a spatially varying tensor field — the same rotation machinery used for monodomain extends directly to bidomain by maintaining two tensor fields ($\mathbf{D}_i$ and $\mathbf{D}_e$) instead of one.

12.7   Boundary Conditions: Two Domains, Two Choices

In the monodomain model (Section 7.4), we needed one boundary condition for one unknown. The bidomain model has two unknowns — the intracellular potential $\phi_i$ and the extracellular potential $\phi_e$ — so we need two boundary conditions at every point on the tissue surface.

Think of a piece of cardiac tissue sitting in a glass dish. The intracellular space (inside the cells) is sealed at the tissue surface — cell membranes continue right up to the edge, and no intracellular current can escape. This is always Neumann. But the extracellular space (between the cells) is open — it may be in contact with a conductive bath (saline solution) or exposed to air. What happens at the boundary depends on what is outside.

There are three canonical physical scenarios:

Scenario$\phi_i$ BC$\phi_e$ BCNull space?Physical setting
Isolated tissue Neumann Neumann Yes (constant $\phi_e$) Heart in air / pericardium
Grounded bath Neumann Dirichlet No Langendorff, tissue wedge
Current injection Dirichlet (local) Neumann Yes (if $\phi_e$ has full Neumann) Intracellular electrode stimulus

The null space is physics, not a bug. When the entire boundary has Neumann conditions on $\phi_e$, the extracellular potential is defined only relative to some reference point — just like altitude is defined relative to sea level. You can add any constant to $\phi_e$ without changing any current flow. In practice, we remove this ambiguity by pinning one node ($\phi_e = 0$ at a single point) or by zeroing the constant mode in a spectral solver. Both approaches are covered in Chapter 15 (Section 15.2).
