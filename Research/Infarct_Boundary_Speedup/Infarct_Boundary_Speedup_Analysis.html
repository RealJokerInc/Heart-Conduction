§0. The Debate

PI's Hypothesis

No-flux (Neumann) boundary conditions at the infarct border cause ionic buildup, which increases the local transmembrane potential gradient and accelerates conduction along the boundary.

Prediction: The monodomain model should automatically capture this speedup because the no-flux BC causes ion accumulation at the obstacle surface.

Student's Counter-Hypothesis

Objection 1: The actual mechanism is wavefront curvature (geometric focusing), not ionic accumulation.

Objection 2: No-flux does NOT imply speedup. Consider a square infarct with uniform planar wave: the boundary parallel to propagation sees zero lateral gradient (∂V/∂y = 0 by symmetry), so no-flux adds no information.

Literature Verdict

The literature overwhelmingly supports the student's position. Boundary speedup arises from wavefront curvature geometry, not from no-flux ionic accumulation. The no-flux BC is a passive constraint that contributes zero energy to the system.

§1. Artificial Infarct: The Curvature Mechanism

When a planar wavefront encounters a physical obstacle (hole punched through tissue — no borderzone heterogeneity), two fundamental physics govern what happens: wavefront curvature and source-sink balance.

1.1 The Eikonal-Curvature Relation

The foundational result, derived from the monodomain equation via matched asymptotic expansion (Zykov 1980, Keener 1988):

\[ v(\kappa) = v_0 - D_\perp \kappa \]

where:

SymbolMeaningSign Convention
\(v_0\)Planar wave conduction velocityPositive (rightward)
\(D_\perp\)Diffusivity perpendicular to wavefront normalPositive
\(\kappa\)Mean curvature of wavefront+ convex, − concave
3b1b Insight: Why Curvature Changes Speed

Think of a row of dominoes falling in a line (planar wave). Now curve that line inward (concave). The dominoes at the center of the concavity receive "push" from a wider arc of already-fallen neighbors. Each domino gets more than one neighbor's worth of push → it falls faster. Conversely, if the line curves outward (convex), each domino has to push a wider arc of standing neighbors → it falls slower.

Mathematically, this is exactly the \(\kappa \frac{\partial V}{\partial n}\) term in the curvilinear Laplacian.

1.2 The Laplacian in Curvilinear Coordinates

In coordinates aligned with the wavefront (n = normal, s = tangential arc-length):

\[ \nabla^2 V = \underbrace{\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial n^2}}_{\text{1D diffusion}} + \underbrace{\kappa \frac{\partial V}{\partial n}}_{\text{curvature source}} + \underbrace{\frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial s^2}}_{\text{tangential}} \]

For a steep wavefront, \(\partial^2 V / \partial s^2\) is small. The curvature term \(\kappa \partial V / \partial n\) acts as an effective additional current source:

CurvatureSign of \(\kappa\)Effect of \(\kappa \frac{\partial V}{\partial n}\)CV Change
Concave (wrapping around obstacle)\(\kappa < 0\)Additional depolarizing currentSPEEDUP
Planar\(\kappa = 0\)No changeBaseline \(v_0\)
Convex (expanding into open tissue)\(\kappa > 0\)Current sink (drain)SLOWDOWN

1.5 Three Obstacle Shapes: Square, Convex, Concave Edges

The curvature mechanism predicts strikingly different behaviour depending on the obstacle's edge shape. A flat edge, a convex bulge, and a concave alcove each induce different wavefront curvature — and therefore different speed changes. If the PI's ionic-buildup hypothesis were correct, all three should speed up identically (same no-flux BC). They don't.

Figure 1b. Three obstacle geometries make different predictions. A. Perfect square: flat edges see no curvature change (v = v₀), speedup only at corners. B. Convex edges: the obstacle bulges into tissue, forcing concave wavefronts along the entire edge → distributed speedup. C. Concave edges: wavefronts expand into bays, developing convex curvature → slowdown along edges, but amplified speedup at the sharper corner points. The ionic-buildup hypothesis predicts uniform speedup for all three (same no-flux BC); experiments match the curvature prediction.

1.6 What Actually Happens at a Flat No-Flux Wall

The closest real phenomenon to the PI's intuition is current reflection. When a wavefront approaches an insulating wall head-on, the electrotonic current that normally drains forward is reflected back, briefly boosting the local dV/dtmax. But this is a transient cable-equation effect at the wavefront tip, not sustained boundary speedup.

Figure 1c. Current reflection at a no-flux wall. Left: In normal propagation, electrotonic current drains forward. Right: Near a wall, reflected current briefly boosts the local upstroke — but the wave is about to stop. This is the closest real phenomenon to the PI's "ionic buildup" intuition, but it is electrical (cable equation), not ionic concentration, and produces no sustained boundary speedup.

1.7 Source-Sink Mismatch: A Complementary Mechanism

Source-sink mismatch (Bhatt & Bhatt, JACC 2018; Ciaccio, EP Europace 2024) provides a complementary perspective. When a wavefront encounters converging geometry, the ratio of depolarized tissue (source) to resting tissue (sink) shifts in favor of the source, accelerating threshold crossing. This is distinct from ionic accumulation — it depends on tissue volume ratios, not ion homeostasis.

§2. Acute Infarct Borderzone: Heterogeneity Mechanisms

In contrast to a clean hole, real acute infarcts feature a complex borderzone (BZ) with gradients in cell coupling, ion channels, fibrosis, and fiber orientation. The dominant finding: conduction is overwhelmingly SLOWED in the BZ, not accelerated.

2.1 Quantitative Summary

\[ \text{CV}_{\text{BZ}} \approx 0.37 \pm 0.16 \text{ m/s} \quad \text{vs.} \quad \text{CV}_{\text{normal}} \approx 0.70 \pm 0.09 \text{ m/s} \] \[ \text{Reduction: } \sim 47\text{--}53\% \]

2.2 Mechanisms by Category

Figure 2. Three categories of mechanisms contributing to CV reduction in the infarct border zone. All three predominantly cause slowdown, not speedup.

2.3 De Bakker's Zigzag Conduction (1993)

The seminal finding from de Bakker et al.: slow conduction in the infarcted human heart follows a "zigzag" course. Activation navigates through branching/merging bundles of surviving myocytes separated by collagenous septa. Local conduction within individual bundles may be near-normal, but the tortuous global path makes apparent transverse conduction very slow.

Key Distinction: Local vs. Apparent CV

In the borderzone, local CV within surviving myocyte strands can be normal, but the apparent (global) CV is slow because the wave must follow a tortuous path. This is fundamentally different from the artificial infarct scenario where geometry is clean and the speedup question is about local wavefront physics.

Critical Finding for the Debate

The literature reports no significant conduction speedup in real infarct border zones compared to remote normal tissue. The borderzone is characterized by slowdown (47-53% CV reduction). Relative acceleration occurs only along fiber axis vs. transverse (anisotropy), not as absolute speedup.

§3. Computational Model Validation

Can Engine V5.4 (monodomain + no-flux BC) correctly capture boundary speedup?

3.1 The Short Answer

Yes — but for the right reason

The monodomain equation inherently contains the curvature-velocity relation \(v(\kappa) = v_0 - D_\perp \kappa\) because this relation is derived from the reaction-diffusion PDE via singular perturbation theory. Any properly-discretized monodomain solver will produce speedup at curved obstacle boundaries — but this speedup comes from the Laplacian operator acting on curved geometry, not from the no-flux BC itself.

3.3 Mesh Resolution: The Critical Factor

\[ \text{Error in CV} < 3\% \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad h_{\text{boundary}} \lesssim 10\;\mu\text{m} \] \[ \text{Qualitatively correct} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad h_{\text{global}} \lesssim 100\;\mu\text{m} \] \[ \text{Spurious artifacts} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad h > 200\;\mu\text{m} \]

For Engine V5.4 with FDM on structured grids, adaptive refinement near obstacle boundaries is essential. The current architecture supports per-node diffusivity, which naturally handles heterogeneity, but mesh resolution near infarct boundaries must be carefully controlled.

3.4 Operator Splitting Considerations

Godunov splitting (1st order) can introduce directional bias in curved wavefront propagation. Strang splitting (2nd order) better preserves curvature effects. For quantitative boundary speedup studies, Strang is recommended.

§4. Mathematical Proof: No-Flux ≠ Speedup

We now present the rigorous proof that no-flux boundary conditions alone cannot cause conduction velocity speedup.

Theorem 1 (1D Traveling Wave Independence)

For the 1D monodomain equation with uniform tissue, the traveling wave velocity \(c^*\) is an eigenvalue of the ODE:

\[ D\frac{d^2 V}{d\xi^2} + c\chi C_m \frac{dV}{d\xi} - \chi I_{\text{ion}}(V, u(\xi)) = 0, \quad \xi = x - ct \]

determined entirely by \(\{D, C_m, \chi, I_{\text{ion}}\}\). The no-flux BC at \(x=0\) constrains a point far in the wake (\(\xi = -ct \to -\infty\)), not at the propagating wavefront. Therefore \(c^*\) is independent of the boundary condition.

Theorem 2 (Redundancy at Parallel Edges — The Square Infarct)

Consider a 2D domain with a rectangular infarct at center. A uniform planar wave propagates in the +x direction. Along the top/bottom edges of the infarct (parallel to propagation):

Proof.

Step 1 (Symmetry): The initial condition is uniform in \(y\): \(V(x,y,0) = V_{\text{init}}(x)\), so \(\partial V/\partial y = 0\) everywhere. By uniqueness of parabolic PDE solutions, this symmetry is preserved: \(V(x,y,t) = V(x,t)\) for all \(t\).

Step 2 (BC is redundant): The no-flux BC at \(y = y_{\text{top}}\) requires \(\partial V/\partial y|_{y_{\text{top}}} = 0\). But Step 1 already gives \(\partial V/\partial y = 0\) everywhere. The BC adds no new information.

Step 3 (1D reduction): With \(\partial^2 V/\partial y^2 = 0\), the monodomain equation reduces to the 1D equation, which propagates at \(v_0\). ■

Figure 3. The square infarct counter-example. A perfectly planar wave has ∂V/∂y = 0 everywhere by symmetry. The no-flux BC (∂V/∂n = 0) is automatically satisfied and adds no information. Result: v = v₀ everywhere along the flat edges.

Theorem 3 (Energy Passivity of No-Flux BC)

The no-flux boundary contributes zero power to the system:

\[ \int_{\partial\Omega} V \, D \frac{\partial V}{\partial n} \, dS = 0 \quad \text{(since } \frac{\partial V}{\partial n}\bigg|_{\partial\Omega} = 0 \text{)} \]

All energy changes come from ionic dynamics (\(I_{\text{ion}}\)) and diffusive dissipation (\(D|\nabla V|^2\)). The boundary cannot amplify or accelerate — it is a passive mirror.

Theorem 4 (Curvature-Velocity Is BC-Independent)

The eikonal-curvature relation \(v(\kappa) = v_0 - D_\perp \kappa\) is derived from the monodomain PDE structure alone (via matched asymptotics / Fredholm solvability). It does not invoke any boundary condition. The same relation holds with Neumann, Dirichlet, or Robin BCs.

Therefore: the BC is shape-determining (it forces the wavefront to curve), not relation-changing (it does not modify the \(v(\kappa)\) law).

Central Principle

\[\boxed{\text{No-Flux BC is PASSIVE} \implies \text{Cannot cause speedup by injection}}\]

\[\boxed{\text{Speedup requires GEOMETRY} (\kappa) \implies \text{Comes from Laplacian modification}}\]

§5. Synthesis & Conclusions

5.1 Answering the Three Questions

Q1: What causes boundary speedup around artificial (hole-punch) infarcts?

Answer: Wavefront curvature. When a planar wave encounters a convex obstacle, the wavefront wraps around the edges, developing concave geometry (\(\kappa < 0\)). By the eikonal-curvature relation \(v(\kappa) = v_0 - D_\perp \kappa\), concave wavefronts propagate faster than planar ones. This is a pure geometric effect of the Laplacian operator, not an ionic accumulation effect.

No-flux BC role: Passive shape constraint. It forces the wavefront to wrap (creating curvature) but does not inject energy. The speedup would occur with any BC that forces wrapping.

Q2: What happens at the border zone of acute infarcts with heterogeneity?

Answer: Conduction is predominantly slowed (47-53% of normal CV) through a multifactorial mechanism: gap junction loss (Cx43 lateralization), fibrosis, Na+ channel downregulation (42% at 5 days), and zigzag pathway tortuosity. There is no net speedup in the borderzone. The temporal evolution shifts from ionic-dominated (acute) to structural-dominated (chronic).

Q3: Can the computational model correctly describe the artifact?

Answer: Yes, partially. The monodomain equation inherently contains curvature effects (the eikonal-curvature relation is derived FROM it). A properly-discretized monodomain solver (mesh ≤ 0.1 mm, Strang splitting) will correctly produce boundary speedup through the Laplacian's curvature term. However, the model cannot capture bath-loading effects (needs bidomain) or explicit ion accumulation dynamics.

§7. Experimental Evidence: An Honest Assessment

Critical Disclosure: The Direct Experiment Has Not Been Done

After comprehensive search across PubMed, PMC, AHA Journals, and Frontiers: no published study directly measures conduction velocity along the edge of an insulating obstacle in cardiac tissue and compares it to CV in the interior. The specific experiment — optical mapping or electrode recordings showing spatially-resolved CV at multiple positions along an obstacle boundary vs. far from it — does not exist in the literature.

What follows is an honest inventory of what does exist, what it actually shows, and what it does not show. No padding, no overclaiming.

7.1 The Experimental Gap

Figure 4. The experimental gap. Left: The experiment needed — spatially resolved CV measurement at multiple points along an insulating boundary vs. interior. Right: What actually exists in the cardiac literature, with honest caveats about what each study does and does not show.

7.2 Why the Gap Exists

Four factors explain why this specific measurement has not been made:

FactorExplanation
Resolution limits Optical mapping: ~100–350 μm/pixel. The boundary layer where curvature effects concentrate is ∼1–3 pixels wide. Edge pixels are contaminated by light scatter from the obstacle.
Edge exclusion practice Standard protocol: CV is measured at ≤80% distance from stimulus to border. Researchers deliberately exclude boundary regions because “closer to the edge one cannot see if the spread is still homogeneous” (Kleber & Rudy, Physiol Rev 2004).
Clinical motivation Cardiologists care about slowing at scar borders (enables reentry). Boundary speedup — a small positive effect — has no clinical consequence and no funding motivation.
Technical difficulty Creating a clean insulating obstacle in tissue (not ablation damage, not agar-filled holes, not pathological scar) requires engineered monolayers or precision laser ablation.

7.3 Indirect Evidence: What Each Study Actually Shows

These studies are relevant to the curvature–CV relationship but none directly measures boundary speedup. Each is presented with an honest statement of what it proves and what it does not.

A. Cabo et al. (1994) — Isthmus Diffraction, Sheep Epicardium

What they measured: CV at the exit of narrow isthmuses (200–600 μm) in epicardial tissue using optical mapping.

What it shows: Convex wavefront expansion at isthmus exit causes CV slowdown proportional to curvature. Below critical width → conduction block. The v(κ) relationship is confirmed for κ > 0 (convex, slowing).

What it does NOT show: CV along a flat or concave boundary (κ ≤ 0). The experiment measures expansion into open tissue after a constriction, not propagation along an obstacle edge. Speedup (κ < 0) is inferred by linear extrapolation, never directly measured.

Figure 5. The v(κ) evidence gap in cardiac tissue. The right half (κ > 0, convex, slowing) is directly measured by Cabo (1994) and Kucera (2000). The left half (κ < 0, concave, speedup) — the regime relevant to obstacle boundary speedup — is extrapolated from the linear fit, never directly measured in cardiac tissue. The BZ reaction (Foerster 1988) measured the full curve but is not cardiac.

B. Cabo et al. (2006) — Spiral Pinning, NRVM Monolayers

What they measured: Average CV around the full perimeter of circular obstacles (0.6–2.6 mm) after spiral wave attachment. CVtip = circumference / cycle length.

What it shows: Average CV increases linearly with obstacle size (larger obstacle → less curvature → faster).

What it does NOT show: Spatially-resolved CV at different positions around the obstacle. The measurement is a single number (perimeter-averaged), not a boundary-layer profile. You cannot distinguish speedup-along-edge from slowdown-at-corners from this data.

C. Orozco et al. (2022) — RF Ablation Lesions, Isolated Hearts

What they measured: CV in concentric zones around ablation lesions using high-density optical mapping.

What it shows: CV decreases 31–33% at the lesion border (0.36 m/s vs 0.52 m/s sham). No speedup at any boundary location.

Why it’s confounded: RF ablation creates tissue damage gradients — not a clean insulating boundary. The slowdown is likely from thermal injury and edema, not from geometric effects. This is the closest thing to a boundary CV measurement in cardiac tissue, but it cannot distinguish geometric from damage effects.

D. Roth (1991) — APD Shortening Near Insulating Boundaries, Frog Heart

What they measured: Action potential duration near a nonconducting boundary using voltage-sensitive dyes in frog cardiac tissue (PMC2447675).

What it shows: APD shortens ~10% near the boundary due to electrotonic current diffusion.

What it does NOT show: Conduction velocity. APD shortening is a temporal measurement, not a spatial propagation speed measurement. Shorter APD does not imply faster CV.

7.5 What Would Settle the Question

The Definitive Experiment (Not Yet Done)

Setup: NRVM monolayer with a photolithographically-defined rectangular obstacle (clean insulating boundary, no tissue damage). Optical mapping at ≤50 μm/pixel resolution.

Protocol: Stimulate a planar wave that propagates past the obstacle. Measure CV at multiple points: (1) along the flat boundary (parallel to propagation), (2) around the corners, (3) in the interior far from the obstacle.

Expected results based on theory:

• Flat edges parallel to propagation: CV = v₀ (no change — the square-infarct redundancy argument)

• Convex corners (wave wrapping around): CV > v₀ (concave wavefront focusing → speedup)

• Interior far from obstacle: CV = v₀ (reference)

This experiment is feasible with current technology (patterned NRVM cultures exist, Kucera/Rohr lab demonstrated the technique in 2000). It has simply not been done for this specific geometry.

7.6 Honest Summary

Where We Stand

The v(κ) relationship is well-established: measured directly in BZ reactions (Foerster 1988), confirmed on the slowing side (κ > 0) in cardiac tissue (Cabo 1994, Kucera 2000). The mathematical derivation from the monodomain PDE is rigorous.

The speedup side (κ < 0) has NOT been directly measured in cardiac tissue. It is inferred from the linearity of v(κ) on the measured side and from the BZ reaction data. This inference is reasonable but is an extrapolation, not a direct measurement.

The ionic buildup hypothesis has zero support — experimental, computational, or theoretical.

No published paper directly measures CV along the edge of a clean insulating obstacle in cardiac tissue. This is a gap in the literature, not a settled question with positive evidence.
