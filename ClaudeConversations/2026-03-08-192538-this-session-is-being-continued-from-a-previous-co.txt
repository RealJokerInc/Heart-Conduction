  Proposed Structure: Appendix B — "A Visual Guide to Linear Algebra"

  3Blue1Brown narration style: geometric intuition first, algebra second. Each
  section answers one question a reader would ask when hitting Part II or III.

  ---
  B.1 — Vectors Are Arrows (and Lists of Numbers)
  - The dual view: geometric (arrow in space) vs algebraic (column of numbers)
  - Linear combinations as "how far along each direction"
  - Span, basis, dimension — in 2D/3D first, then generalize to N
  - Cardiac hook: A voltage field on a 5-node cable is a vector in R^5

  B.2 — Matrices Are Transformations
  - A matrix maps one vector to another: stretch, rotate, project
  - Column picture: columns of A are where basis vectors land
  - Matrix-vector product as "asking the matrix a question"
  - Cardiac hook: The Laplacian L maps a voltage field to its curvature field

  B.3 — Eigenvalues: The DNA of a Matrix
  - Eigenvectors = directions the matrix only stretches (no rotation)
  - Eigenvalue = the stretch factor along that direction
  - Geometric picture: ellipse from unit circle (eigenvalues = semi-axes)
  - Why they matter: decompose any vector into eigen-directions, apply the 
  stretch, recombine
  - Cardiac hook: Spectral solvers exploit this — transform into eigenbasis,
  divide by eigenvalues, transform back

  B.4 — Symmetric Matrices: When Geometry Is Clean
  - Symmetric = A equals its transpose = eigenvectors are perpendicular
  - The spectral theorem: every symmetric matrix is a rotation × stretch ×
  rotation back
  - Real eigenvalues guaranteed — no complex surprises
  - Why symmetry matters for solvers: CG needs it, Chebyshev needs it
  - Cardiac hook: The monodomain stiffness matrix L is symmetric because "if
  node i affects node j, node j affects node i equally"

  B.5 — Positive Definite: The Bowl-Shaped World
  - Positive definite = all eigenvalues > 0 = the energy function $x^T A x$ is a
   bowl
  - The energy surface picture: SPD → bowl with unique minimum; indefinite →
  saddle
  - Semi-definite = bowl with a flat direction (zero eigenvalue)
  - Why SPD is paradise: CG works, energy minimization has a unique answer,
  Cholesky exists
  - Cardiac hook: $A_{\text{para}} = (1/\Delta t) I - \theta L_i$ is SPD because
   the identity term overwhelms the negative Laplacian eigenvalues

  B.6 — Indefinite Matrices and Saddle Points
  - What happens when some eigenvalues are positive, some negative
  - The saddle surface: looks like a mountain pass — uphill in one direction,
  downhill in another
  - Why CG fails: it tries to roll downhill to a minimum, but a saddle has no
  minimum
  - What works instead: MINRES (minimize residual, not energy), GMRES (general),
   or decouple the problem
  - Cardiac hook: The full 2N×2N bidomain system is symmetric indefinite. That's
   why we split it into two SPD sub-problems (Ch 14)

  B.7 — The Condition Number: How Hard Is Your Problem?
  - $\kappa = \lambda_{\max} / \lambda_{\min}$: ratio of biggest to smallest
  stretch
  - Geometric picture: the energy bowl is round ($\kappa \approx 1$) or
  extremely elongated ($\kappa \gg 1$)
  - CG convergence: $O(\sqrt{\kappa})$ iterations — the more elongated, the
  slower
  - Grid refinement makes $\kappa$ worse: $\kappa \propto 1/\Delta x^2$ for the
  Laplacian
  - Cardiac hook: Monodomain $A_{\text{para}}$ has $\kappa \sim O(1/\Delta t)$;
  bidomain elliptic $A_{\text{ellip}}$ has $\kappa \sim O(N)$ — much harder

  B.8 — The Null Space: When Solutions Aren't Unique
  - Null space = vectors that the matrix maps to zero
  - If $A$ has a null space, $Ax = b$ has infinitely many solutions (or none)
  - Geometric picture: the transformation collapses one dimension — information
  is lost
  - How to fix it: pin a variable (remove the collapsed direction), or project
  it out
  - Cardiac hook: The Neumann Laplacian has null space = constant vector. Adding
   a constant to $\phi_e$ changes nothing, so we pin one node

  B.9 — Sparse Matrices: When Most Entries Are Zero
  - Dense matrix: store all $N^2$ entries. Sparse: store only the nonzeros
  - The Laplacian on a 100×100 grid: 10,000 unknowns but only ~50,000 nonzeros
  (5 per row)
  - Storage formats: COO (simplest), CSR (fastest for SpMV)
  - SpMV cost: $O(\text{nnz})$ not $O(N^2)$ — the engine lives and dies by this
  - Why you never form $A^{-1}$: inverse of a sparse matrix is dense
  - Cardiac hook: Engine V1 stores L_i, L_e as sparse tensors; every solver step
   is SpMV + vector ops

  B.10 — Block Matrices: Systems of Systems
  - When you have two unknowns ($V_m$ and $\phi_e$), the system naturally has
  2×2 block structure
  - Block operations: block diagonal, block triangular, Schur complement
  - The Schur complement: "solve the first equation for $V_m$ in terms of
  $\phi_e$, substitute into the second"
  - Block decoupling: if you can approximately separate the blocks, solve each
  independently
  - Cardiac hook: The bidomain block system and why Algorithm 14.1 solves two
  N×N systems instead of one 2N×2N system

  B.11 — Preconditioning: Making Hard Problems Easy
  - Idea: instead of solving $Ax = b$, solve $M^{-1}Ax = M^{-1}b$ where $M
  \approx A$
  - The perfect preconditioner ($M = A$) solves in one step — but is as
  expensive as the original
  - The trivial preconditioner ($M = I$) changes nothing
  - The art: find $M$ that is cheap to invert but close to $A$
  - Jacobi ($M = \text{diag}(A)$): simplest, moderately effective, $O(N)$
  - Spectral preconditioner: use DCT solve as $M^{-1}$ for an approximately
  isotropic problem
  - Cardiac hook: Engine V1's three-tier strategy = spectral (exact for
  isotropic) → spectral-preconditioned PCG (anisotropic) → PCG alone (general)

  B.12 — Eigenvalue Bounds Without Computing Eigenvalues
  - Gershgorin circle theorem: every eigenvalue lives in a disc centered at
  $a_{ii}$ with radius = row sum of off-diagonals
  - One pass over the matrix → bounds on $\lambda_{\min}$, $\lambda_{\max}$
  - Why this matters: Chebyshev iteration needs these bounds; CG doesn't (but
  convergence analysis does)
  - Cardiac hook: Engine V1's Chebyshev solver calls Gershgorin once at setup,
  caches the bounds

  Appendix C (NEW) — "A Visual Guide to Numerical Analysis"

  This is the bridge between "here are the equations" (A) and "here are the
  matrices" (B). It answers: when you discretize, what can go wrong, and how do 
  you control it?

  C.1 — The Fundamental Trade-Off: Accuracy vs Cost
  - Discretization = replacing continuous with discrete (finite grid, finite
  time step)
  - Truncation error: what you throw away when you replace $df/dx$ with
  $(f_{i+1} - f_i)/\Delta x$
  - Order of accuracy: $O(\Delta x)$, $O(\Delta x^2)$ — what the exponent means
  geometrically
  - Cardiac hook: 5-point Laplacian is $O(\Delta x^2)$; this controls how fine
  your mesh must be

  C.2 — Stability: Will Your Simulation Explode?
  - The CFL condition as a speed limit: information cannot travel faster than
  one grid cell per time step
  - Forward Euler on the heat equation: derive the $\Delta t \leq \Delta x^2 /
  (2dD)$ bound visually
  - What instability looks like: checkerboard oscillations, exponential blowup
  - The eigenvalue picture: amplification factor $g(\lambda) = 1 + \Delta t
  \cdot \lambda$ must satisfy $|g| \leq 1$
  - Cardiac hook: monodomain CFL with RK4 (Ch 10); bidomain CFL 100× worse (Ch
  14)

  C.3 — Stability Regions: A Map of Safety
  - Plot the stability region in the complex $z = \Delta t \cdot \lambda$ plane
  - Forward Euler: disc of radius 1 centered at $-1$
  - RK2: bigger disc. RK4: even bigger, with lobes
  - The key insight: all eigenvalues of $\Delta t \cdot L$ must fit inside the 
  region
  - How to read the map: parabolic eigenvalues lie on the negative real axis
  - Cardiac hook: Why RK4 allows 2× larger $\Delta t$ than RK2 for the same grid
   (Ch 10)

  C.4 — Implicit Methods: Removing the Speed Limit
  - A-stability: the entire left half-plane is inside the stability region → no 
  CFL restriction
  - Backward Euler: unconditionally stable but $O(\Delta t)$ — heavy damping
  - Crank–Nicolson: $O(\Delta t^2)$ + A-stable — but preserves high-frequency
  oscillations
  - L-stability: not just stable but damps high-frequency errors — backward
  Euler has it, CN does not
  - The price: each step requires solving a linear system $Ax = b$
  - Cardiac hook: CN oscillations near steep wavefronts (Ch 11, 14); BDF2 is
  L-stable and 2nd-order

  C.5 — Convergence: Does Refining the Grid Get You Closer?
  - Lax equivalence theorem (intuitive version): consistency + stability =
  convergence
  - Consistency: does $\Delta x \to 0$ recover the PDE? (truncation error → 0)
  - Stability: does the method not blow up?
  - Convergence: does the discrete solution approach the continuous solution?
  - Order of convergence: $|u - u_h| \leq C \cdot \Delta x^p$ — the $p$ tells
  you how fast
  - Richardson extrapolation: run at two resolutions, estimate the error
  - Cardiac hook: Why validation tests check convergence rate, not just "does it
   look right" (Ch 10)

  C.6 — Operator Splitting: Divide and Conquer (moved from Ch 9 theory)
  - When your PDE has two parts with different character (stiff + non-stiff,
  local + global)
  - Godunov splitting: first-order, solve A then B
  - Strang splitting: second-order, solve A/2 then B then A/2
  - Splitting error: $O(\Delta t)$ or $O(\Delta t^2)$ — comes from the
  commutator $[A, B]$
  - When splitting is free: if the operators commute, splitting is exact
  - Cardiac hook: reaction–diffusion splitting (Ch 9); bidomain elliptic "comes
  along for free" (Ch 14)

  C.7 — From Physical Space to Transform Space (moved from A.8)
  - The orchestra analogy: time domain → frequency domain
  - Eigenfunction expansion: $u(x,t) = \sum_k \hat{u}_k(t) \psi_k(x)$
  - Why transforms work: differentiation becomes multiplication
  - The FFT: $O(N \log N)$ via divide-and-conquer
  - Keep existing A.8 content, enhance with 3B1B geometric visuals

  C.8 — DCT, DST, and Boundary Conditions (moved from A.9)
  - Cosine basis → Neumann BCs; Sine basis → Dirichlet BCs
  - DCT eigenvalues: $\lambda_k = \frac{2}{\Delta x^2}(\cos(\pi k/N) - 1)$
  - DST eigenvalues: $\lambda_k = \frac{2}{\Delta x^2}(\cos(\pi k/(N+1)) - 1)$
  - 2D: eigenvalues add (tensor product)
  - The zero mode: $k=0$ has $\lambda_0 = 0$ under Neumann → null space
  - Mixed BCs: DCT on one axis, DST on the other
  - Keep existing A.9 content, add the "why $N$ vs $N+1$" derivation gap

  C.9 — Iterative Solvers: CG, PCG, and Chebyshev (moved from A.10, expanded)
  - Energy minimization: solving $Ax=b$ = finding the bottom of a bowl
  - Gradient descent → zigzags. Conjugate gradient → no zigzags
  - A-conjugate directions: "orthogonal in the shape of the bowl, not in
  Euclidean space"
  - PCG algorithm (boxed, keep from A.10)
  - Chebyshev iteration: predetermined step sizes from polynomial theory — no
  inner products
  - Convergence: $O(\sqrt{\kappa})$ for CG; Chebyshev similar but no sync points
  - Warm-starting: use last time step's solution → 3-8 iterations instead of
  20-50
  - Keep existing A.10 content, add Chebyshev section (currently only in Ch 15)

  C.10 — When CG Fails: Solvers for Harder Problems
  - CG needs SPD. What if the matrix isn't?
  - MINRES: for symmetric indefinite (saddle-point problems)
  - GMRES: for non-symmetric (general, but memory-hungry)
  - The practical alternative: don't solve the hard system — decouple it into 
  SPD sub-problems
  - Three-tier solver selection: spectral (exact, $O(N\log N)$) → PCG+spectral
  (iterative) → PCG (general)
  - Cardiac hook: bidomain decoupled strategies avoid GMRES entirely (Ch 14)
